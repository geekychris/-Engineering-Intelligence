== Research foundations and source notes

The AI-assisted engineering framework in this chapter draws on empirical studies of coding assistants, human–AI interaction, software-agent benchmarks, information retrieval, causal evaluation, and current AI risk-management standards. It extends those foundations into a production-oriented model centered on Engineering Changes, human validation, provenance, economics, and long-term capability effects.

=== Task-level productivity evidence

Controlled studies have reported that AI coding assistants can reduce completion time for some bounded programming tasks. See <<peng2023>>.

These findings are useful but should be interpreted narrowly. A task-level time reduction does not automatically imply:

* lower end-to-end Engineering Change cost;
* lower review or validation effort;
* improved maintainability;
* fewer production defects;
* increased portfolio throughput;
* realized financial savings;
* durable benefit after novelty and learning effects.

The chapter therefore treats task completion time as one possible mechanism measure rather than as a universal productivity outcome.

=== Usability, comprehension, and debugging

Research on AI code-generation tools has identified challenges involving expectation mismatch, verification, debugging, comprehension, and trust. See <<vaithilingam2022>>.

These findings support the chapter’s distinction between generation and useful contribution. Code that is produced quickly may still require substantial effort to inspect, understand, integrate, test, secure, or maintain.

The relevant work system includes both the machine’s production and the human’s response.

=== Interaction patterns

Studies of programmer interaction with code-generating models describe iterative behaviors such as acceleration, exploration, steering, repair, and evaluation. See <<barke2023>>.

This supports a richer event model than simple accepted-versus-rejected suggestions.

Useful interaction states include:

* requested;
* generated;
* displayed;
* inspected;
* accepted unchanged;
* accepted with modification;
* rejected;
* ignored;
* retried;
* superseded;
* reverted;
* independently reimplemented;
* associated with downstream rework or defect evidence.

=== Repository-level agent benchmarks

Repository-level benchmarks such as SWE-bench evaluate whether software agents can resolve real issue-derived tasks in existing repositories. See <<jimenez2024>>.

These benchmarks provide important capability evidence because they require navigation, localization, editing, and test interaction across realistic codebases.

They still differ from enterprise production in several ways:

* the task and acceptance criterion are predefined;
* organizational coordination may be absent;
* production deployment and operational consequence are usually out of scope;
* security, privacy, and policy constraints differ;
* repository and tool access may be simplified;
* long-term maintainability is rarely observed;
* treatment cost and human oversight may not match production practice.

The chapter therefore treats benchmark performance as one evidence layer rather than as proof of autonomous production readiness.

=== Acceptance is not value

Suggestion acceptance is an adoption or interaction measure.

It does not establish that the accepted contribution:

* was correct;
* was novel;
* reduced net human effort;
* improved code quality;
* prevented a defect;
* reduced delivery latency;
* remained in the final artifact;
* improved downstream outcomes.

This distinction follows the broader measurement-validity principle in <<messick1995>> and the indicator-gaming concerns in <<campbell1979>> and <<goodhart1984>>.

The chapter therefore separates acceptance, contribution, quality, attention, and outcome measures.

=== Precision, recall, and ranked findings

AI review systems often produce findings or recommendations rather than code.

Precision and recall provide standard measures for evaluating finding relevance and coverage. See <<manning2008>>.

Ranking quality can be measured with discounted cumulative gain or normalized discounted cumulative gain where severity and position matter. See <<jarvelin2002>>.

Production evaluation should also account for:

* duplicate findings;
* equivalent findings expressed differently;
* severity calibration;
* false-positive handling time;
* findings already known to the author;
* findings that prompt useful inspection without direct acceptance;
* defects prevented downstream;
* unsupported or unverifiable claims.

=== Human validation burden

AI output transfers part of the work from production to verification.

The chapter defines validation burden as the human attention required to determine whether machine output is correct, appropriate, safe, maintainable, and consistent with intent.

This burden can include:

* reading generated code or analysis;
* checking assumptions;
* reproducing claims;
* running tests;
* consulting domain experts;
* correcting subtle errors;
* evaluating security and privacy consequences;
* documenting why output was accepted.

Cognitive-load and interruption research in <<sweller1988>> and <<mark2008>> provides part of the theoretical basis for treating this work as economically meaningful.

=== Cognitive offload and cognitive reload

The chapter defines paired concepts:

* *cognitive offload* — human reasoning or production work genuinely avoided because a reliable machine contribution performed it;
* *cognitive reload* — additional human reasoning created by noisy, ambiguous, incomplete, or untrustworthy machine output.

Net attention change can be expressed as:

[stem]
++++
NetAttentionChange = AuthoringAttentionReduced - ValidationAttentionAdded - ReworkAttentionAdded
++++

This is an Engineering Intelligence-specific operational model. It should be calibrated with task observation, event evidence, and practitioner studies rather than assumed from output volume.

=== Provenance and reproducibility

An AI contribution cannot be interpreted correctly without treatment provenance.

The chapter therefore requires capture of:

* provider;
* model and model version;
* prompt or instruction version;
* retrieved context;
* tool permissions and calls;
* temperature and relevant configuration;
* initiating actor;
* delegation chain;
* timestamps and latency;
* input and output size;
* cost;
* policy and guardrail version.

This is compatible with the entity, activity, and agent concepts in W3C PROV. See <<moreau2013>>.

Because models and hosted services can change, reproducibility may require retained outputs, evaluation fixtures, configuration snapshots, and vendor version attestations even when exact model weights are unavailable.

=== Causal evaluation

Voluntary adoption creates strong selection bias. Early users may differ in skill, enthusiasm, task mix, or management support.

The chapter therefore relies on the causal methods described in Chapter 6 and grounded in <<rubin1974>>, <<holland1986>>, <<shadish2002>>, <<imbens2015>>, <<hernan2020>>, and <<kohavi2020>>.

A credible AI evaluation distinguishes:

* assignment;
* access;
* actual invocation;
* successful exposure;
* interaction and disposition;
* downstream contribution;
* mature outcomes.

Randomized, clustered, switchback, stepped-wedge, or quasi-experimental designs may be appropriate depending on the intervention.

=== Treatment-version drift

AI systems change rapidly through model updates, prompt changes, retrieval changes, policy changes, and tool integrations.

A study can become difficult to interpret when the treatment changes during the observation period.

The chapter therefore treats treatment version as a first-class causal variable and requires:

* version-effective intervals;
* exposure linked to exact configuration;
* shadow evaluation before major changes;
* separate analysis when treatment changes materially;
* rollback capability;
* post-update replication.

=== Quality and security guardrails

Faster completion is not sufficient when quality or safety degrades.

Relevant guardrails include:

* escaped defects;
* security findings;
* privacy violations;
* rollback and incident rates;
* test adequacy;
* maintainability and complexity;
* unsupported dependency introduction;
* license or provenance risk;
* reviewer confidence;
* long-term rework.

AI risk-management principles are supported by the NIST AI Risk Management Framework and its generative-AI profile. See <<nist2023-airmf>> and <<nist2024-genai>>.

Management-system and risk-process standards are represented by <<iso42001>> and <<iso23894>>.

These standards guide governance and control; they do not prove that any particular AI system is effective or safe.

=== Economic evaluation

AI economics should include more than licensing or token cost.

The chapter’s cost model includes:

* subscription and platform cost;
* inference, retrieval, storage, and indexing;
* integration and administration;
* security and governance;
* human validation;
* false-positive handling;
* rework and rollback;
* migration and switching;
* vendor concentration and exit risk;
* quality-adjusted attention released.

The broader cost-benefit framework is grounded in <<boardman2018>>, while delayed productivity realization is informed by <<brynjolfsson1993>> and <<brynjolfsson2017>>.

Released minutes should not be reported as cash savings unless spending is actually avoided or removed.

=== Work-mix and heterogeneity

AI effects are likely to vary across:

* language and framework;
* repository maturity;
* change complexity;
* developer experience;
* task novelty;
* domain risk;
* available tests and documentation;
* model and prompt version;
* organization-specific tooling.

Average effects can conceal both high-value and harmful subgroups.

The chapter therefore recommends predeclared subgroup analysis, work-mix adjustment, and replication before broad policy.

=== Skill formation and capability effects

Short-term efficiency may coexist with long-term changes in skill.

Potential outcomes include:

* faster learning through explanation and examples;
* broader access to unfamiliar domains;
* reduced practice in core reasoning;
* weaker debugging or code-reading capability;
* dependence on model availability;
* changed mentoring and apprenticeship patterns;
* convergence toward model-preferred solutions.

The current evidence base is not mature enough to support universal conclusions. The chapter treats skill formation and atrophy as long-horizon research and governance questions requiring longitudinal study.

=== Automation bias and overreliance

Users may accept machine output too readily when it appears authoritative, fluent, or confident.

The chapter addresses this through:

* uncertainty and evidence display;
* required human accountability for consequential acceptance;
* independent validation for high-risk actions;
* calibrated confidence rather than stylistic certainty;
* logging of overrides and reversals;
* periodic tests of human capability without assistance.

Professional obligations concerning harm, competence, honesty, and accountability are informed by <<acm2018-ethics>>.

=== Agent authority and delegation

Autonomous agents require a richer governance model than interactive suggestions.

The chapter distinguishes:

* permission to inspect;
* permission to propose;
* permission to modify;
* permission to execute tests;
* permission to merge;
* permission to deploy;
* permission to alter infrastructure or data;
* permission to delegate to other agents.

Each action should carry actor identity, delegated authority, policy version, evidence, validation responsibility, and rollback path.

The delegation-graph and agent-authority model are original Engineering Intelligence extensions compatible with provenance concepts in <<moreau2013>> and risk-governance principles in <<nist2023-airmf>> and <<nist2024-genai>>.

=== Model monoculture and correlated risk

Broad adoption of one model or provider can create correlated failure modes:

* repeated insecure patterns;
* common reasoning blind spots;
* synchronized service disruption;
* shared supply-chain risk;
* systemic licensing or policy issues;
* reduced diversity of solution approaches.

The chapter therefore treats provider concentration, model diversity, fallback, and correlated-error testing as portfolio-level risk concerns.

This is a forward-looking operational extension rather than a settled empirical standard.

=== Continuous evaluation

One-time vendor evaluation is insufficient because models, prompts, repositories, and workflows change.

A continuous evaluation system should include:

* stable golden datasets;
* representative production samples;
* shadow comparisons;
* quality and safety guardrails;
* drift detection;
* model and prompt versioning;
* cost tracking;
* human disagreement review;
* downstream outcome follow-up.

Benchmark evidence, online experiment evidence, and production evidence should remain distinct but connected.

=== Original Engineering Intelligence contributions

The following are original synthesis or domain-specific extensions proposed by this book:

* AI as a first-class Engineering Change participant;
* machine-contribution provenance across model, prompt, retrieval, tools, and delegation;
* paired cognitive-offload and cognitive-reload measures;
* net attention economics;
* accepted-contribution share rather than acceptance alone;
* validation burden as a primary outcome;
* treatment-version drift as a causal and operational risk;
* continuous evaluation linked to downstream Engineering Change outcomes;
* agent authority and delegation graphs;
* autonomous-action reversal and rollback measures;
* model-monoculture and correlated-risk analysis;
* long-term skill and capability guardrails.

These constructs should be treated as hypotheses and operating standards to validate, not as settled universal laws.
