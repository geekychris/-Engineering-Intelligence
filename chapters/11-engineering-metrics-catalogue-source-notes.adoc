== Research foundations and source notes

The metric catalogue in this chapter draws on software-delivery research, multidimensional productivity frameworks, queueing theory, information retrieval, measurement validity, and indicator-governance research. It extends those foundations into a governed system of Engineering Intelligence measures tied to explicit decisions and Engineering Change evidence.

=== Delivery performance

The catalogue’s delivery and reliability measures build on the research synthesized in <<forsgren2018>>.

Measures such as deployment frequency, lead time, restoration behavior, and change-failure outcomes are useful because they describe system-level delivery capability rather than individual activity.

Their interpretation still depends on:

* service and repository boundaries;
* release and deployment definitions;
* work mix;
* risk class;
* observation window;
* outcome maturity;
* data quality.

The catalogue therefore treats delivery measures as governed metric products rather than universally comparable raw numbers.

=== Multidimensional productivity

The SPACE framework argues that productivity should be considered across satisfaction and well-being, performance, activity, communication and collaboration, and efficiency and flow. See <<forsgren2021-space>>.

The DevEx framework emphasizes feedback loops, cognitive load, and flow state. See <<noda2023-devex>>.

These foundations support the catalogue’s refusal to collapse engineering performance into one score.

Instead, the catalogue groups measures across:

* flow;
* quality;
* attention;
* coordination;
* architecture;
* AI participation;
* economics;
* experimentation;
* governance;
* evidence quality.

=== Queueing and flow

Little’s Law relates average work in progress, throughput, and average flow time. See <<little1961>>.

Kingman’s work and the operations literature explain why utilization and variability can create nonlinear waiting. See <<kingman1961>> and <<hopp2011>>.

These foundations support measures such as:

* work in progress;
* queue depth;
* arrival rate;
* service rate;
* service time;
* wait time;
* blocked time;
* tail latency;
* utilization of constrained resources;
* flow efficiency.

The catalogue distinguishes these measures because elapsed time alone cannot explain whether work is active, queued, blocked, or waiting for scarce attention.

=== Quality-adjusted flow

Faster delivery can be harmful when it increases rework, incidents, security findings, or operational burden.

The catalogue therefore pairs speed measures with guardrails such as:

* escaped defect rate;
* rollback rate;
* incident consequence;
* rework share;
* support burden;
* security and privacy findings;
* outcome durability.

This approach is consistent with the system-level delivery perspective in <<forsgren2018>> and the reliability principles in <<beyer2016>> and <<beyer2018>>.

=== Precision and recall

AI review tools, static analyzers, classifiers, and retrieval systems often produce findings that can be evaluated through precision and recall. See <<manning2008>>.

The catalogue requires explicit definitions for:

* what constitutes a finding;
* duplicate handling;
* relevance;
* severity;
* eligible population;
* adjudication;
* mature outcome window.

Without these definitions, precision and recall from different evaluations are not comparable.

=== Ranking metrics

When findings are ordered by importance, rank-sensitive measures become relevant.

Discounted cumulative gain and normalized discounted cumulative gain account for graded relevance and position. See <<jarvelin2002>>.

The catalogue uses ranking metrics where reviewer attention is scarce and high-value findings should appear early.

NDCG should not be interpreted as defect prevention by itself. It measures ranking quality against a defined relevance judgment.

=== Validity

Messick’s use-specific view of validity provides the foundation for requiring every metric to identify its intended interpretation and use. See <<messick1995>>.

Hand’s broader treatment of measurement reinforces the point that definitions and representation choices shape what can be concluded. See <<hand2004>>.

The catalogue therefore records:

* construct;
* decision purpose;
* eligible population;
* unit of analysis;
* scale and unit;
* computation;
* quality requirements;
* limitations;
* permitted and prohibited uses.

=== Reliability

A metric must be sufficiently consistent for its intended use.

Reliability concerns include:

* stable source semantics;
* reproducible transformation;
* labeler agreement;
* reconstruction consistency;
* metric-version determinism;
* temporal stability where expected.

Cronbach’s reliability work provides one important psychometric foundation. See <<cronbach1951>>.

Reliability alone is insufficient: a consistently biased metric can remain reliable while invalid.

=== Indicator gaming

Campbell’s Law and Goodhart’s Law describe the risk that indicators become corrupted when used as targets. See <<campbell1979>> and <<goodhart1984>>.

Catalogue measures can be gamed through:

* change slicing;
* superficial approvals;
* suppressed incident reporting;
* altered eligibility;
* trivial AI suggestions;
* exclusion of blocked work;
* shifted work outside the measured boundary.

Each high-consequence metric should therefore include a gaming analysis and balancing measures.

=== Denominators and exposure

Rates depend on governed denominators.

For example, an AI acceptance rate can be computed over all generated suggestions, displayed suggestions, actionable suggestions, unique suggestions, or suggestions with mature outcomes.

The catalogue requires explicit eligibility, exposure, and denominator contracts because each version answers a different question.

=== Distributions and tails

Engineering measures are often skewed or heavy-tailed.

The catalogue therefore favors:

* medians;
* quantiles;
* tail probabilities;
* distributions;
* subgroup views;
* uncertainty intervals;
* sample counts.

Average incident cost, review latency, or change size can be dominated by a small number of extreme observations.

=== Attention measures

The catalogue’s attention measures draw on cognitive-load theory and interruption research. See <<sweller1988>> and <<mark2008>>.

Relevant measures include:

* review and validation effort;
* context-recovery proxies;
* fragmentation;
* scarce-specialist demand;
* queue delay;
* interruption frequency;
* attention yield.

These measures are latent or model-based and should be reported with uncertainty and privacy controls.

=== Coordination measures

Coordination metrics are informed by Conway’s Law, socio-technical congruence, and distributed-development research. See <<conway1968>>, <<cataldo2006>>, and <<herbsleb2007>>.

Relevant measures include:

* team and ownership boundaries;
* handoffs;
* approval boundaries;
* synchronization points;
* owner-discovery time;
* dependency-related waiting;
* ownership entropy.

These measures should describe system structure rather than serve as team rankings.

=== Architecture measures

Architecture metrics are informed by information hiding and modularity in <<parnas1972>> and team cognitive-load concepts in <<skelton2019>>.

The catalogue includes measures for:

* dependency depth;
* fan-in and fan-out;
* change coupling;
* cycles;
* validation surface;
* reversibility;
* blast radius;
* cognitive surface.

Structural measures should be calibrated against observed flow, effort, incidents, and rework.

=== AI measures

The AI metric family is informed by coding-assistant studies in <<peng2023>>, <<vaithilingam2022>>, and <<barke2023>>, and repository-level agent benchmarks in <<jimenez2024>>.

The catalogue distinguishes:

* generation;
* exposure;
* interaction;
* acceptance;
* contribution;
* validation burden;
* downstream quality;
* net attention effect;
* economic realization.

Acceptance rate alone is treated as insufficient evidence of value.

=== Economic measures

Economic measures draw on cost-benefit analysis in <<boardman2018>> and productivity-realization research in <<brynjolfsson1993>> and <<brynjolfsson2017>>.

The catalogue distinguishes:

* cash savings;
* avoided cost;
* released capacity;
* modeled outcome value;
* risk reduction;
* option value;
* realized value.

This separation reduces the risk of presenting theoretical time savings as actual financial savings.

=== Causal status

A metric movement is not automatically a causal effect.

The catalogue labels values as:

* observed;
* reconstructed;
* inferred;
* predicted;
* experimentally estimated;
* causally estimated under declared assumptions.

The methodological foundation comes from <<rubin1974>>, <<holland1986>>, <<pearl2009>>, <<imbens2015>>, and <<hernan2020>>.

=== Data-quality metrics

Metric quality depends on source and linkage quality.

The catalogue therefore includes:

* source freshness;
* completeness;
* schema conformance;
* identity-resolution rate;
* Engineering Change linkage coverage;
* confidence calibration;
* outcome maturity;
* correction rate;
* drift.

This follows the validity principle that evidence quality is part of interpretation rather than a separate implementation concern.

=== Governance metrics

Governance measures track whether the measurement system is being used responsibly.

Examples include:

* percentage of metrics with owners;
* percentage with prohibited-use declarations;
* access-review completion;
* correction and appeal resolution;
* metric incidents;
* stale or deprecated definitions;
* unsupported individual-level uses;
* decision follow-up completion.

Privacy and professional principles are informed by <<nist2020-privacy>> and <<acm2018-ethics>>.

=== Original Engineering Intelligence contributions

The following are original synthesis or domain-specific extensions proposed by this book:

* the unified metric catalogue spanning flow, quality, attention, economics, AI, architecture, governance, and evidence quality;
* Engineering Change as the default aggregation unit;
* net attention effect;
* cognitive offload and reload;
* accepted-contribution share;
* validation surface and cognitive surface;
* ownership entropy;
* outcome-maturity metrics;
* reconstruction-quality metrics;
* explicit causal-status labels;
* value-realization measures linked to decision records;
* prohibited-use and quality-state fields as part of every metric definition.

These measures should be calibrated, versioned, and validated in each organizational context rather than treated as universal constants.
