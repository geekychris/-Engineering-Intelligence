= The Science of Engineering Intelligence

[abstract]
Engineering Intelligence becomes a discipline only when its claims can be tested, reproduced, challenged, and improved. This chapter defines the scientific foundations of the field: its objects of study, theory-building practices, measurement standards, replication requirements, benchmark design, shared datasets, reporting conventions, and research agenda. It argues that engineering organizations should not merely consume metrics, but participate in a cumulative science of software delivery.

== From analytics practice to scientific discipline

Engineering analytics is often local and ephemeral. A team builds a dashboard, answers an immediate question, and then moves on. Definitions remain undocumented, causal assumptions remain implicit, and results cannot be compared across organizations.

A scientific discipline requires more.

It needs:

* explicit constructs;
* observable units of analysis;
* falsifiable hypotheses;
* reliable measurement;
* transparent uncertainty;
* reproducible methods;
* cumulative evidence;
* mechanisms for correction.

Engineering Intelligence should aspire to these properties while recognizing that software delivery is a changing socio-technical system rather than a controlled physical environment.

== The object of study

The primary object of study is not the individual engineer. It is the production and operation of Engineering Changes within an organizational system.

An Engineering Change connects:

* an intended outcome;
* human and machine participants;
* code, configuration, data, and infrastructure artifacts;
* review and validation activity;
* deployment and release mechanisms;
* production consequences;
* economic cost and value;
* organizational and architectural context.

This unit permits analysis across tools and workflow implementations while reducing the temptation to equate visible activity with individual performance.

== Levels of explanation

Engineering Intelligence requires explanations at multiple levels.

=== Artifact level

Properties of code, tests, configuration, models, schemas, and infrastructure definitions.

=== Change level

The complete sequence through which an intended modification is designed, implemented, reviewed, validated, deployed, and observed.

=== Queue and workflow level

How work competes for reviewers, CI capacity, environments, approvals, and release windows.

=== Team level

Coordination structure, expertise distribution, ownership, interruptions, and local operating practices.

=== Architecture level

Dependencies, coupling, interfaces, blast radius, and the constraints imposed by the software system.

=== Portfolio level

Allocation of engineering capacity across products, platforms, risk reduction, modernization, compliance, and exploration.

=== Enterprise level

Capital allocation, governance, talent systems, vendor strategy, and the aggregate relationship between engineering investment and organizational outcomes.

A useful theory should state the level at which it operates and the mechanisms that connect it to other levels.

== Constructs and observables

Many important engineering concepts cannot be observed directly.

Examples include:

* cognitive load;
* change complexity;
* review quality;
* coordination cost;
* architectural risk;
* developer experience;
* organizational learning;
* engineering value.

These are constructs. They must be represented through observable indicators, and those indicators must be validated.

For example, cognitive load might be estimated through interruption frequency, concurrent work, context switching, review span, survey responses, and task-reconstruction studies. No single signal should automatically be treated as the construct itself.

== A theory-to-evidence chain

Every important claim should be traceable through a chain:

. construct;
. proposed mechanism;
. observable indicators;
. measurement model;
. hypothesis;
. study design;
. analysis;
. decision implication;
. replication status.

image::../figures/mermaid/science-evidence-cycle.mmd[The Engineering Intelligence scientific evidence cycle]

This chain discourages the common pattern in which a convenient telemetry field becomes a metric, the metric becomes a target, and the target becomes an unsupported claim about performance.

== Falsifiable hypotheses

A hypothesis should describe an expected relationship that evidence could contradict.

Weak statement:

[quote]
AI code review improves engineering productivity.

Stronger statement:

[quote]
For eligible medium-risk pull requests, enabling the AI reviewer will reduce median human review effort by at least 15 percent without increasing escaped-severity-one-or-two defects, after controlling for repository and change complexity.

The stronger statement defines:

* population;
* treatment;
* expected effect;
* outcome;
* guardrail;
* comparison;
* adjustment variables.

Falsifiability forces clarity before data is examined.

== Descriptive, predictive, causal, and prescriptive claims

Claims must be labeled by type.

=== Descriptive

What happened?

Example: review wait time increased during the quarter.

=== Predictive

What is likely to happen?

Example: this change has a high probability of requiring rollback.

=== Causal

What would happen under an intervention?

Example: introducing a merge queue reduced integration failures.

=== Prescriptive

What action should be taken given objectives and constraints?

Example: route this change to a security specialist and delay deployment until validation completes.

A predictive model does not establish that acting on its prediction will improve outcomes. A correlation does not establish an intervention effect. A prescriptive recommendation requires explicit objectives, costs, and guardrails.

== Measurement validity

A scientifically useful metric requires several forms of validity.

=== Construct validity

Does the metric represent the intended concept?

=== Content validity

Does it cover the important dimensions of that concept?

=== Criterion validity

Does it agree with a credible external reference or outcome?

=== Convergent validity

Do different measures of the same construct move together where expected?

=== Discriminant validity

Can the metric distinguish the construct from related but different concepts?

=== Ecological validity

Does the measure remain meaningful in real engineering conditions?

A metric can be computed correctly and still be scientifically invalid.

== Reliability and repeatability

Reliability asks whether measurement is stable under equivalent conditions.

Sources of unreliability include:

* inconsistent source events;
* identity-resolution errors;
* changing workflow semantics;
* annotation disagreement;
* unstable model inference;
* non-deterministic reconstruction;
* incomplete deployment linkage;
* hidden metric-version changes.

Reliability should be reported using suitable methods such as test-retest stability, inter-rater agreement, internal consistency, reconstruction agreement, and confidence intervals.

== Measurement invariance

A metric may behave differently across languages, repositories, teams, business domains, or workflow types.

Before comparing groups, ask whether the measure has equivalent meaning.

For example, pull-request size may be a reasonable complexity indicator in one codebase and nearly meaningless in a generated-code repository. Review count may represent diligence in one workflow and mandatory approval policy in another.

Cross-group comparison requires evidence of measurement invariance or explicit qualification.

== Calibration

Risk scores and probability estimates should be calibrated.

If a model assigns 0.30 defect probability to many changes, approximately 30 percent of comparable changes should exhibit the defined outcome over time.

Calibration should be assessed:

* globally;
* by repository and domain;
* by complexity band;
* by model version;
* by important subpopulation;
* under distribution shift.

A highly ranked model can still be operationally misleading when its probabilities are poorly calibrated.

== Ground truth is constructed

Engineering datasets rarely contain perfect truth.

Defect labels may depend on:

* whether an incident was detected;
* whether it was linked to the correct change;
* how severity was assigned;
* whether a rollback represented failure or precaution;
* whether a reviewer recognized the issue;
* whether the organization records near misses.

Ground-truth creation should document adjudication rules, uncertainty, disagreement, missing outcomes, and label provenance.

== Annotation science

Human labeling is itself a measurement process.

A rigorous annotation protocol should specify:

* label ontology;
* inclusion and exclusion rules;
* examples and counterexamples;
* annotator qualifications;
* blinded or non-blinded conditions;
* calibration exercises;
* disagreement resolution;
* confidence capture;
* periodic drift review.

For difficult judgments, preserve annotator distributions rather than collapsing all disagreement into a single authoritative label.

== Benchmark design

Benchmarks can accelerate progress, but they can also distort it.

A useful Engineering Intelligence benchmark should include:

* a clearly defined task;
* representative populations;
* realistic class imbalance;
* temporal splits;
* repository separation where appropriate;
* leakage controls;
* uncertainty and annotation metadata;
* cost-sensitive evaluation;
* subgroup reporting;
* a protected holdout set.

Benchmarks should reward useful operational behavior, not merely leaderboard optimization.

== Retrieval and ranking evaluation

Many Engineering Intelligence systems rank findings, changes, experts, dependencies, or interventions.

Evaluation should therefore include ranking measures such as:

* precision at k;
* recall at k;
* mean reciprocal rank;
* normalized discounted cumulative gain;
* average precision;
* expected utility at review budget;
* severe-finding recall;
* time-to-first-actionable result.

A system that finds the right issue but buries it below twenty low-value comments may be technically correct and operationally ineffective.

== Economic evaluation of models

Model quality should be translated into consequences.

Relevant quantities include:

* expert minutes consumed;
* false-positive validation cost;
* defect cost avoided;
* latency introduced;
* infrastructure and inference cost;
* opportunity cost;
* adoption effects;
* risk concentration.

The best statistical model is not necessarily the best engineering intervention.

== Internal replication

Before broad rollout, an organization should replicate important findings across:

* multiple teams;
* different repositories;
* time periods;
* workflow variants;
* complexity strata;
* organizational boundaries.

Internal replication tests whether a result is local, seasonal, policy-dependent, or robust enough to guide enterprise decisions.

== External replication

Cross-organization replication is difficult because data and workflows differ, but it is essential for a cumulative field.

External studies should distinguish:

* exact replication of method;
* conceptual replication of mechanism;
* transportability to a new population;
* adaptation to a different workflow;
* failure to reproduce.

Non-replication should be treated as evidence about boundary conditions, not merely as embarrassment.

== Reproducibility packages

A publishable Engineering Intelligence study should provide, where privacy and security permit:

* research question and hypotheses;
* analysis plan;
* metric definitions and versions;
* event and entity schemas;
* code or executable specifications;
* data-quality report;
* sample construction logic;
* statistical model details;
* robustness checks;
* synthetic or de-identified example data;
* decision record.

Where raw data cannot be shared, organizations can still publish transformation logic, schemas, synthetic fixtures, and reproducible aggregate analyses.

== Pre-registration

For consequential experiments, pre-register:

* hypotheses;
* primary and secondary outcomes;
* guardrails;
* eligibility;
* assignment;
* exposure definition;
* exclusion rules;
* sample-size rationale;
* stopping criteria;
* analysis model;
* subgroup analyses.

Pre-registration does not prevent exploration. It distinguishes confirmatory evidence from discoveries made after inspecting the data.

== Registered reports

A stronger practice is to review the study question and method before results are known.

Registered reports reduce publication bias and encourage organizations to value well-designed negative findings. The result becomes informative because the method was credible, not because the effect was exciting.

== Negative results

Engineering organizations often suppress negative results:

* a tool produced no measurable benefit;
* an intervention helped only one workflow;
* a model failed under real operational load;
* a metric could not be validated;
* an expected effect disappeared after adjustment.

These findings prevent repeated waste. A mature discipline records them.

== Meta-analysis

As studies accumulate, meta-analysis can estimate effect distributions rather than relying on one headline number.

For example, the effect of AI review on human effort may vary by:

* programming language;
* change complexity;
* reviewer expertise;
* repository maturity;
* model family;
* prompting strategy;
* organizational policy.

A random-effects view is often more realistic than assuming one universal effect.

== Transportability

Decision makers must ask whether evidence from one setting applies to another.

Transportability depends on similarities in:

* population;
* architecture;
* workflow;
* incentives;
* tooling;
* risk tolerance;
* outcome definition;
* intervention fidelity.

The burden is not to prove that environments are identical, but to identify which differences could alter the causal mechanism.

== Natural experiments

Engineering organizations continually produce quasi-experimental variation:

* phased tool rollouts;
* policy thresholds;
* infrastructure outages;
* team migrations;
* vendor changes;
* repository transfers;
* staggered platform adoption.

With careful assumptions, methods such as difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, interrupted time series, and instrumental variables can extract useful evidence.

Natural experiments should not be presented as randomized trials. Their identifying assumptions must be explicit and tested where possible.

== Longitudinal research

Many effects appear only over time.

Examples include:

* skill development or atrophy;
* architectural degradation;
* review-culture change;
* accumulated automation debt;
* model adaptation;
* organizational learning;
* delayed quality outcomes.

Longitudinal designs should distinguish short-term novelty effects from sustained change.

== System adaptation

Engineering systems react to measurement and intervention.

People learn the metric. Teams alter workflow. Models change the distribution of work. Policies redirect attention. Vendors update products.

This means an intervention effect is not permanently fixed. Engineering Intelligence requires continuous revalidation and explicit study of adaptation.

== Multi-method research

Telemetry alone cannot explain every mechanism.

Strong studies may combine:

* event data;
* controlled experiments;
* repository mining;
* surveys;
* interviews;
* cognitive walkthroughs;
* diary studies;
* observational fieldwork;
* incident review;
* economic modeling.

Qualitative evidence is especially valuable for discovering mechanisms, interpreting anomalies, and identifying constructs not captured by instrumentation.

== Shared research infrastructure

A cumulative discipline benefits from shared infrastructure:

* canonical event contracts;
* Engineering Change schemas;
* metric definition registries;
* benchmark task specifications;
* synthetic data generators;
* experiment templates;
* analysis libraries;
* privacy-preserving collaboration methods;
* replication registries;
* evidence maps.

Standardization should support comparison without freezing local innovation.

== Privacy-preserving collaboration

Organizations may collaborate without exchanging raw proprietary data.

Possible approaches include:

* federated analysis;
* secure aggregation;
* differential privacy;
* common query packages executed locally;
* standardized aggregate reporting;
* synthetic datasets;
* trusted research enclaves.

Privacy protection must be evaluated against re-identification risk, especially for small teams and rare events.

== Evidence maturity levels

Claims can be classified by maturity.

=== Level 0: anecdote

A plausible observation without systematic measurement.

=== Level 1: descriptive evidence

A measured pattern with documented definitions and data quality.

=== Level 2: replicated association

A relationship observed across populations or periods with relevant adjustment.

=== Level 3: credible causal evidence

A randomized or defensible quasi-experimental estimate.

=== Level 4: replicated causal mechanism

Consistent causal evidence with understood boundary conditions.

=== Level 5: operationally validated policy

A mechanism translated into a governed intervention with durable net benefit.

The maturity level should accompany important recommendations.

== Scientific reporting standard

Every major result should state:

* question;
* claim type;
* population;
* unit of analysis;
* period;
* treatment or exposure;
* outcome definition;
* metric versions;
* data coverage;
* uncertainty;
* design assumptions;
* robustness checks;
* limitations;
* replication status;
* decision consequence.

This reporting standard makes results easier to audit and compare.

== An open evidence graph

Over time, studies can be represented as an evidence graph connecting:

* constructs;
* metrics;
* datasets;
* interventions;
* populations;
* mechanisms;
* findings;
* replications;
* contradictions;
* decisions.

Such a graph would allow decision makers to inspect not only the latest result, but the complete provenance and maturity of a claim.

== Research agenda

Important research areas include:

=== Engineering Change reconstruction

How accurately can changes be reconstructed across fragmented enterprise systems?

=== Cognitive load

Which behavioral and self-reported measures best predict harmful overload without becoming surveillance instruments?

=== AI collaboration

When do AI participants substitute for, complement, or increase human effort?

=== Review science

What review structures maximize defect discovery, knowledge transfer, and attention efficiency?

=== Architecture and organizational structure

How do dependency topology, ownership, and team design interact?

=== Value attribution

How can engineering investment be linked to product, reliability, and strategic outcomes without false precision?

=== Metric governance

Which institutional practices prevent gaming while preserving useful accountability?

=== Long-run capability

How do automation and AI affect learning, expertise, and resilience over years rather than weeks?

== Institutional design for research

Large organizations should create mechanisms for cumulative learning:

* a research review board for consequential studies;
* a registry of experiments and natural experiments;
* standard evidence packages;
* reusable benchmark datasets;
* protected publication of negative findings;
* practitioner and domain review;
* periodic synthesis of accumulated evidence.

This need not become academic bureaucracy. Its purpose is to reduce repeated weak studies and unsupported enterprise decisions.

== Anti-patterns

=== Dashboard empiricism

A visible pattern is treated as explanation.

=== Metric essentialism

A proxy is treated as the underlying construct.

=== One-company universalism

A local result is presented as a law of software engineering.

=== Benchmark overfitting

Systems optimize a public test while losing operational usefulness.

=== Causal language without causal design

Associations are described as improvements caused by an intervention.

=== Hidden researcher degrees of freedom

Outcomes, exclusions, and models are selected after results are visible.

=== Publication of winners only

Negative and contradictory findings disappear.

=== Permanent validity

A metric or model is assumed to remain valid after the system adapts.

== Key takeaways

* Engineering Intelligence should develop as a cumulative empirical discipline, not a collection of dashboards.
* The Engineering Change is the primary unit of study, embedded in workflow, architecture, organizational, and economic context.
* Constructs must be separated from their observable indicators and validated explicitly.
* Claims must distinguish description, prediction, causation, and prescription.
* Reproducibility, replication, negative results, and transportability are essential.
* Benchmarks must represent operational utility, uncertainty, cost, and realistic populations.
* Scientific findings require continuous revalidation because engineering systems adapt.
* Shared schemas, methods, and privacy-preserving collaboration can create an evidence base larger than any one organization.

== Open questions

* Which constructs should become foundational standards for Engineering Intelligence?
* What minimum evidence package should be required before an enterprise metric influences policy?
* How can organizations share replication results without exposing proprietary systems or employee data?
* Which engineering effects are stable enough to generalize across companies?
* How should contradictory evidence be represented in executive decision products?
* Can an open evidence graph become a practical foundation for cumulative engineering science?

== Scientific practice checklist

. State the construct, mechanism, population, and unit of analysis.
. Label the claim as descriptive, predictive, causal, or prescriptive.
. Version metrics, data, models, and analysis logic.
. Report coverage, missingness, reliability, validity, and uncertainty.
. Pre-register consequential confirmatory studies.
. Preserve exploratory analysis as exploratory.
. Test calibration, subgroup behavior, and transportability.
. Replicate before broad policy adoption.
. Publish negative and contradictory results internally.
. Revalidate after workflow, model, or incentive changes.
. Package studies for reproduction without exposing sensitive data.
. Connect findings to an explicit evidence maturity level and decision record.
