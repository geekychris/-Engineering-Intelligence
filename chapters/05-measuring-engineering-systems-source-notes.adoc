== Research foundations and source notes

The measurement framework in this chapter draws on classical measurement theory, psychometrics, construct validity, indicator governance, developer-productivity research, and statistical reasoning. It extends those foundations into a governed metric system for software engineering.

=== Measurement scales and permissible operations

Stevens’s classification of nominal, ordinal, interval, and ratio scales provides the basic framework for understanding which mathematical operations are meaningful for a measure. See <<stevens1946>>.

The practical implication is that engineering data types that look numeric are not automatically interchangeable.

Examples include:

* severity classes may be ordinal rather than interval;
* confidence bands may be ordered categories rather than probabilities;
* counts may be ratio-scale values but still require exposure or population normalization;
* ranks preserve order but not meaningful distance;
* composite scores may not support multiplication or ratio interpretation.

The chapter therefore requires every metric specification to identify its unit, scale, aggregation behavior, and interpretation.

=== Construct definition

A construct is the underlying concept an organization seeks to understand, such as maintainability, cognitive load, delivery responsiveness, review effectiveness, or quality.

Messick’s treatment of validity emphasizes that validity concerns the evidence supporting an intended interpretation and use, not merely the mechanical correctness of a score. See <<messick1995>>.

Hand provides a broader treatment of how measurement choices shape what can be observed and concluded. See <<hand2004>>.

This supports a central rule in the chapter:

[quote]
A metric is not valid in the abstract. It is more or less valid for a specific construct, population, decision, and consequence.

=== Reliability

Reliability concerns the consistency and reproducibility of measurement.

Cronbach’s coefficient alpha is one influential method for evaluating internal consistency among indicators. See <<cronbach1951>>. The broader principle is that a measure should behave consistently enough for its intended use.

Engineering examples include:

* whether independent labelers agree on review findings;
* whether a reconstruction model produces stable Engineering Change membership;
* whether a survey scale behaves consistently across repeated administrations;
* whether a metric changes because the system changed or because instrumentation changed;
* whether identical evidence produces the same metric version.

Reliability is necessary but not sufficient. A consistently wrong measure can be highly reliable and invalid.

=== Content, criterion, and construct evidence

The chapter’s validation model includes several forms of evidence.

*Content evidence* asks whether the metric covers the important dimensions of the construct.

*Criterion evidence* asks whether the metric relates appropriately to an external outcome or reference.

*Convergent evidence* asks whether it agrees with different measures of the same construct.

*Discriminant evidence* asks whether it remains distinct from related but different constructs.

*Predictive evidence* asks whether it forecasts relevant future outcomes.

*Consequential evidence* asks what happens when the metric is used in practice.

This integrated treatment follows the use-oriented validity perspective in <<messick1995>>.

=== Measurement invariance

A metric may not mean the same thing across teams, technologies, work types, or time.

Examples include:

* story points with different local semantics;
* pull-request size under different repository strategies;
* review latency where review policies differ;
* incident counts where reporting culture differs;
* AI acceptance where tools expose different suggestion units;
* survey responses across organizational cultures.

The chapter therefore requires subgroup, temporal, and workflow checks before cross-population comparison.

Measurement invariance is not guaranteed by a shared column name or dashboard label.

=== Multidimensional engineering productivity

The SPACE framework argues that developer productivity should be considered across satisfaction and well-being, performance, activity, communication and collaboration, and efficiency and flow. See <<forsgren2021-space>>.

The DevEx framework emphasizes feedback loops, cognitive load, and flow state. See <<noda2023-devex>>.

Developer-experience research also supports the importance of practitioner perception and context. See <<fagerholm2012>> and <<meyer2014>>.

These foundations support the chapter’s metric-bundle approach rather than a universal productivity score.

A useful bundle often includes:

* primary outcome;
* quality and safety guardrails;
* mechanism indicators;
* attention or experience measures;
* adoption and exposure measures;
* economic measures;
* data-quality measures.

=== Delivery performance

Delivery-performance measures such as deployment frequency, lead time, restoration time, and change-failure behavior are important system-level outcomes. The research synthesis in <<forsgren2018>> provides the principal foundation used by this book.

These measures should still be interpreted with care:

* definitions differ across systems;
* repository and service boundaries matter;
* release and deployment are not always identical;
* work mix and risk vary;
* averages may conceal tails and subgroups;
* local optimization can damage another outcome.

The chapter therefore treats delivery measures as governed metrics within a larger system rather than complete proxies for engineering performance.

=== Queue and flow measurement

Little’s Law relates average work in progress, throughput, and flow time. See <<little1961>>.

Kingman’s work and the operations literature describe the nonlinear effect of utilization and variability on waiting. See <<kingman1961>> and <<hopp2011>>.

These foundations support measures such as:

* queue depth;
* wait time;
* service time;
* blocked time;
* work in progress;
* throughput;
* arrival and service variability;
* tail latency;
* utilization of constrained resources.

They also explain why reducing one stage’s average duration may not improve end-to-end flow.

=== Indicator corruption and gaming

Campbell warned that when a quantitative indicator is used heavily for social decision-making, it becomes vulnerable to corruption and can distort the process it is intended to monitor. See <<campbell1979>>.

Goodhart’s Law expresses a related principle: once a measure becomes a target, it may cease to function as a useful measure. See <<goodhart1984>>.

Engineering examples include:

* increasing commit count by splitting trivial changes;
* reducing review time by approving superficially;
* reducing incident counts by discouraging incident declaration;
* improving story-point velocity by changing estimates;
* increasing AI acceptance by suggesting trivial edits;
* reducing cycle time by excluding blocked or failed work.

The chapter therefore requires gaming analysis, balancing measures, and prohibited uses in the metric specification.

=== Missing data

Missing values have different meanings.

A field may represent:

* true zero;
* not applicable;
* not instrumented;
* not observed;
* delayed evidence;
* unresolved linkage;
* suppressed data;
* failed processing;
* outcome not yet mature.

Collapsing these states into zero or null can materially bias a metric.

The chapter treats missingness as part of metric semantics and quality rather than a downstream cleanup issue.

=== Denominators and eligibility

A rate is only meaningful when its denominator is governed.

For example, AI-finding acceptance may be defined over:

* all generated findings;
* findings shown to a human;
* findings eligible for disposition;
* unique findings after deduplication;
* findings with mature outcomes;
* findings within supported languages or repositories.

Different denominators answer different questions.

The chapter therefore requires executable eligibility and denominator definitions for every rate or proportion.

=== Aggregation and ecological error

Group-level patterns do not automatically describe individuals, and aggregate comparisons can reverse when population composition changes.

The chapter’s work-mix adjustment, subgroup analysis, and restriction on individual interpretation are designed to reduce ecological and compositional errors.

A team with longer cycle time may handle more complex, risky, or cross-system changes. A portfolio average can improve because easy work increased rather than because the process improved.

=== Distributions rather than averages

Engineering measures are often skewed, heavy-tailed, censored, or multimodal.

The chapter therefore recommends reporting:

* medians and quantiles;
* tail probabilities;
* distribution plots;
* stratification by relevant work classes;
* uncertainty intervals;
* sample and eligibility counts;
* mature versus immature outcomes.

Averages remain useful, but they should not be the sole representation of flow, cost, review effort, or incident impact.

=== Precision, recall, and ranking

When engineering tools produce findings or ranked recommendations, information-retrieval measures become relevant.

Precision and recall are grounded in the standard information-retrieval treatment in <<manning2008>>.

Discounted cumulative gain and normalized discounted cumulative gain support evaluation of ranked results where the position and graded relevance of findings matter. See <<jarvelin2002>>.

The chapter’s evaluation approach also requires:

* adjudication rules;
* duplicate handling;
* partial relevance;
* severity weighting;
* finding-level and change-level analysis;
* confidence intervals;
* disagreement review.

=== Calibration

A probabilistic score should be interpreted through calibration as well as discrimination.

A model assigning 0.8 probability should be correct approximately 80 percent of the time in the relevant calibrated population.

The chapter extends this principle beyond predictive models to:

* Engineering Change linkage confidence;
* defect-risk estimates;
* blast-radius predictions;
* finding-confidence scores;
* automated policy thresholds.

Calibration must be evaluated by population and over time because drift can invalidate earlier interpretations.

=== Metric lineage and reproducibility

A published metric should be traceable to:

* source evidence;
* source and schema versions;
* identity and linkage logic;
* transformations;
* metric version;
* quality state;
* publication time;
* correction history.

This requirement is compatible with the provenance concepts in <<moreau2013>> and the replayable data-system principles in <<kleppmann2017>> and <<akidau2018>>.

A metric without a reproducible definition and evidence path is a report, not decision-grade evidence.

=== Metric governance

Metric governance is part of measurement quality because access, purpose, interpretation, and consequence affect the validity of use.

The chapter therefore requires:

* accountable owner;
* decision purpose;
* permitted and prohibited uses;
* quality threshold;
* review cadence;
* versioning and deprecation;
* correction procedure;
* privacy and aggregation controls.

Privacy-risk principles are informed by <<nist2020-privacy>>, while professional concerns around harm, fairness, honesty, and accountability are informed by <<acm2018-ethics>>.

=== Original Engineering Intelligence contributions

The following are original synthesis or domain-specific extensions proposed by this book:

* a metric as a versioned decision product rather than a query;
* a required decision-purpose field;
* executable eligibility and denominator contracts;
* explicit prohibited-use declarations;
* a quality state published with every metric result;
* policy-grade metric maturity;
* integration of source, linkage, identity, and outcome maturity into metric quality;
* metric incidents and rollback;
* decision-specific validity thresholds;
* metric bundles connecting outcomes, guardrails, mechanisms, economics, exposure, and quality;
* mandatory distinction among observed, reconstructed, inferred, predicted, and causal values.

These constructs should be validated through practical use, correction records, decision outcomes, practitioner review, and periodic independent audit.
