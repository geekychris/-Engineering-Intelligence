== Research foundations and source notes

The experimentation framework in this chapter draws on the potential-outcomes tradition, structural causal models, randomized experiments, quasi-experimental design, preregistration, and practical online experimentation. It extends those foundations into an Engineering Intelligence study system connected to Engineering Changes, tool exposure, guardrails, and economic outcomes.

=== Potential outcomes and the causal question

The potential-outcomes framework defines causal effects by comparing outcomes that would occur under different treatment conditions for the same unit. See <<rubin1974>>, <<holland1986>>, <<imbens2015>>, and <<hernan2020>>.

For an Engineering Change, team, repository, or service stem:[i], let:

[stem]
++++
Y_i(1)
++++

represent the outcome under treatment and:

[stem]
++++
Y_i(0)
++++

represent the outcome under the comparator.

The individual causal effect is:

[stem]
++++
\tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)
++++

Only one potential outcome can normally be observed for a given unit at a given time. This is the fundamental problem of causal inference described in <<holland1986>>.

The purpose of experimental and quasi-experimental design is to construct a credible estimate of the missing counterfactual.

=== Estimands before estimators

A study should define the causal quantity of interest before selecting a statistical method.

Relevant estimands include:

* average treatment effect;
* average treatment effect on treated units;
* intention-to-treat effect;
* treatment-on-the-treated effect;
* complier average causal effect;
* noninferiority margin;
* effect within a declared Engineering Change class;
* effect on the median, tail, or probability of a harmful outcome.

The distinction among assignment, exposure, adoption, and treatment compliance determines which estimands are identifiable.

This ordering follows the design-first perspective in <<imbens2015>> and <<hernan2020>>.

=== Randomization

Random assignment provides a principled way to make treatment groups comparable in expectation.

Randomization does not guarantee that every observed sample will be balanced, nor does it solve problems of missing outcomes, noncompliance, spillover, measurement error, or treatment changes.

The chapter therefore requires:

* deterministic and auditable assignment;
* a declared assignment unit;
* balance and sample-ratio checks;
* treatment-version recording;
* outcome and exposure quality monitoring;
* analysis consistent with the assignment design.

These requirements are consistent with randomized-experiment foundations in <<rubin1974>>, <<shadish2002>>, <<imbens2015>>, and practical experimentation guidance in <<kohavi2020>>.

=== Assignment unit and analysis unit

The randomization unit may differ from the analysis unit.

Examples include:

* assigning repositories while analyzing Engineering Changes;
* assigning teams while analyzing individual deployments;
* assigning time windows while analyzing review events;
* assigning reviewers while analyzing review findings;
* assigning pull requests while analyzing downstream incidents.

When assignment is clustered, standard errors and power calculations must account for within-cluster dependence. Treating correlated observations as independent can materially overstate precision.

=== Treatment exposure

Assignment is not equivalent to treatment exposure.

A repository may be assigned an AI review tool while the tool fails to run, does not support the language, produces no visible findings, or is bypassed by the developer. A developer may have access to a coding assistant without using it for the observed task.

The chapter therefore requires observed exposure evidence such as:

* treatment invocation;
* version and configuration;
* successful completion;
* visible output;
* interaction or disposition;
* duration and dosage;
* fallback or crossover.

Intention-to-treat analysis should generally preserve original assignment. Exposure-based analyzes may be useful but can reintroduce selection bias unless supported by an appropriate identification strategy.

=== Causal graphs

Structural causal models and directed acyclic graphs provide a language for representing causal assumptions, confounding, mediation, selection, and collider bias. See <<pearl2009>>.

A causal graph is not evidence by itself. It is a formal statement of assumptions that helps determine:

* which variables should be controlled;
* which variables should not be controlled;
* what mechanisms are hypothesized;
* where selection can bias the estimate;
* which outcomes and mediators require separate analysis.

For example, controlling for a post-treatment variable such as tool usage or review comments can distort an intention-to-treat estimate when that variable is itself affected by treatment.

=== Confounding

A confounder influences both treatment selection and outcome.

In engineering organizations, common confounders include:

* team maturity;
* repository complexity;
* work mix;
* developer experience;
* release season;
* organizational change;
* incident load;
* language and framework;
* voluntary tool adoption;
* leadership sponsorship.

Randomization addresses measured and unmeasured pre-treatment confounding in expectation. Observational studies must rely on stronger assumptions and design strategies.

=== Interference and spillover

The stable-unit-treatment-value assumption can fail in software organizations because one unit’s treatment can affect another unit’s outcome.

Examples include:

* treated and untreated developers sharing code or reviewers;
* AI-generated patterns propagating across repositories;
* one team’s merge queue changing another team’s dependency timing;
* a treated reviewer influencing untreated pull requests;
* shared CI capacity creating cross-group queue effects.

The chapter therefore requires explicit interference analysis and may use cluster randomization, network-aware assignment, geographic or organizational separation, saturation designs, or spillover estimands.

These concerns are part of the design and validity traditions discussed in <<shadish2002>>, <<imbens2015>>, and <<hernan2020>>.

=== Noncompliance and crossover

Units may not receive their assigned treatment, and control units may gain access to treatment.

The study should distinguish:

* assignment failure;
* treatment unavailability;
* user non-use;
* technical incompatibility;
* contamination;
* deliberate crossover;
* treatment interruption.

Intention-to-treat remains a primary policy estimand because it measures the effect of assigning the intervention under realistic compliance. Instrumental-variable methods may estimate effects among compliers under additional assumptions. See <<angrist2009>> and <<imbens2015>>.

=== Quasi-experimental designs

Randomization is not always feasible. The chapter therefore includes several quasi-experimental methods.

==== Difference-in-differences

Difference-in-differences compares changes over time between treated and comparison groups.

Its credibility depends on assumptions such as parallel untreated trends, no treatment anticipation, stable composition, and appropriate handling of staggered timing.

The method is discussed in <<angrist2009>> and broader causal-inference references including <<hernan2020>>.

==== Regression discontinuity

Regression discontinuity uses a treatment threshold in an assignment variable.

It can provide strong local causal evidence when the threshold is enforced, the running variable cannot be manipulated precisely, and outcomes are smooth around the cutoff absent treatment. See <<angrist2009>>.

==== Interrupted time series

Interrupted time series examines level or trend changes after a clearly timed intervention.

It requires careful modeling of seasonality, autocorrelation, concurrent interventions, and preexisting trends. A single before-and-after comparison is not an interrupted-time-series design.

==== Matched controls and weighting

Matching and weighting attempt to create comparable treated and control populations based on measured pre-treatment covariates.

They cannot remove bias from unmeasured confounding and should be accompanied by overlap diagnostics, balance checks, sensitivity analysis, and explicit causal assumptions.

==== Synthetic controls

Synthetic-control methods construct a weighted comparison from untreated populations to approximate the treated unit’s pre-intervention trajectory.

They are most useful when a small number of aggregate units receive treatment and a credible donor pool exists.

=== Switchback and time-based designs

Switchback experiments alternate treatment across time windows and are useful when infrastructure or policies cannot be assigned permanently at individual-unit level.

They require:

* appropriate washout periods;
* control of time-of-day and day-of-week effects;
* stable treatment implementation;
* analysis at the randomization-window level;
* protection against carryover and anticipation.

Shared CI capacity, routing policy, or reviewer-assistance treatments may be suitable candidates.

=== Stepped-wedge rollout

In a stepped-wedge design, clusters transition from control to treatment over scheduled periods.

This can support operationally necessary phased rollout, but treatment effects can be confounded with calendar time unless the design and analysis account for period effects.

The chapter treats stepped-wedge rollout as a study design, not as automatic evidence merely because deployment was staggered.

=== Outcomes, mechanisms, and guardrails

A strong study separates:

* primary outcomes;
* guardrail outcomes;
* mechanism measures;
* adoption and exposure;
* data-quality measures;
* economic realization measures.

This prevents a mechanism such as increased tool usage or reduced authoring time from being mistaken for the final outcome.

Practical online-experiment guidance in <<kohavi2020>> supports the use of primary metrics, guardrails, data-quality checks, and trustworthy experimentation infrastructure.

=== Power and minimum detectable effect

Power analysis links sample size, variability, clustering, significance threshold, and the smallest effect the study is designed to detect.

A study that cannot detect a decision-relevant effect should not interpret a nonsignificant result as evidence of no effect.

Engineering studies often face:

* clustered assignment;
* highly skewed outcomes;
* rare defects or incidents;
* delayed outcome maturity;
* heterogeneous work classes;
* changing treatment versions.

Simulation-based power analysis is often more credible than a simple independent-sample formula.

=== Noninferiority and equivalence

Some engineering decisions ask whether a cheaper, faster, or more autonomous process is not unacceptably worse than the current process.

Noninferiority designs require a predeclared margin representing the largest acceptable degradation. The margin should be justified by operational, safety, or economic consequence rather than chosen after observing results.

Equivalence testing asks whether effects are sufficiently close within both positive and negative bounds.

These designs are more appropriate than failure to reject a superiority null when the decision is about acceptable similarity.

=== Multiple outcomes and repeated analysis

Testing many metrics, subgroups, or time windows increases the chance of false-positive findings.

The chapter therefore distinguishes:

* one or a small number of confirmatory primary outcomes;
* predeclared guardrails;
* exploratory secondary analysis;
* correction or hierarchical methods where appropriate;
* transparent reporting of all examined outcomes.

Repeatedly inspecting results and stopping when significance appears also changes error properties. Sequential designs require declared monitoring and stopping methods.

Practical concerns around trustworthy online experimentation are covered in <<kohavi2020>>.

=== Preregistration and pre-analysis plans

Preregistration separates confirmatory analysis from analyzes chosen after observing results. See <<nosek2018>>.

The Engineering Intelligence pre-analysis plan records:

* causal question and estimand;
* eligibility;
* assignment and exposure definitions;
* outcome and guardrail versions;
* exclusion rules;
* covariates;
* missing-data handling;
* statistical model;
* subgroup analysis;
* stopping rules;
* decision thresholds.

Preregistration does not prohibit exploration. It makes the distinction between confirmatory and exploratory work visible.

=== Missing outcomes and attrition

Outcome data may be missing because work is abandoned, deployment is delayed, telemetry fails, or the observation window closes before incidents mature.

The study should report missingness by treatment group and reason. Complete-case analysis can be biased when missingness depends on treatment or outcome.

Approaches may include:

* inverse-probability weighting;
* multiple imputation;
* bounds;
* sensitivity analysis;
* time-to-event methods;
* explicit immature-outcome states.

No method compensates for unexamined missingness assumptions.

=== Heterogeneous treatment effects

Average effects can conceal important differences across:

* language;
* repository size;
* change complexity;
* developer experience;
* team workflow;
* risk class;
* model or prompt version;
* type of AI contribution;
* baseline performance.

Subgroup analysis should be motivated, adequately powered, and protected against overinterpretation. Exploratory heterogeneity can generate hypotheses for replication rather than immediate policy.

=== External validity and transportability

A causal estimate is local to its study population, intervention version, comparator, period, and operating context.

Results from one organization, task, repository class, or AI model may not transport to another context.

The chapter therefore records boundary conditions and promotes replication across teams and time. This follows the internal- and external-validity tradition in <<shadish2002>>.

=== Reproducibility

A study should preserve enough information to reproduce the analytical result:

* source snapshots or durable references;
* assignment table;
* treatment and exposure versions;
* metric definitions;
* transformation lineage;
* analysis code;
* environment and dependency versions;
* exclusions and corrections;
* generated tables and figures.

The broader importance and difficulty of reproducibility is illustrated by <<open-science2015>>, while FAIR stewardship principles are described in <<wilkinson2016>>.

=== Decision rules and post-study follow-up

A statistical result does not make the organizational decision automatically.

The decision should integrate:

* estimated effect and uncertainty;
* guardrails;
* operational feasibility;
* cost and value;
* fairness and governance;
* implementation risk;
* reversibility;
* evidence maturity.

The chapter therefore links the study result to a separate decision record and value-realization plan.

Long-term follow-up is required because novelty effects, adoption, skill development, maintenance burden, and downstream quality can change after the experimental window.

=== Original Engineering Intelligence contributions

The following are original synthesis or domain-specific extensions proposed by this book:

* Engineering Change as an assignment, exposure, and outcome unit;
* explicit machine-treatment provenance including model, prompt, retrieval, and tool versions;
* integration of assignment, actual exposure, human disposition, and downstream consequence;
* experiment readiness reviews tied to data-quality and governance floors;
* economic and attention outcomes within the causal protocol;
* treatment-version drift as a first-class validity threat;
* value-realization follow-up after causal evaluation;
* decision records linked to immutable study evidence;
* study registries spanning randomized, quasi-experimental, and observational evidence;
* replication across Engineering Change classes as an organizational learning mechanism.

These extensions should be evaluated through repeated studies, independent review, and transparent reporting of null, negative, and contradictory results.
