= Measuring Engineering Systems

[abstract]
Engineering organizations produce abundant telemetry, but telemetry is not measurement. This chapter defines the measurement hierarchy from events to decisions, explains construct validity, reliability, sensitivity, and robustness, and introduces a taxonomy for flow, quality, economic, human, AI, and architectural metrics. It also covers derived measures, guardrails, metric versioning, statistical treatment, and common anti-patterns.

== Measurement as a decision discipline

The purpose of measurement is to improve a decision. A dashboard that cannot change a decision is descriptive at best and distracting at worst.

Engineering Intelligence therefore begins every metric definition with a decision question. Examples include:

* Should review capacity be added?
* Is CI the dominant constraint?
* Is an AI reviewer reducing total human effort?
* Are changes becoming riskier after a workflow change?
* Which repository boundaries create avoidable coordination cost?

Each question implies a stakeholder, an action that would be taken if the answer changed, and a threshold at which the action becomes justified. A metric that cannot be traced to such a triad is descriptive telemetry, not measurement. The remainder of this chapter treats every construct — from event to composite index — as machinery in service of one of these questions.

== The measurement hierarchy

Engineering data becomes progressively more useful as interpretation is added:

image::../figures/mermaid/measurement-hierarchy.mmd[Engineering measurement hierarchy]

image::figures/plots/measurement-onion.png[The measurement onion — interpretation is added inward from event to decision,pdfwidth=4.5in,align=center]

The layers are:

. *Events* — source observations such as pull-request creation, review submission, CI completion, deployment, or incident declaration.
. *Observations* — normalized facts derived from events, such as time to first review or number of failed checks.
. *Features* — explanatory variables such as dependency depth, ownership boundaries, or semantic complexity.
. *Metrics* — summarized quantities designed to answer a question.
. *Models* — relationships used to explain, predict, or estimate counterfactual outcomes.
. *Decisions* — actions taken because of the evidence.

Confusing these layers creates weak analytics. A commit count is an observation. It becomes a metric only when attached to a valid construct and decision.

== Properties of a good metric

=== Decision relevance

A metric should inform a specific operational, strategic, or experimental decision.

=== Construct validity

The metric should measure the concept it claims to represent. Pull-request count has low validity as a general productivity measure because it is strongly affected by repository and slicing conventions.

=== Reliability

Repeated measurement under equivalent conditions should produce similar values. Instrumentation gaps, inconsistent timestamps, and identity errors reduce reliability.

=== Sensitivity

A metric should respond when the underlying construct changes. A review-friction measure that does not move after a major queue reduction has weak sensitivity.

=== Robustness

A metric should resist gaming and remain interpretable across reasonable workflow differences.

=== Explainability

Users should be able to determine why a metric changed and drill down to its component evidence.

=== Stability

Noise should be quantified. Important measures should include intervals, sample size, and control limits where appropriate.

=== Collection cost

Instrumentation, storage, computation, governance, and human correction all have costs. High-cost measures should support high-value decisions.

== Metric taxonomy

[cols="1,3"]
|===
|Category |Examples

|Flow
|Lead time, active time, queue time, review latency, deployment latency, work in process, critical-path duration

|Quality
|Escaped defects, change-failure rate, rollback rate, severity-weighted incidents, security findings, rework

|Economic
|Human attention cost, AI cost, infrastructure cost, coordination cost, cost of delay, expected failure loss

|Human
|Context recovery, concurrent cognitive inventory, review burden, scarce-expertise utilization

|AI
|Finding precision, recall, duplication, validation burden, action rate, cognitive offload, cognitive reload

|Architecture
|Dependency depth, fan-in, fan-out, blast radius, repository spread, ownership boundaries, deployment coupling
|===

No category should be optimized independently. Faster flow with worse quality is not an improvement; lower review cost with greater cognitive reload may not create value.

== Direct and derived metrics

A direct metric is computed closely from observed events. Examples include build duration, time to first review, or number of deployment attempts.

A derived metric depends on a model. Examples include cognitive burden, expected cost of delay, blast-radius score, or Engineering Change complexity.

Derived metrics are often more decision-relevant but require stronger governance:

* published definitions;
* model version;
* training and calibration data;
* confidence intervals;
* known limitations;
* validation evidence;
* recomputation history.

Without this scaffolding a derived metric behaves like a black box that quietly changes meaning when its underlying model is retrained. Governance is not administrative overhead: it is what allows a Cognitive Offload Index or a blast-radius score to survive scrutiny in a change-management review or a post-incident audit. Direct metrics can be re-derived from raw events; derived metrics can only be trusted to the extent that their assumptions are stated and traceable.

== Leading and lagging measures

Leading indicators change before the outcome of interest. Review queue age, CI instability, and dependency growth may precede delivery or quality problems.

Lagging indicators confirm realized outcomes. Incidents, escaped defects, customer impact, and realized cost appear after release.

A strong scorecard pairs them. Leading indicators support intervention; lagging indicators validate whether the intervention mattered.

== Local and system-level measures

Local measures describe one stage, team, repository, or participant. System-level measures describe the complete Engineering Change or portfolio.

Local optimization can harm the system. For example, reducing CI cost by running fewer tests may increase rework and incidents. Reducing reviewer effort may shift burden to authors.

Engineering Intelligence preserves both views and makes the relationship explicit.

== Composite metrics

Composite metrics summarize several related signals. They should not conceal their components.

A Review Friction Index might combine normalized queue age, response cycles, unresolved comments, context-recovery events, and verification effort:

[stem]
++++
RFI(e) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k z_k(e)
++++

where stem:[z_k] are standardized components and stem:[w_k] are calibrated weights.

Weights should be learned or justified, not chosen merely to make the result look plausible. Users must be able to inspect component values.

== Guardrail metrics

Every optimization metric should have guardrails.

An AI reviewer experiment may target lower human review effort while guarding against increases in severe defects, rollback rate, security escapes, author misunderstanding, and long-term reviewer disengagement.

A change is considered beneficial only if the primary outcome improves and guardrails remain within predeclared tolerances.

== Statistical treatment

Engineering metrics are usually skewed, heavy-tailed, seasonal, and clustered by team and repository. Means alone are rarely sufficient.

Recommended summaries include:

* median and selected quantiles;
* confidence or credible intervals;
* sample size and missingness;
* distribution plots;
* control limits;
* stratification by change class;
* hierarchical estimates for sparse groups.

=== Control charts

Control charts help distinguish common-cause variation from exceptional changes. They are useful for operational monitoring when metric definitions and populations remain stable.

=== Time-series effects

Seasonality, release freezes, reorganizations, holidays, incidents, and tool migrations can create apparent trends. Time-series models should record external events and definition changes.

=== Bayesian updating

Bayesian models are useful when evidence arrives sequentially, sample sizes are small, or prior domain knowledge matters. They support direct statements such as the probability that an intervention improved a metric by at least a meaningful threshold.

== Metric versioning

A metric without a version is not reproducible evidence.

Version changes may include event-source changes, identity-resolution changes, new exclusion rules, updated reconstruction models, cost assumptions, or revised severity weights.

Every material metric should retain:

* metric name and semantic version;
* definition and formula;
* source schemas;
* population and exclusions;
* model version;
* computation time;
* lineage to raw evidence.

Semantic versioning conveys the nature of a change: a patch corrects computation, a minor version adds a compatible refinement, a major version breaks historical comparability. A dashboard that mixes versions without labelling them silently confuses evidence. Where historical continuity matters, retain the prior version alongside the new one and backfill only when the semantics genuinely align.

== Comparison and normalization

Comparisons should control for work mix. Relevant variables include Engineering Change complexity, potential blast radius, repository topology, team count, domain, novelty, and deployment type.

Raw comparisons between teams are usually invalid because teams receive different work and operate under different constraints.

Normalization can use stratification, regression adjustment, matching, hierarchical models, or experimental assignment. It should never erase meaningful heterogeneity.

== Measurement anti-patterns

=== Activity as productivity

Commit count, lines changed, comments, and pull-request volume are easy to count and easy to game. They should not become universal performance measures.

=== Individual ranking

System telemetry is confounded by assignment, role, complexity, and invisible work. Individual league tables damage trust and measurement validity.

=== One universal score

Engineering effectiveness is multidimensional. A single score hides trade-offs and invites optimization against the formula.

=== Silent definition changes

Historical comparisons become invalid when definitions change without versioning and backfill.

=== Correlation as causation

A team using a tool may outperform because it adopted the tool early, has stronger leadership, or receives different work. Experiments or causal methods are required.

=== Dashboard accumulation

Adding metrics without retiring unused ones creates noise. Every dashboard should have an owner, audience, decision, and review date.

== Designing a measurement catalogue

Each metric specification should include:

[cols="1,3"]
|===
|Field |Purpose

|Name and version
|Stable identifier for reproducibility

|Decision
|Question the metric informs

|Construct
|Concept being measured

|Formula
|Exact computation

|Unit and population
|Scope and denominator

|Sources
|Required events and entities

|Latency
|Near-real-time, daily, weekly, or retrospective

|Validation
|Evidence of validity and reliability

|Bias and gaming
|Known failure modes

|Guardrails
|Outcomes that must not worsen

|Owner
|Accountability for definition and maintenance
|===

== Example: measuring review latency

A weak definition is "time from pull-request open to first review."

A stronger definition specifies business-time handling, draft exclusions, bot reviews, reviewer assignment, author self-review, reopened pull requests, time zones, and whether the unit is pull request or Engineering Change.

The metric should be paired with active review effort, queue age, complexity, reviewer capacity, and quality outcomes. A decrease can mean better flow or superficial review.

== Example: measuring AI review quality

For labeled findings:

[stem]
++++
Precision = \frac{TP}{TP+FP}
++++

[stem]
++++
Recall = \frac{TP}{TP+FN}
++++

Ranking quality can be measured with NDCG when high-severity findings should appear first. Operational analysis should also include duplication, action type, validation effort, latency, and downstream outcome.

High precision with very low recall may miss important defects. High recall with poor ranking may overwhelm authors. No single number is sufficient.

== Key takeaways

* Telemetry becomes measurement only when connected to a construct and decision.
* Good metrics are relevant, valid, reliable, sensitive, robust, explainable, stable, and worth their collection cost.
* Flow, quality, economics, human factors, AI, and architecture must be evaluated together.
* Derived metrics require model governance, versioning, uncertainty, and validation.
* Guardrails prevent local optimization from damaging the wider system.
* Comparisons must control for work mix and organizational context.
* Metric catalogues should be governed products, not undocumented queries.

== Open questions

* Which constructs can be measured reliably without invasive telemetry?
* How should organizations retire metrics that have become targets and lost validity?
* What normalization methods best preserve meaningful differences while enabling comparison?
* How can metric catalogues remain understandable as models and schemas evolve?
* Which leading indicators provide actionable warning without excessive false alarms?

== Implementation checklist

. Inventory current dashboards and identify the decision supported by each metric.
. Separate events, observations, features, metrics, models, and decisions in the data architecture.
. Create a versioned metric catalogue with lineage and owners.
. Add uncertainty, sample size, and missingness to important measures.
. Define guardrails for every optimization objective.
. Prohibit unsupported individual productivity rankings.
. Stratify or adjust comparisons for Engineering Change context.
. Validate derived measures against simpler baselines and downstream outcomes.
. Record external events and definition changes in time-series analysis.
. Retire metrics that no longer support a decision.
