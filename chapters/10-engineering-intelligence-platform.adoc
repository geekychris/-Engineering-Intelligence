= The Engineering Intelligence Platform

[abstract]
The preceding chapters define the concepts, metrics, and evidence required to understand engineering systems. This chapter turns those ideas into a concrete platform architecture. It describes the major services, storage systems, APIs, computation paths, governance controls, decision products, and operating model needed to build an Engineering Intelligence platform incrementally in a large enterprise.

== Platform objectives

An Engineering Intelligence platform should answer four classes of questions:

* *What happened?* — reconstruct Engineering Changes, queues, reviews, deployments, and outcomes.
* *Why did it happen?* — identify constraints, complexity, dependencies, and external factors.
* *What is likely to happen next?* — predict delay, risk, cost, and quality outcomes.
* *What should we change?* — support experiments, routing, prioritization, and investment decisions.

The platform is not merely a reporting warehouse. It is an evidence and decision system.

== Core architectural principles

. Preserve immutable raw evidence.
. Separate canonical entities from source-specific identifiers.
. Treat the Engineering Change as the primary unit of analysis.
. Version events, features, metrics, models, and policies.
. Support both near-real-time operations and retrospective recomputation.
. Keep causal experiment assignment independent from metric computation.
. Expose uncertainty, provenance, and data quality with every derived result.
. Minimize behavioral surveillance and prohibit unsupported individual ranking.

== Service boundaries

A practical service decomposition includes:

=== Source connector service

Connects to Git hosting, issue tracking, CI/CD, AI tools, observability, service catalogues, and organizational systems. It translates source records into canonical envelopes while retaining original payloads.

=== Identity service

Resolves people, AI actors, repositories, services, teams, environments, and tools into stable temporal identities.

=== Engineering Change service

Creates, links, merges, splits, and closes Engineering Changes. It exposes lifecycle state, graph membership, confidence, and provenance.

=== Topology service

Maintains repository, service, data, deployment, ownership, and business dependency graphs.

=== Feature service

Publishes versioned point-in-time features for changes, repositories, teams, services, and experiments.

=== Metric service

Executes governed metric definitions and exposes current and historical values with lineage and uncertainty.

=== Experiment service

Manages eligibility, assignment, exposure, guardrails, external factors, and experiment status.

=== Model service

Hosts predictive, causal, ranking, and reconstruction models with versioned inputs and monitoring.

=== Decision product service

Provides APIs, dashboards, alerts, and reports tailored to operational, engineering, product, and executive users.

== Canonical APIs

The Engineering Change API should expose operations such as:

[source,http]
----
GET /engineering-changes/{id}
GET /engineering-changes/{id}/graph
GET /engineering-changes/{id}/metrics
POST /engineering-changes/{id}/members
POST /engineering-changes/{id}:merge
POST /engineering-changes/{id}:split
----

A metric API should require explicit version and time context:

[source,http]
----
GET /metrics/review-friction/v2.1
  ?entityType=engineering_change
  &entityId=EC-01J8...
  &asOf=2026-07-15T22:00:00Z
----

A result should return value, interval, sample basis, quality state, and lineage.

== Storage architecture

No single database is optimal for all workloads.

[cols="1,3"]
|===
|Store |Purpose

|Object storage or lakehouse
|Immutable raw events, normalized history, batch features, reproducible snapshots

|Event broker
|Low-latency transport, ordering, replay, stream processing

|Relational store
|Experiment registry, metric catalogue, governance metadata, operational state

|Graph store
|Engineering Change, repository, service, ownership, and dependency relationships

|Search index
|Interactive discovery across changes, artifacts, findings, and explanations

|Feature store
|Point-in-time online and offline model inputs

|Analytical warehouse
|Large-scale aggregation, ad hoc analysis, and reporting
|===

The canonical source of truth should be explicit for every entity and fact.

== Stream processing

Streaming computation maintains:

* current lifecycle state;
* queue depth and age;
* experiment exposure;
* AI interaction state;
* build and deployment progression;
* freshness and quality alerts;
* rapidly changing guardrails.

State transitions should be deterministic and replayable. Stream processors should emit changelogs rather than silently mutate opaque state.

== Batch and graph computation

Batch computation handles:

* historical reconstruction;
* semantic code analysis;
* dependency graph generation;
* ownership and collaboration networks;
* complexity and blast-radius features;
* experiment datasets;
* cost and value recomputation;
* model training and calibration.

Incremental graph processing is preferable where possible, but complete recomputation must remain available for recovery and definition changes.

== Metric specification language

A declarative metric definition reduces divergence between dashboards and experiments.

[source,yaml]
----
id: review_friction
version: 2.1.0
entity: engineering_change
construct: review_process_friction
population: accepted_changes
features:
  - review_queue_minutes
  - review_response_cycles
  - context_recovery_events
  - validation_minutes
aggregation: weighted_sum
uncertainty: bootstrap_clustered_by_repository
guardrails:
  - severe_escape_rate
  - rollback_rate
owner: engineering-intelligence
----

The execution engine can compile the definition into SQL, batch, or streaming plans.

== Policy and routing engine

Metrics and models become operational when they influence routing and policy.

Examples include:

* send high-blast-radius changes to specialist review;
* suppress low-confidence duplicate AI comments;
* prioritize aged changes with high cost of delay;
* require stronger rollout controls for irreversible migrations;
* allocate scarce reviewers to changes with highest expected risk reduction.

Every policy should be versioned, explainable, and linked to an experiment or governance decision.

== Decision products

Different audiences require different views.

=== Engineer view

Shows current change state, unresolved dependencies, review queues, AI findings, expected bottlenecks, and production outcome.

=== Team view

Shows flow distributions, work in process, review constraints, quality, cognitive burden, and experiment status.

=== Platform view

Shows infrastructure latency, tool cost, data quality, model drift, and source freshness.

=== Executive view

Shows portfolio-level cost, value, risk, capacity constraints, and evidence from interventions.

A good decision product permits drill-down from aggregate conclusion to raw evidence without exposing unnecessary individual detail.

== Data contracts and ownership

Each domain should own the quality of the events it emits. The platform team owns canonical contracts, identity, lineage, and shared computation.

A data contract includes schema, semantics, owner, SLO, classification, retention, compatibility, and quality checks.

Producers should not be able to change semantics silently.

== Platform SLOs

Suggested service-level objectives include:

* event ingestion availability;
* source-to-platform lag;
* maximum duplicate and loss rate;
* Engineering Change reconstruction latency;
* metric freshness;
* experiment assignment availability;
* replay completion time;
* lineage completeness;
* data-quality alert time.

Freshness should be visible in every product. Stale metrics should not appear current.

== Security and privacy architecture

The platform may contain source code, prompts, behavioral metadata, organizational relationships, and financial assumptions.

Required controls include:

* encryption and key management;
* least-privilege service identities;
* row and column access control;
* pseudonymous identity by default;
* classified event handling;
* retention and deletion workflows;
* export controls;
* audit trails;
* separation between system analysis and performance management.

== Reference implementation roadmap

=== Phase 1: evidence foundation

Ingest source-control, CI, and deployment events. Establish canonical envelopes, raw storage, replay, and identity resolution.

=== Phase 2: Engineering Change graph

Introduce explicit IDs where practical and probabilistic reconstruction elsewhere. Expose graph and lifecycle APIs.

=== Phase 3: governed metrics

Create the metric catalogue, feature pipelines, lineage, and quality indicators. Deliver flow and quality views.

=== Phase 4: experimentation

Add deterministic assignment, exposure logging, external-factor registry, guardrails, and reproducible analysis datasets.

=== Phase 5: AI and cognitive instrumentation

Capture AI contributions, human actions, validation burden, trust, and quality-adjusted offload.

=== Phase 6: predictive and prescriptive intelligence

Add delay, risk, complexity, and routing models. Introduce policy decisions only after experimental validation.

== Suggested technology-neutral components

The design can be implemented with different stacks, but common component classes include:

* event broker;
* object store or lakehouse;
* stream processor;
* SQL transformation engine;
* graph database or graph-processing layer;
* relational metadata store;
* feature store;
* search engine;
* model-serving platform;
* dashboard and notebook environment;
* policy engine.

Avoid coupling the conceptual architecture to one vendor.

== Build-versus-buy decisions

Buy commodity capabilities when they do not define strategic differentiation: standard connectors, visualization, cataloguing, or infrastructure monitoring.

Retain control of canonical event history, identity, Engineering Change reconstruction, metric definitions, experiment assignments, proprietary AI telemetry, and policy logic.

Vendor products should integrate through contracts rather than become the only location where evidence exists.

== Operating model

A successful platform requires more than software.

Recommended roles include:

* platform engineering;
* data engineering;
* applied statistics and causal inference;
* ML and information retrieval;
* developer experience;
* security and privacy;
* domain representatives;
* metric and experiment owners.

A governance council should approve high-impact metrics, experiments, and individual-level uses.

== Common failure modes

=== Building a universal data lake without decisions

Data accumulates while operational value remains unclear.

=== Coupling metrics to dashboards

Definitions diverge across products and cannot be reproduced.

=== Hiding provenance

Users cannot challenge or correct derived conclusions.

=== Centralizing all ownership

The platform team cannot understand every source and domain without producer contracts.

=== Automating policy too early

Predictive models are deployed before validation and create self-reinforcing bias.

=== Ignoring operating model

Technology exists, but no one owns definitions, experiments, or action.

== Worked example: sequencing platform delivery for first-year value

A newly formed engineering-intelligence team has a mandate to demonstrate value within twelve months. They have four candidate initial capabilities: an ingestion layer, Engineering Change reconstruction, a metric governance layer, and an experiment platform.

Ranking by decision-value-per-effort:

- Ingestion + descriptive review-latency dashboard: 3 engineer-quarters, unblocks the CTO's monthly reliability review, low uncertainty.
- Engineering Change reconstruction: 6 engineer-quarters, unblocks blast-radius scoring and coordination-cost measurement, medium uncertainty because linkage confidence depends on source-system semantics.
- Metric governance layer: 4 engineer-quarters, defers value but is a prerequisite for anything shown on an executive scorecard.
- Experiment platform: 8 engineer-quarters, high value but useless until reconstruction is in place.

The team has 18 engineer-quarters for the year. A naive plan would try to build all four in parallel; the resulting slippage delays every deliverable.

A sequenced plan takes ingestion + descriptive dashboard through governance in the first quarter (7 engineer-quarters), builds reconstruction against that governed ingest in Q2-Q3 (6 engineer-quarters, with governance work continuing in the background at 1 engineer-quarter), and starts the experiment platform in Q4 (4 engineer-quarters against the reconstructed graph). By month twelve, the team has produced two governed executive-scorecard metrics, one causal experiment on review routing, and a blast-radius measure the incident review committee has adopted — a small, credible portfolio that justifies year-two funding.

The alternative sequencing — experiment platform first — would have delivered no consumable output for eight months and made continued investment politically difficult.

== Key takeaways

* The Engineering Intelligence platform is an evidence, experimentation, and decision system—not merely a dashboard stack.
* Clear service boundaries separate collection, identity, graph, features, metrics, experiments, models, and decision products.
* Immutable evidence, temporal identities, point-in-time features, and versioned definitions are foundational.
* Operational policies should be explainable, versioned, and experimentally validated.
* Different audiences require different views, but all should share one governed semantic layer.
* Incremental adoption should deliver value before advanced prediction or automation.

== Open questions

* Which platform capabilities should be centralized versus federated to teams?
* How can metric specification languages remain expressive without becoming ungovernable?
* What is the best storage pattern for temporal engineering graphs at enterprise scale?
* How should policy engines incorporate uncertainty and human override?
* Which parts of the platform are likely to become standardized industry infrastructure?

== Implementation checklist

. Define platform objectives and decision products before selecting technology.
. Establish canonical events, immutable evidence, and temporal identity.
. Implement the Engineering Change and topology services.
. Create versioned feature, metric, and experiment services.
. Expose lineage, confidence, and data quality through APIs.
. Separate assignment, exposure, and outcome computation.
. Add policy routing only after validated experiments.
. Publish platform SLOs and freshness indicators.
. Establish privacy, security, retention, and governance controls.
. Build incrementally from evidence capture to prescriptive intelligence.
