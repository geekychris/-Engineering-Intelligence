== Research foundations and source notes

The experiment playbooks in this chapter operationalize the causal-inference foundations introduced earlier. They draw on randomized experiments, quasi-experimental design, online experimentation, preregistration, information retrieval, and reproducibility.

=== Randomized evaluation

The potential-outcomes tradition provides the core framework for estimating causal effects. See <<rubin1974>>, <<holland1986>>, <<imbens2015>>, and <<hernan2020>>.

The playbooks therefore require:

* a declared estimand;
* an explicit assignment unit;
* a credible comparator;
* auditable randomization;
* exposure and compliance tracking;
* analysis consistent with the assignment design.

Randomization improves comparability in expectation, but it does not remove problems caused by missing outcomes, spillover, treatment drift, or poor measurement.

=== Practical online experimentation

The operating discipline for trustworthy experiments is informed by <<kohavi2020>>.

This supports playbook requirements for:

* sample-ratio and assignment checks;
* primary outcomes and guardrails;
* data-quality monitoring;
* predeclared stopping rules;
* treatment-version control;
* reproducible analysis;
* decision follow-up.

Engineering experiments should not rely on a dashboard trend and a post hoc explanation.

=== Quasi-experimental alternatives

When randomization is infeasible, the playbooks use methods such as:

* difference-in-differences;
* regression discontinuity;
* interrupted time series;
* matched comparison groups;
* synthetic controls;
* instrumental variables.

The methodological foundations are described in <<shadish2002>>, <<angrist2009>>, <<imbens2015>>, and <<hernan2020>>.

Each method relies on assumptions that must be made explicit and tested where possible. A before-and-after comparison alone is not a causal design.

=== Preregistration

Preregistration helps distinguish confirmatory analysis from exploration. See <<nosek2018>>.

The playbooks therefore record before analysis:

* hypotheses;
* estimands;
* outcomes and guardrails;
* eligibility;
* exclusions;
* covariates;
* missing-data handling;
* subgroup analysis;
* stopping and decision rules.

Exploratory work remains valuable, but it should be labeled as such.

=== Power and decision relevance

Power analysis should be based on the smallest effect that would change the decision.

Engineering studies often require simulation because outcomes are clustered, skewed, rare, delayed, or heterogeneous.

A study that is underpowered for a decision-relevant effect should not interpret a nonsignificant result as proof of no effect.

=== Cluster and switchback designs

Teams, repositories, reviewers, and time windows may be more appropriate assignment units than individuals.

Clustered designs must account for within-cluster correlation. Switchback designs must account for:

* time effects;
* carryover;
* washout;
* shared capacity;
* assignment-window integrity.

These design concerns follow the general experimental principles in <<shadish2002>>, <<imbens2015>>, and <<kohavi2020>>.

=== Noninferiority and equivalence

Some playbooks ask whether a cheaper or faster process is not unacceptably worse than the current process.

That question requires a predeclared noninferiority margin rather than a failed superiority test.

Equivalence designs are appropriate when the decision depends on demonstrating that differences lie within acceptable positive and negative bounds.

=== AI review and finding evaluation

AI review experiments use precision and recall as defined in standard information retrieval. See <<manning2008>>.

Rank-sensitive evaluation can use NDCG when high-value findings should appear early. See <<jarvelin2002>>.

The playbooks also require:

* duplicate handling;
* severity and partial relevance;
* adjudication procedures;
* reviewer disagreement analysis;
* false-positive handling effort;
* downstream defect and rework evidence.

Finding quality is not equivalent to product value, so these metrics remain mechanisms within a broader outcome bundle.

=== AI coding-assistant playbooks

The coding-assistant playbooks are informed by empirical studies in <<peng2023>>, <<vaithilingam2022>>, and <<barke2023>>.

These studies support measuring more than completion speed. Relevant outcomes include:

* validation effort;
* correctness;
* comprehension;
* rework;
* downstream quality;
* net attention effect;
* long-term maintainability.

Voluntary adoption should be treated as a source of selection bias.

=== Repository-level agent evaluation

Agent playbooks use repository-level benchmark evidence such as <<jimenez2024>> as one capability layer.

Production readiness requires additional evidence for:

* permission boundaries;
* tool-call behavior;
* human oversight;
* rollback;
* operational consequence;
* cost;
* long-term maintainability.

Benchmark success is not sufficient evidence for autonomous production authority.

=== Architecture and workflow interventions

Architecture, review-policy, CI, and platform interventions are often evaluated through phased or quasi-experimental designs.

Relevant methods include:

* staggered rollout;
* matched repositories;
* interrupted time series;
* difference-in-differences;
* synthetic controls.

The playbooks require mechanism measures and guardrails because an intervention can improve one stage while moving delay or risk elsewhere.

=== Reproducibility

The reproducibility concerns illustrated by <<open-science2015>> and the FAIR principles in <<wilkinson2016>> support retaining:

* study protocol;
* assignment table;
* treatment configuration;
* source snapshots or durable references;
* metric versions;
* analysis code;
* generated outputs;
* deviations and corrections.

A study result should be reproducible even after dashboards, models, or organizational structures change.

=== Governance and ethics

Experiments involving detailed practitioner data or AI systems require purpose limitation, privacy controls, and professional accountability.

Relevant foundations include <<nist2020-privacy>>, <<nist2023-airmf>>, <<nist2024-genai>>, and <<acm2018-ethics>>.

The playbooks therefore require risk review, minimum necessary data, transparent treatment, and explicit restrictions on unsupported individual employment use.

=== Original Engineering Intelligence contributions

The following are original synthesis or domain-specific extensions proposed by this book:

* reusable experiment playbooks organized around Engineering Change outcomes;
* explicit linkage among assignment, exposure, human disposition, and downstream consequence;
* machine-treatment provenance across model, prompt, retrieval, tools, and configuration;
* experiment-readiness gates tied to data-quality and governance floors;
* attention and economic outcomes as first-class experimental measures;
* treatment-version drift controls;
* value-realization follow-up after study completion;
* linked study, evidence, metric, and decision records;
* replication across Engineering Change classes before enterprise-wide policy.

These playbooks should evolve as evidence accumulates and should preserve null, negative, and contradictory findings rather than only successful interventions.
