== Research foundations and source notes

The governance, ethics, and adoption framework in this chapter draws on measurement validity, indicator corruption, privacy engineering, professional ethics, cybersecurity governance, AI risk management, organizational learning, and socio-technical change. It extends those foundations into a practical operating model for legitimate Engineering Intelligence.

=== Measurement changes behavior

Campbell’s Law describes how indicators used for consequential decision-making become vulnerable to corruption and can distort the process they are intended to monitor. See <<campbell1979>>.

Goodhart’s Law expresses a related principle: when a measure becomes a target, it can cease to function as a useful measure. See <<goodhart1984>>.

These foundations support the chapter’s treatment of behavioral adaptation as part of the measured system rather than as misconduct by default.

Examples include:

* splitting work to improve visible throughput;
* suppressing incident declaration;
* optimizing AI acceptance rather than contribution;
* avoiding difficult work;
* moving coordination outside instrumented channels;
* reducing review depth to improve latency.

The governance response is to design incentive-aware metric bundles, guardrails, periodic validity review, and retirement mechanisms.

=== Use-specific validity

Messick’s validity framework emphasizes that validity concerns the evidence supporting a score’s intended interpretation and use, including consequences. See <<messick1995>>.

The chapter therefore treats governance as part of measurement validity rather than as an external compliance layer.

A metric may be appropriate for diagnosing a queue and invalid for ranking individuals. The same computation can have different validity depending on population, decision, and consequence.

=== Purpose limitation and data minimization

The NIST Privacy Framework provides a risk-based foundation for identifying, governing, controlling, communicating, and protecting privacy risk. See <<nist2020-privacy>>.

The chapter applies these principles through:

* declared purposes;
* collection minimization;
* proportional granularity;
* retention limits;
* pseudonymization;
* restricted re-identification;
* correction and appeal;
* purpose-bound access.

The preferred design is to collect the least detailed data capable of supporting the declared decision.

=== Privacy by architecture

Policy alone is insufficient when broad raw access makes misuse easy.

The chapter therefore requires structural controls such as:

* separate identity vaults;
* pseudonymous analytical data;
* aggregation by default;
* minimum group-size thresholds;
* row- and column-level authorization;
* audited identity lookup;
* deletion propagation;
* short retention for detailed behavioral telemetry.

This is an Engineering Intelligence-specific implementation of privacy engineering principles informed by <<nist2020-privacy>>.

=== Professional ethics

The ACM Code of Ethics emphasizes contributing to society, avoiding harm, honesty, fairness, privacy, competence, and accountability. See <<acm2018-ethics>>.

These principles support the chapter’s restrictions on:

* covert surveillance;
* unsupported individual productivity scoring;
* automated employment decisions from engineering telemetry;
* punitive use of experimental metrics;
* opaque or unchallengeable classifications;
* use beyond the original declared purpose.

Professional responsibility applies to the designers, operators, analysts, managers, and decision-makers using the system.

=== Cybersecurity governance

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework provides a foundation for governing, identifying, protecting, detecting, responding to, and recovering from cybersecurity risk. See <<nist2024-csf>>.

Engineering Intelligence systems can reveal sensitive source metadata, organizational structure, vulnerabilities, and strategic work.

The chapter therefore treats security controls as governance controls, including:

* least privilege;
* secrets management;
* encryption;
* audit logging;
* environment isolation;
* incident response;
* vendor-risk review;
* tested recovery.

=== AI and model governance

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework and its generative-AI profile provide foundations for governing, mapping, measuring, and managing AI risk. See <<nist2023-airmf>> and <<nist2024-genai>>.

Management-system and risk-process standards are represented by <<iso42,001>> and <<iso23,894>>.

The chapter operationalizes these principles through model records containing:

* accountable owner;
* intended and prohibited uses;
* training and evaluation evidence;
* feature and sensitive-attribute review;
* subgroup performance;
* calibration and uncertainty;
* drift monitoring;
* human override;
* rollback and retirement criteria;
* version history.

These standards guide governance but do not prove that a model is suitable for a particular decision.

=== Human accountability

AI systems, classifiers, risk scores, and generated explanations may inform decisions, but responsibility remains with accountable human roles.

The chapter distinguishes between:

* evidence generation;
* automated recommendation;
* delegated action;
* policy approval;
* consequential decision.

The person or body making the decision should be able to inspect evidence, understand uncertainty, override the system, and document rationale.

=== Contestability and correction

A legitimate measurement system must permit challenge and correction.

The chapter’s metric-rights model includes the ability to:

* know what is measured;
* understand definitions;
* inspect relevant data where appropriate;
* correct factual errors;
* challenge inferred links or classifications;
* understand retention and access;
* know whether data affects consequential decisions.

Contestability improves both fairness and data quality because affected practitioners often possess context unavailable to centralized instrumentation.

=== Fairness and disparate impact

Fairness cannot be reduced to equal raw metric values across populations performing different work.

Potential sources of disparate impact include:

* legacy-system ownership;
* role and tenure;
* accessibility-related workflows;
* distributed time zones;
* specialist responsibilities;
* repository and domain complexity;
* unequal access to tools or support.

The chapter therefore evaluates error rates, burden distribution, policy effects, and decision consequence by relevant subgroup.

The goal is not identical averages; it is avoidance of unjustified harm from invalid comparison or routing.

=== Individual-level measurement

Some actor identity is needed for provenance, permissions, handoffs, and correction.

That need does not justify named productivity scorecards.

The chapter’s default controls—pseudonymization, aggregation, restricted lookup, minimum group sizes, and explicit review of new uses—follow the use-specific validity and privacy principles in <<messick1995>> and <<nist2020-privacy>>.

Individual data may support private self-reflection or factual correction without becoming managerial ranking data.

=== Experiment ethics

Experiments involving practitioners can alter workload, security, review quality, or access to tools.

The causal-design foundations in <<shadish2002>>, <<imbens2015>>, <<hernan2020>>, and <<kohavi2020>> support the chapter’s requirements for:

* risk and reversibility review;
* guardrails;
* treatment transparency appropriate to the design;
* burden analysis;
* post-study retention decisions;
* separation from punitive employment action.

Low-risk interface experiments are ethically different from experiments that alter security review or collect detailed behavioral traces.

=== Agent governance

Agentic systems introduce questions of permission, delegation, evidence, shutdown, and side-effect accountability.

The chapter therefore requires explicit autonomy tiers and distinguishes authority to:

* inspect;
* recommend;
* modify;
* execute tests;
* merge;
* deploy;
* alter infrastructure or data;
* delegate to other agents.

This is an Engineering Intelligence-specific extension of provenance and AI risk-governance principles in <<moreau2013>>, <<nist2023-airmf>>, and <<nist2024-genai>>.

=== Trust as a system dependency

Trust affects whether practitioners correct data, participate in experiments, disclose problems, and accept conclusions.

The chapter’s trust model is conceptual rather than a mechanically optimized score.

Trust is strengthened by:

* accuracy;
* transparency;
* visible local benefit;
* correction mechanisms;
* fair process;
* restraint;
* reliable enforcement of prohibited uses.

Trust is weakened by opaque, punitive, surprising, or obviously incomplete measurement.

=== Organizational learning

Argyris and Schön distinguish single-loop learning from double-loop learning, where organizations reconsider the assumptions and governing values behind their actions. See <<argyris1978>>.

Senge’s learning-organization work emphasizes systems thinking, shared learning, and collective capability. See <<senge1990>>.

The chapter therefore treats disputes, null results, corrections, and metric retirement as learning mechanisms rather than governance failures.

=== Practitioner participation

Engineering Intelligence is a socio-technical system. Practitioner participation improves construct definition, acceptable-use policy, error correction, and adoption.

The broader socio-technical context is informed by <<conway1968>>, <<cataldo2006>>, and <<herbsleb2007>>.

The chapter recommends including affected engineers in metric councils, experiment review, model governance, and policy design.

This reduces the risk that centrally designed measures misunderstand local workflow or redistribute burden invisibly.

=== Adoption sequencing

The chapter recommends beginning with visible system problems that already matter to practitioners, such as unreliable CI, review queues, ownership ambiguity, or incident linkage.

This sequencing is consistent with developer-experience research emphasizing feedback loops, cognitive load, and flow. See <<forsgren2021-space>>, <<noda2023-devex>>, and <<fagerholm2012>>.

Adoption should expand only after the system demonstrates usefulness, accuracy, transparency, and restraint.

=== Governance as versioned change

Metric, model, and policy changes should follow software-like lifecycle discipline:

* proposal;
* review;
* validation;
* versioned release;
* migration;
* communication;
* monitoring;
* rollback;
* retirement.

This is an original Engineering Intelligence operating principle informed by reliable-change practices in <<humble2010>>, <<beyer2016>>, and <<beyer2018>>.

Silent dashboard or policy changes undermine auditability and trust.

=== Auditability

High-impact decisions should be reconstructable from:

* evidence and metric versions;
* model and policy versions;
* experiment records;
* access history;
* approvals;
* external factors;
* final rationale;
* later correction or appeal.

The provenance foundation is provided by <<moreau2013>>.

Auditability protects affected practitioners and the organization by making the decision path inspectable.

=== Original Engineering Intelligence contributions

The following are original synthesis or domain-specific extensions proposed by this book:

* metric rights for engineering practitioners;
* explicit acceptable, heightened-governance, inappropriate, and prohibited use classes;
* a required gaming-surface analysis for consequential metrics;
* purpose-bound access combining role, population, classification, and declared use;
* correction, dispute, and temporary-suspension procedures;
* governance maturity tied to evidence quality and practitioner trust;
* versioned metric and policy releases with rollback;
* autonomy tiers and delegation records for engineering agents;
* decision audit trails spanning evidence, model, experiment, policy, and rationale;
* trust treated as a functional dependency of valid measurement;
* adoption beginning with local practitioner value rather than executive ranking.

These constructs should be evaluated through audit outcomes, dispute resolution, data corrections, practitioner participation, trust evidence, observed incentive effects, and the consequences of decisions made with the system.
