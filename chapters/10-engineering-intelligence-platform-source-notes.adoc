== Research foundations and source notes

The platform architecture in this chapter draws on data-intensive systems, event-time stream processing, provenance, site reliability engineering, privacy engineering, cybersecurity governance, and AI risk management. It extends those foundations into a decision-oriented evidence platform for software engineering.

=== Durable logs and derived state

The architectural distinction between durable evidence and derived views is grounded in modern data-intensive system design. See <<kleppmann2017>>.

The chapter therefore separates:

* raw source evidence;
* canonical events;
* identity and ownership resolution;
* Engineering Change reconstruction;
* analytical projections;
* metric and model outputs;
* generated explanations;
* organizational decisions.

This separation supports replay, correction, reproducibility, and semantic evolution.

=== Event time and stream processing

Event-time semantics, late data, watermarks, and replayable stream processing are grounded in <<akidau2018>>.

These principles support the platform’s treatment of:

* source event time;
* ingestion and processing time;
* delayed deployment and incident evidence;
* outcome maturity;
* retractions and recomputation;
* shadow processing during semantic changes.

A current dashboard value is not necessarily a final analytical result when important downstream evidence has not matured.

=== Provenance

The W3C PROV model provides a general foundation for representing entities, activities, agents, derivation, generation, attribution, and association. See <<moreau2013>>.

The platform applies provenance across the complete evidence path, including source records, reconstruction, metrics, models, experiments, explanations, and decisions.

The central requirement is that observed evidence, inferred relationships, predictions, causal estimates, generated narratives, and human decisions remain distinguishable.

=== Polyglot analytical architecture

Different workloads require different storage and serving patterns.

The chapter’s use of immutable evidence stores, operational state, analytical warehouses, graph projections, search indexes, feature stores, and governed registries follows the tradeoff-oriented approach in <<kleppmann2017>>.

Polyglot storage should not produce polyglot truth. Authoritative semantics, identifiers, lineage, and versions must remain shared.

=== Service reliability

The platform is a production service whose failures can affect consequential organizational decisions.

SRE principles concerning service-level objectives, error budgets, operational toil, monitoring, incident response, and reliable change are grounded in <<beyer2016>> and <<beyer2018>>.

Relevant objectives include:

* connector freshness and completeness;
* schema-conformance rate;
* identity-resolution quality;
* linkage quality;
* metric publication latency;
* replay and correction latency;
* serving availability;
* audit-log completeness.

The chapter extends SRE thinking from infrastructure availability to evidence integrity.

=== Privacy engineering

The NIST Privacy Framework provides the basis for identifying, governing, controlling, communicating, and protecting privacy risk. See <<nist2020-privacy>>.

The platform applies these principles through:

* purpose limitation;
* data minimization;
* pseudonymization;
* re-identification isolation;
* aggregation thresholds;
* retention and deletion controls;
* practitioner correction and appeal;
* access auditing.

The platform should collect the least detailed evidence necessary for the declared decision.

=== Cybersecurity governance

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework provides a general foundation for governing, identifying, protecting, detecting, responding to, and recovering from cybersecurity risk. See <<nist2024-csf>>.

Relevant controls include:

* least-privilege connectors;
* secrets management;
* encryption;
* workload and tenant isolation;
* supply-chain review;
* immutable audit evidence;
* incident response;
* tested backup and recovery;
* vendor-risk management.

Engineering Intelligence data can reveal organizational structure, vulnerabilities, code activity, and sensitive initiatives, making it a high-value security target.

=== AI governance

AI-enabled explanations, classifications, recommendations, and agents introduce additional risks.

The chapter’s governance approach is informed by <<nist2023-airmf>>, <<nist2024-genai>>, <<iso42001>>, and <<iso23894>>.

These foundations support:

* model and prompt registries;
* treatment and configuration versioning;
* evaluation and monitoring;
* human accountability;
* bounded permissions;
* rollback;
* vendor and data-use controls;
* generated-content provenance.

The standards guide risk management; they do not establish that any individual model is effective or safe for a particular engineering decision.

=== Registries as institutional memory

The platform includes registries for metrics, models, experiments, evidence, decisions, and governed uses.

This design supports reproducibility, review, and organizational learning by preserving:

* definitions;
* owners;
* versions;
* assumptions;
* evidence;
* limitations;
* outcomes;
* review dates;
* correction history.

The broader organizational-learning foundations are discussed in <<argyris1978>> and <<senge1990>>.

=== Decision products versus dashboards

The platform is designed to serve bounded decision products rather than unrestricted metric browsing.

A decision product should include:

* the decision question;
* eligible population;
* evidence and quality state;
* metrics and uncertainty;
* causal status;
* economic interpretation;
* guardrails;
* assumptions and limitations;
* recommended review date.

This use-specific approach follows the validity principles in <<messick1995>> and reduces the indicator misuse described in <<campbell1979>> and <<goodhart1984>>.

=== Purpose-bound serving

Role-based access is combined with declared purpose, population, data classification, and use restrictions.

This is an original Engineering Intelligence extension aligned with the privacy-risk principles in <<nist2020-privacy>>.

Examples include:

* pseudonymous analysis for measurement scientists;
* aggregated team views for managers;
* raw connector diagnostics for platform operators;
* restricted re-identification for correction or authorized investigation;
* prohibition of unsupported individual employment use.

=== Reproducibility and correction

A decision-grade platform must reproduce the evidence path behind a result.

This requires:

* source and schema versions;
* transformation lineage;
* identity and reconstruction versions;
* metric and model versions;
* experiment assignment and exposure;
* correction history;
* generated artifacts and publication manifests.

The chapter’s correction model requires replay and downstream impact analysis when evidence or semantics change.

This follows the provenance and replay foundations in <<moreau2013>>, <<kleppmann2017>>, and <<akidau2018>>.

=== Original Engineering Intelligence contributions

The following are original synthesis or domain-specific extensions proposed by this book:

* a platform centered on Engineering Change reconstruction rather than source-specific dashboards;
* explicit separation of evidence, reconstruction, inference, prediction, causal estimate, explanation, and decision;
* connected metric, model, experiment, evidence, decision, and governed-use registries;
* purpose-bound serving products;
* semantic and evidence-quality SLOs;
* correction-triggered decision-impact review;
* outcome-maturity state;
* confidence-aware graph relationships;
* machine-participation and delegation provenance;
* a decision-first platform roadmap tied to realized value rather than connector count.

These contributions should be evaluated through reliability, correction accuracy, practitioner trust, reproducibility, governance outcomes, and the quality of decisions the platform improves.
