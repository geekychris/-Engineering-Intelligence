== Research foundations and source notes

The scientific framework in this chapter draws on measurement theory, causal inference, reproducibility, open science, cumulative evidence, and organizational learning. It extends those foundations into a research discipline for studying software engineering systems in production.

=== Measurement theory

The distinction among nominal, ordinal, interval, and ratio scales originates with <<stevens1946>>.

Construct validity is grounded in <<messick1995>>, while broader concerns about how measurement choices shape interpretation are discussed in <<hand2004>>.

The chapter therefore requires every scientific claim to state:

* the construct;
* the observed indicators;
* the scale and unit;
* the eligible population;
* the intended interpretation;
* the permitted use;
* known limitations.

A numerical output is not automatically a valid measure of the underlying construct.

=== Reliability

Reliability concerns the consistency of a measurement process.

The chapter draws on <<cronbach1951>> to distinguish repeatability from validity.

Relevant reliability questions include:

* Would the same source evidence produce the same metric version?
* Would independent labelers agree?
* Would reconstruction logic produce stable linkage?
* Would the result remain similar under repeated measurement where stability is expected?

A reliable measure can still be invalid if it consistently measures the wrong thing.

=== Causal inference

The chapter’s causal foundation comes from <<rubin1974>>, <<holland1986>>, <<pearl2009>>, <<imbens2015>>, and <<hernan2020>>.

These traditions support explicit separation among:

* observed association;
* prediction;
* intervention effect;
* mediation;
* mechanism;
* transportability.

Engineering Intelligence should not describe a temporal correlation as causal merely because the explanation is plausible.

=== Experimental design

The design-first approach is informed by <<shadish2002>>, <<angrist2009>>, and <<kohavi2020>>.

Scientific practice therefore includes:

* declared estimands;
* assignment and analysis units;
* primary outcomes and guardrails;
* power analysis;
* missing-data plans;
* treatment-version control;
* stopping rules;
* reproducible analysis.

The research question and decision should determine the design rather than the available dashboard determining the question.

=== Preregistration

Preregistration and pre-analysis planning help distinguish confirmatory analysis from exploration. See <<nosek2018>>.

The chapter therefore recommends registering:

* hypotheses;
* causal assumptions;
* outcomes;
* exclusions;
* subgroup analyzes;
* analysis methods;
* decision thresholds.

Exploratory analysis remains valuable, but its evidential status should be reported honestly.

=== Reproducibility

The reproducibility concerns illustrated by <<open-science2015>> support retaining enough evidence and computation to recreate published results.

The chapter requires preservation of:

* source snapshots or durable references;
* schemas and transformations;
* identity and reconstruction versions;
* metric and model versions;
* experiment assignment and exposure;
* analysis code;
* generated tables and figures;
* deviations and corrections.

A result that can be viewed but not reconstructed is weak scientific evidence.

=== FAIR evidence stewardship

The FAIR principles emphasize making research objects findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable. See <<wilkinson2016>>.

In an enterprise context, accessibility remains subject to privacy, security, and contractual constraints.

The chapter therefore interprets FAIR as governed discoverability and reuse with metadata, provenance, identifiers, and access controls.

=== Replication

Replication tests whether a finding persists across teams, repositories, task classes, time periods, and treatment versions.

A result from one group or one model version should not become universal policy automatically.

The chapter distinguishes:

* exact computational reproduction;
* direct replication;
* conceptual replication;
* operational replication in a new context.

Contradictory replication results are evidence about boundary conditions rather than failures to be hidden.

=== Cumulative evidence

Individual studies should contribute to a cumulative evidence base.

The chapter therefore records:

* intervention and comparator;
* population;
* context;
* effect estimate and uncertainty;
* treatment version;
* guardrails;
* study quality;
* replication status;
* realized outcome.

This enables later synthesis across studies rather than repeated isolated pilots.

=== Heterogeneity

Engineering effects are likely to vary by language, architecture, team structure, task complexity, developer experience, and organizational context.

The chapter therefore treats heterogeneity as scientifically important rather than as noise to average away.

Subgroup findings should be predeclared where possible, adequately powered, and replicated before policy use.

=== External validity

A causal result is local to its population, treatment, comparator, period, and operating conditions.

The internal- and external-validity tradition in <<shadish2002>> supports the chapter’s requirement to document boundary conditions.

Transporting a result requires evidence that the relevant causal mechanisms and populations are sufficiently similar.

=== Model evaluation

Models used for reconstruction, classification, ranking, prediction, or routing require evaluation appropriate to their function.

Relevant foundations include precision and recall from <<manning2008>> and ranked relevance from <<jarvelin2002>>.

The chapter also requires:

* calibration;
* subgroup performance;
* uncertainty;
* drift monitoring;
* abstention behavior;
* downstream decision impact.

Offline predictive performance alone is insufficient when model output changes human behavior or policy.

=== Negative and null results

Scientific practice is distorted when only positive findings are retained.

The chapter therefore treats null, negative, and contradictory outcomes as first-class evidence.

These results can reveal:

* interventions that do not generalize;
* hidden costs;
* weak mechanisms;
* boundary conditions;
* invalid metrics;
* insufficient power;
* implementation failure.

Preserving these findings reduces repeated waste and supports cumulative learning.

=== Measurement drift

Source systems, workflows, models, and organizational structures change over time.

A metric can continue computing successfully while its meaning changes.

The chapter therefore requires:

* semantic versioning;
* drift monitoring;
* periodic revalidation;
* correction and replay;
* retirement of invalid measures.

This extends ordinary data-drift practice into construct and policy drift.

=== Research ethics

Engineering research may involve practitioner behavior, sensitive telemetry, and consequential AI systems.

Relevant foundations include <<nist2020-privacy>>, <<nist2023-airmf>>, <<nist2024-genai>>, and <<acm2018-ethics>>.

The chapter therefore requires:

* purpose limitation;
* minimization;
* proportionality;
* transparent governance;
* human accountability;
* restrictions on punitive individual use;
* retention and deletion plans.

Scientific value does not override privacy, fairness, or professional responsibility.

=== Open science within enterprise constraints

Organizations may be unable to publish raw source code, telemetry, or personnel data.

The chapter therefore promotes layered openness:

* publish definitions and protocols where possible;
* share synthetic or de-identified fixtures;
* preserve internal reproducibility;
* publish aggregate findings and limitations;
* enable independent internal review;
* document unavailable evidence honestly.

This is an Engineering Intelligence-specific interpretation of open-science principles for enterprise settings.

=== Organizational learning

Argyris and Schön’s double-loop learning supports questioning the assumptions behind measurement and policy. See <<argyris1978>>.

Senge’s systems-thinking perspective supports learning across technical and organizational boundaries. See <<senge1990>>.

The scientific program should therefore review not only whether an intervention worked, but whether the original causal model and decision process were sound.

=== Original Engineering Intelligence contributions

The following are original synthesis or domain-specific extensions proposed by this book:

* Engineering Intelligence as a cumulative applied science of software engineering systems;
* Engineering Change as a common observational and experimental unit;
* explicit evidence states for observed, reconstructed, inferred, predicted, and causal claims;
* treatment-version drift as a scientific validity threat;
* integrated metric, model, experiment, evidence, and decision registries;
* outcome maturity as part of evidential status;
* enterprise-compatible open science;
* scientific retention of null, negative, and contradictory findings;
* replication across Engineering Change classes before policy generalization;
* post-decision study of realized value and unintended consequence.

These contributions should be evaluated by reproducibility, replication success, forecast calibration, decision quality, correction history, and the organization’s ability to learn from accumulated evidence.
