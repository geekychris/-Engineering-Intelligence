= The Engineering Metrics Catalogue

[abstract]
Engineering Intelligence requires a governed catalogue of metrics whose definitions, purposes, formulas, instrumentation, biases, and actions are explicit. This chapter provides a practical reference model for defining and operating metrics across flow, quality, economics, human attention, AI participation, architecture, and experimentation. The objective is not to maximize metric count, but to create a coherent set of measures that support decisions and remain reproducible over time.

== Why a catalogue matters

Metrics are often created as local queries, embedded dashboard formulas, or spreadsheet calculations. Over time, several definitions of the same concept emerge. "Cycle time" may mean commit to merge, pull-request open to merge, work start to deployment, or intent to accepted outcome. All can be valid, but they are not interchangeable.

A metric catalogue establishes one governed location for:

* semantic definition;
* decision purpose;
* unit of analysis;
* formula and exclusions;
* source events and lineage;
* model and metric version;
* uncertainty and data quality;
* known biases and gaming risks;
* intended actions and guardrails.

== Metric specification template

Every metric should contain the following fields:

[cols="1,3"]
|===
|Field |Description

|Identifier
|Stable machine-readable name

|Title
|Human-readable name

|Version
|Semantic version of definition and computation

|Construct
|Concept the metric is intended to measure

|Decision
|Decision or question the metric supports

|Unit of analysis
|Engineering Change, pull request, repository, service, team, portfolio, or time interval

|Population
|Eligible entities and exclusion rules

|Formula
|Exact mathematical or algorithmic definition

|Sources
|Events, entities, features, and models required

|Latency
|Expected time from source event to availability

|Uncertainty
|Confidence, credible interval, or quality state

|Interpretation
|What an increase or decrease may mean

|Failure modes
|Biases, confounders, gaming, and invalid uses

|Guardrails
|Outcomes that must remain within tolerance

|Owner
|Accountable group for maintenance and review
|===

== Flow metrics

=== Engineering Change lead time

Definition: elapsed time from accepted intent to accepted outcome.

[stem]
++++
LT(e) = t_{accepted\ outcome}(e) - t_{accepted\ intent}(e)
++++

Purpose: portfolio planning, delay analysis, and end-to-end flow optimization.

Instrumentation: planning events, Engineering Change lifecycle, deployment and outcome evidence.

Interpretation: lower values may indicate faster delivery, simpler work, smaller scope, or weaker verification. Always stratify by complexity and outcome class.

=== Active work time

Definition: inferred or observed duration during which human or AI participants actively worked on the change.

Purpose: distinguish execution effort from waiting.

Failure modes: passive telemetry may undercount offline reasoning and overcount open-but-idle sessions.

=== Queue time

Definition: duration in a state where progress awaits a constrained resource or policy.

[stem]
++++
QT(e) = \sum_{q \in Q_e} duration(q)
++++

Useful subtypes include review queue, CI queue, approval queue, environment queue, and deployment queue.

=== Flow efficiency

[stem]
++++
FE(e) = \frac{ActiveTime(e)}{LeadTime(e)}
++++

Flow efficiency is diagnostic, not an objective to maximize mechanically. Safety-critical work may rationally include waiting or observation windows.

=== Work in process

Definition: number of active Engineering Changes at a point in time or over a period.

Weighted WIP should incorporate complexity and unresolved involvement rather than treating every change as equal.

=== Aging work

Definition: age distribution of open Engineering Changes relative to expected duration for their class.

Purpose: detect stalled work before aggregate lead time worsens.

== Review metrics

=== Time to first substantive review

Bots, assignment acknowledgements, and non-substantive comments should be excluded unless explicitly part of the construct.

=== Active review effort

Definition: estimated human and AI effort spent understanding, verifying, and responding to a change.

=== Review response cycles

Definition: number of meaningful author-reviewer iteration loops before acceptance.

A high value may indicate poor initial quality, unclear expectations, complex work, or effective defect discovery.

=== Review queue age

Definition: age of changes awaiting review, preferably severity- or cost-of-delay-weighted.

=== Review Friction Index

A composite diagnostic metric:

[stem]
++++
RFI(e) = \sum_k w_k z_k(e)
++++

Potential components include queue age, response cycles, context recovery, unresolved discussions, and validation effort.

The index must expose component values and should be calibrated against delay and rework.

== Quality metrics

=== Change failure rate

Definition: proportion of accepted outcomes that require rollback, hotfix, incident response, or material remediation within a declared window.

The remediation threshold and attribution window must be explicit.

=== Escaped defect rate

Definition: defects attributable to a change and discovered after the designated release boundary.

Use severity weighting and confidence of attribution.

=== Severity-weighted defect cost

[stem]
++++
DWC = \sum_i w_{severity(i)} \times count_i
++++

Weights should reflect expected impact or policy, and remain versioned.

=== Rework ratio

[stem]
++++
RR(e) = \frac{PostAcceptanceRework(e)}{TotalEffort(e)}
++++

Rework includes corrective changes linked to the original Engineering Change. Do not count planned follow-on development as rework.

=== Detection stage distribution

Shows where defects are found: authoring, AI review, human review, CI, staging, rollout, production, or customer report.

Earlier detection is often cheaper, but stage alone does not establish value without severity and effort.

== Economic metrics

=== Total Engineering Change cost

[stem]
++++
C_{total} = C_{human}+C_{AI}+C_{tool}+C_{infra}+C_{coord}+C_{delay}+C_{quality}+C_{risk}
++++

This is a model with explicit assumptions, not a directly observed number.

=== Cost per accepted outcome

[stem]
++++
CPO = \frac{\sum_e C_{total}(e)}{N_{accepted\ outcomes}}
++++

Use only within comparable outcome classes.

=== Cost of delay

Definition: value lost because an outcome arrives later than its relevant value curve.

Report ranges and scenario assumptions rather than false precision.

=== Scarce-attention consumption

Definition: attention-weighted effort from constrained roles or expertise pools.

Purpose: identify organizational bottlenecks and evaluate routing policies.

=== Tool net value

[stem]
++++
TNV = Benefit_{labor}+Benefit_{quality}+Benefit_{delay}-Cost_{license}-Cost_{infra}-Cost_{validation}
++++

Benefits should be causal estimates whenever possible.

== Human and cognitive metrics

=== Context recovery time

Definition: time required to resume productive work after a material interruption or delay.

=== Concurrent cognitive inventory

[stem]
++++
CI_p(t)=\sum_{e \in Active_p(t)} w_e u_{p,e}(t)
++++

Use for system research and workload design, not individual performance ranking.

=== Cognitive Offload Index

[stem]
++++
COI = \frac{\hat{W}_{control}-\hat{W}_{treatment}}{\hat{W}_{control}}
++++

Must be paired with quality, learning, and trust guardrails.

=== Cognitive reload

Definition: additional human work caused by inspecting, correcting, rejecting, or recovering from tool or AI output.

=== Attention fragmentation

Possible measure: number and weighted severity of task transitions within active work windows.

The metric requires careful validation because transitions can also reflect healthy collaboration or deliberate batching.

== AI metrics

=== Finding precision and recall

Use labeled findings and severity weighting.

=== Precision at k

Measures the proportion of relevant findings among the top stem:[k] positions shown to a human.

Purpose: evaluate whether limited attention is directed to useful findings.

=== NDCG

Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain evaluates ranking when findings have graded relevance or severity.

=== Duplicate finding rate

Definition: proportion of AI findings duplicating another AI or human finding without additional value.

=== Human validation burden

Measures inspection, investigation, correction, and recovery effort attributable to AI output.

=== Action rate

Tracks direct application, modified application, equivalent independent fix, follow-up issue, acknowledgement, rejection, and no response.

=== AI contribution latency

Time from change state to useful AI contribution. Late findings may have low value even if correct.

=== AI Leverage Factor

A decision model relating value and avoided human or quality cost to AI and validation cost.

== Architecture and topology metrics

=== Repository spread

Number of repositories participating in one Engineering Change.

Interpret as topology, not intrinsic effort.

=== Dependency depth

Longest dependency path among affected components.

=== Critical-path duration

Longest duration-weighted path through the Engineering Change dependency graph.

=== Ownership boundary count

Number of distinct ownership or approval boundaries crossed.

=== Blast Radius Score

Composite of users, systems, data, financial exposure, availability, compliance, and reversibility.

=== Change surface to impact surface ratio

[stem]
++++
SIR(e) = \frac{ImpactSurface(e)}{ChangeSurface(e)}
++++

A high ratio can identify small changes with disproportionate reach.

== Experiment metrics

=== Treatment exposure rate

[stem]
++++
ExposureRate = \frac{ExposedEligibleUnits}{AssignedTreatmentUnits}
++++

=== Compliance rate

Measures adherence to the assigned workflow or policy.

=== Average treatment effect

[stem]
++++
ATE = E[Y(1)-Y(0)]
++++

=== Minimum practical effect attainment

Probability or confidence that the effect exceeds a predeclared practical threshold.

=== Guardrail breach rate

Frequency and severity with which experiment guardrails exceed tolerance.

=== Experiment decision latency

Time from experiment registration to evidence-backed decision.

== Data quality metrics

=== Event completeness

Proportion of expected source events received.

=== Linkage coverage

Proportion of artifacts linked to Engineering Changes above a confidence threshold.

=== Identity ambiguity rate

Proportion of events whose actor, repository, service, or team identity remains unresolved or multiply matched.

=== Freshness

Age of the newest complete data available to a metric.

=== Replay divergence

Difference between streaming results and authoritative recomputation.

== Metric bundles

Metrics should often be consumed as bundles rather than isolated values.

=== AI review bundle

* human review effort;
* AI validation burden;
* severe finding recall;
* precision at k;
* duplicate rate;
* cycle time;
* escaped defects;
* tool and inference cost.

=== Flow bundle

* lead time;
* active time;
* queue time by stage;
* WIP;
* aging distribution;
* flow efficiency;
* accepted outcome rate.

=== Platform bundle

* event freshness;
* linkage coverage;
* metric latency;
* replay divergence;
* service availability;
* source failure rate.

== Metric lifecycle

A metric progresses through proposed, experimental, validated, operational, deprecated, and retired states.

A proposed metric should not appear in executive scorecards. A validated metric has evidence of construct validity and reliability. An operational metric has an owner, SLO, lineage, and action process.

== Review and retirement

Metrics should be reviewed periodically for:

* continuing decision relevance;
* changed workflows or source semantics;
* gaming or behavioral adaptation;
* data quality;
* overlap with other metrics;
* evidence of action;
* cost of maintenance.

A metric that no longer informs a decision should be retired, not preserved indefinitely because it once appeared in a dashboard.

== Common failure modes

=== Name without definition

Two teams report "cycle time" using different start and end points.

=== Formula without construct

A number is precisely computed but does not validly represent the concept claimed.

=== Metric without decision

Dashboards accumulate measures with no operational action.

=== Score without components

Composite metrics hide the reason for movement and invite gaming.

=== Dollar value without assumptions

Derived economics appear exact despite uncertain rates, attribution, and counterfactuals.

=== Individual ranking

System metrics are repurposed as performance scores, damaging trust and validity.

== Key takeaways

* A governed metric catalogue is necessary for reproducible Engineering Intelligence.
* Every metric requires a construct, decision, unit, formula, lineage, uncertainty, owner, and failure modes.
* Flow, quality, economics, cognition, AI, architecture, experiments, and data quality should be represented together.
* Composite metrics must preserve drill-down into components.
* Derived measures require explicit models and validation.
* Metrics should have lifecycle states and retirement criteria.

== Open questions

* How should metric definitions be standardized across companies while preserving local context?
* What evidence threshold should move a derived metric from experimental to operational?
* How can metric catalogues encode causal assumptions explicitly?
* Which measures remain robust after becoming organizational targets?

== Implementation checklist

. Create a versioned metric specification schema.
. Require decision, construct, unit, formula, sources, uncertainty, guardrails, and owner.
. Build initial bundles for flow, quality, AI review, and platform health.
. Separate experimental metrics from operational scorecards.
. Expose component drill-down for every composite measure.
. Publish data quality and freshness with metric values.
. Establish periodic review and retirement.
. Prohibit unsupported individual ranking.
. Preserve historical versions for reproducibility.
. Link every operational metric to an explicit action process.
