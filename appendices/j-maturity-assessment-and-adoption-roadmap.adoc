= Maturity Assessment and Adoption Roadmap

[abstract]
This appendix provides a structured method for assessing an organization’s current Engineering Intelligence capability and planning its next stages. It is designed for organizations that already possess substantial engineering telemetry but lack a coherent evidence-to-decision system. The assessment evaluates evidence quality, semantics, measurement, experimentation, economics, governance, platform architecture, organizational learning, and AI readiness. It also provides a practical first-90-day launch plan and a staged roadmap for advancing without attempting an enterprise-wide transformation all at once.

== Assessment philosophy

A maturity model should not become a ranking exercise or a ceremonial scorecard.

Its purpose is to answer three questions:

. Which capabilities are currently strong enough to support consequential decisions?
. Which missing or weak capabilities create the greatest decision risk?
. What is the smallest next investment that closes a complete evidence-to-decision loop?

The assessment should evaluate demonstrated operating capability rather than the existence of policies, dashboards, or architecture diagrams.

== Maturity levels

The six levels below describe what an Engineering Intelligence capability looks like as it accumulates. The transitions between levels are as important as the levels themselves — each ratchet closes a specific gap that was previously invisible, and reversing course is possible when a level's investment fails to hold. Read the diagram below as capabilities that must exist before the next level's practices can be sustained, not as a linear timeline.

image::../figures/mermaid/maturity-ratchet.mmd[Maturity ratchet — the capability added at each transition,align=center]

=== Level 0: fragmented telemetry

The organization has source-specific dashboards and reports but no stable cross-system model.

In practice, this level looks like teams answering the same question three different ways depending on which tool they open first. Cross-team performance discussions rely on screenshots and story-time rather than shared evidence, and a leadership request for "the number" produces a two-week investigation whose result cannot be reproduced next quarter.

Typical characteristics:

* metrics are defined locally;
* pull requests, tickets, deployments, and incidents cannot be connected reliably;
* current ownership is applied to historical evidence;
* activity counts dominate;
* AI tools are evaluated through usage or anecdote;
* decisions are not linked to evidence versions;
* data quality is mostly implicit.

Decision risk is high because evidence is incomplete, inconsistent, and difficult to reproduce.

=== Level 1: descriptive visibility

The organization has several useful operational metrics and a basic shared vocabulary.

At this level a leader can open a dashboard and see how flow, quality, and reliability move over time without arguing about definitions. What remains missing is mechanism: nobody can yet say whether a change in the numbers reflects a change in the underlying system, a change in the workload, or a change in how the tools reported it.

Typical characteristics:

* major source systems are ingested;
* some team and repository metrics are standardized;
* basic lineage and data-quality monitoring exist;
* leadership can describe broad flow and reliability patterns;
* privacy and access rules are documented;
* measurement remains mostly descriptive.

The organization can identify patterns but cannot consistently explain mechanisms or causal effects.

=== Level 2: connected Engineering Change evidence

The organization can connect intent, artifacts, review, validation, deployment, release, and outcomes for a meaningful population.

Reaching this level is the first point at which _end-to-end_ questions become answerable — "which changes actually reached production and stayed there," "which repository boundaries added coordination delay to changes that spanned them," "how much reviewer time did the top 5% of high-blast-radius changes consume." Reconstruction is imperfect and confidence bands appear on every joined dataset, but analysts stop reasoning about pull requests and start reasoning about Engineering Changes.

Typical characteristics:

* Engineering Change relationships are reconstructed;
* linkage confidence is visible;
* identity and ownership are temporal;
* source contracts and quality SLOs exist;
* metrics are computed from shared evidence;
* correction and replay are operational;
* decision products begin to replace dashboard browsing.

The organization can analyze end-to-end flow and consequence rather than isolated tool objects.

=== Level 3: governed decision intelligence

Metrics and analyses are treated as versioned, governed products.

At Level 3 a metric change is a release, not a query edit. Consequential measures carry semantic versions, published assumptions, and uncertainty. Decision records reference the specific metric version that was used, so a decision made a year ago can be re-inspected under the same rules. Prohibited uses are enforceable, not aspirational, and dashboards that fail their governance criteria are retired rather than tolerated.

Typical characteristics:

* metric specifications include purpose, population, formula, quality, and prohibited use;
* uncertainty accompanies consequential measures;
* governance uses are registered;
* privacy, access, correction, and appeal operate in practice;
* economic evaluations distinguish capacity from savings;
* decision records include evidence, assumptions, and review dates;
* negative results are retained.

The organization can make defensible portfolio and operational decisions from governed evidence.

=== Level 4: experimental learning system

The organization routinely evaluates interventions causally.

At this level, "we rolled it out and things got better" is no longer treated as evidence that a tool worked. Interventions are launched with an assignment mechanism, an exposure signal, guardrails, and a pre-analysis plan; results carry intervals and are validated against realized long-term outcomes rather than immediate metric movement. Negative results are shipped alongside positive ones because they remove interventions that would otherwise linger in policy by default.

Typical characteristics:

* deterministic assignment and observed exposure are available;
* guardrails and stop rules are operational;
* randomized and quasi-experimental methods are used appropriately;
* pre-analysis plans and reproducibility packages are standard;
* findings are replicated across contexts;
* value realization is measured after adoption;
* intervention versions are tracked over time.

The organization can distinguish plausible improvement from measured causal effect.

=== Level 5: adaptive engineering organization

Engineering Intelligence is embedded in operating policy and organizational learning.

The distinguishing property of Level 5 is that the organization's own operating model is a subject of measurement. Policies, thresholds, review routing, capacity allocation, and even the metrics themselves are treated as interventions with lifecycles: they are versioned, evaluated for continued fit, and retired when the evidence changes. A reorganization does not reset the learning; the graph of decisions, evidence, and outcomes survives the org chart.

Typical characteristics:

* decisions, evidence, metrics, models, and outcomes form a persistent graph;
* portfolio investment adapts to evidence maturity and realized value;
* bounded automated policies operate with audit, appeal, and rollback;
* AI agents have identity, authority, and validation provenance;
* long-term capability and skill effects are monitored;
* learning is preserved across teams and reorganizations;
* the organization continuously revises assumptions and operating models.

The organization is capable of learning how it engineers, not merely measuring what it did.

== Assessment dimensions

The maturity level should be scored separately for each dimension. A single average can hide critical weaknesses.

== Decision orientation

=== Diagnostic questions

* Are major measurement efforts tied to named decisions?
* Does every consequential analysis have a decision owner?
* Are credible alternatives identified?
* Are decisions recorded with assumptions, conditions, and review dates?
* Can the organization show cases where evidence changed or stopped a decision?

=== Evidence of maturity

* active decision-question registry;
* bounded evidence requests;
* decision records;
* review-date compliance;
* examples of reversal or modification based on later evidence.

=== Common failure

Dashboards exist without a clear user, decision, or action.

== Source evidence and data contracts

=== Diagnostic questions

* Are source semantics documented?
* Are event time and ingestion time both preserved?
* Can data be replayed?
* Are connector completeness and freshness measured?
* Are deletions and corrections propagated?
* Are source owners accountable for changes?

=== Evidence of maturity

* source inventory;
* versioned data contracts;
* immutable raw evidence;
* connector SLOs;
* backfill and replay records;
* schema-change review.

=== Common failure

Source APIs are treated as stable facts while tool semantics change silently.

== Engineering Change semantics

=== Diagnostic questions

* Can the organization connect intent to production outcome?
* Are relationships explicit, deterministic, or probabilistic?
* Is linkage confidence visible?
* Are changes spanning multiple repositories or services represented?
* Can corrections be applied without deleting history?

=== Evidence of maturity

* canonical Engineering Change identifier;
* relationship model;
* confidence bands;
* reconstruction version;
* known linkage-coverage metrics;
* correction workflow.

=== Common failure

The pull request becomes the permanent analytical unit even when consequence spans multiple systems.

== Identity, ownership, and organizational context

=== Diagnostic questions

* Are people, service accounts, bots, assistants, and agents distinguished?
* Is team and service ownership resolved as of event time?
* Are reorganizations preserved historically?
* Can analysts work with pseudonymous identities?
* Is re-identification restricted and audited?

=== Evidence of maturity

* canonical actor model;
* temporal ownership intervals;
* identity-confidence measures;
* isolated identity mapping;
* access logs;
* correction process.

=== Common failure

Current organizational structure is applied retrospectively, distorting historical comparison.

== Metric quality

=== Diagnostic questions

* Does each metric identify its construct and decision purpose?
* Are unit, population, interval, formula, and aggregation explicit?
* Are missing data and bot activity handled deliberately?
* Are uncertainty, validity, and prohibited uses documented?
* Are metric versions visible in dashboards and APIs?

=== Evidence of maturity

* metric registry;
* validation records;
* quality gates;
* versioned computation;
* interpretation guidance;
* deprecation and retirement history.

=== Common failure

A familiar metric name conceals several incompatible implementations.

== Human attention and work-system measurement

=== Diagnostic questions

* Can active effort be separated from elapsed delay?
* Are queues, interruption, rework, and validation burden measured?
* Does analysis account for work mix and complexity?
* Are local experience and qualitative evidence included?
* Are measures prohibited from unsupported individual ranking?

=== Evidence of maturity

* active-time methods with known limitations;
* queue-state instrumentation;
* review and validation sessions;
* experience sampling or practitioner interviews;
* work-mix adjustment;
* acceptable-use controls.

=== Common failure

Visible activity is treated as a substitute for cognitive work or productivity.

== Experimentation and causal inference

=== Diagnostic questions

* Can treatment assignment be reproduced?
* Is actual exposure observed?
* Are primary outcomes and guardrails predeclared?
* Are clustering, spillover, and noncompliance considered?
* Are observational claims distinguished from causal claims?
* Are negative and null results preserved?

=== Evidence of maturity

* experiment registry;
* assignment and exposure service;
* readiness review;
* pre-analysis plans;
* reproducibility packages;
* replication records.

=== Common failure

Before-and-after movement is reported as causal improvement.

== Economic evaluation

=== Diagnostic questions

* Are alternatives and counterfactuals explicit?
* Are implementation, validation, migration, support, and exit costs included?
* Is released capacity separated from savings?
* Are realization and fragmentation assumptions visible?
* Are bottlenecks and downstream constraints considered?
* Are forecast and actual value tracked?

=== Evidence of maturity

* decision-grade business cases;
* scenario and sensitivity analysis;
* break-even models;
* benefit-dependency map;
* realization ledger;
* renewal review.

=== Common failure

Minutes saved are multiplied by salary and presented as financial savings.

== Architecture and complexity intelligence

=== Diagnostic questions

* Are repository, service, dependency, deployment, and ownership graphs connected?
* Can blast radius be estimated and calibrated?
* Are architectural boundaries linked to change effort and outcomes?
* Can coupling and ownership fragmentation be analyzed over time?
* Are architectural decisions linked to later evidence?

=== Evidence of maturity

* service and repository catalogue;
* dependency graph;
* change-coupling analysis;
* incident-change linkage;
* architecture decision records;
* longitudinal architecture metrics.

=== Common failure

Static code-complexity measures are interpreted as complete architecture quality.

== Governance, privacy, and trust

=== Diagnostic questions

* Are permitted, restricted, and prohibited uses explicit?
* Is access purpose-bound and time-limited?
* Can practitioners inspect and correct attributed evidence?
* Are small groups and re-identification protected?
* Are vendor data use and model training constrained contractually?
* Are governance incidents detectable and handled?

=== Evidence of maturity

* governed-use registry;
* access recertification;
* practitioner notice;
* correction and appeal records;
* audit logs;
* incident and exception management.

=== Common failure

Governance exists as policy text but not as enforced architecture and operating practice.

== AI and agent readiness

=== Diagnostic questions

* Are model, prompt, retrieval, tool, and configuration versions captured?
* Can human and machine contribution be attributed appropriately?
* Is validation burden measured?
* Are quality, security, cost, and capability guardrails present?
* Are agent authority and delegation boundaries explicit?
* Can a model or agent version be rolled back rapidly?

=== Evidence of maturity

* model and prompt registries;
* AI activity provenance;
* contribution and rework measures;
* continuous evaluation;
* delegation graph;
* bounded action policies;
* rollback tests.

=== Common failure

AI adoption, generated output, or acceptance is reported as productivity or value.

== Platform and operational reliability

=== Diagnostic questions

* Are freshness, completeness, linkage, identity, and serving SLOs defined?
* Can source or metric incidents be detected quickly?
* Are environments isolated appropriately?
* Is cost tracked by source, pipeline, and product?
* Can the platform recover from corruption or regional failure?
* Are semantic changes run in shadow mode?

=== Evidence of maturity

* platform SLOs;
* quality dashboards;
* incident procedures;
* disaster-recovery tests;
* lineage impact analysis;
* workload and cost controls.

=== Common failure

The system measures engineering reliability without operating as a reliable system itself.

== Organizational learning

=== Diagnostic questions

* Are decisions revisited after implementation?
* Are negative results and failed interventions preserved?
* Can learning survive team turnover and reorganization?
* Are practitioner observations incorporated?
* Are assumptions and boundary conditions recorded?
* Does portfolio allocation change based on realized evidence?

=== Evidence of maturity

* learning reviews;
* decision follow-up;
* evidence registry;
* replication agenda;
* value-realization review;
* retired or reversed policy records.

=== Common failure

Reports are produced, presented once, and then lost as organizational memory.

== Scoring method

Score each dimension from 0 to 5 using demonstrated evidence.

[cols="1,4",options="header"]
|===
|Score |Meaning

|0
|Capability is absent or entirely informal.

|1
|Local examples exist, but semantics and ownership are inconsistent.

|2
|A repeatable process exists for a limited population or workflow.

|3
|The capability is governed and used for consequential decisions.

|4
|The capability is validated, monitored, and reproducible across several contexts.

|5
|The capability is adaptive, integrated, and improves through accumulated evidence.
|===

A score should cite specific evidence and limitations.

Example:

[source,yaml]
----
assessment:
  dimension: experimentation_and_causal_inference
  score: 2
  evidence:
    - repository_level_randomization_available_for_two_platforms
    - primary_metrics_versioned
  limitations:
    - treatment_exposure_not_consistently_recorded
    - no_standard_reproducibility_package
    - limited_guardrail_latency
  next_capability:
    - implement_exposure_service
    - standardize_readiness_and_analysis_records
----

== Weighting by decision risk

Not every dimension has equal priority for every organization.

A tool-evaluation program may prioritize:

* experimentation;
* metric quality;
* AI provenance;
* economics;
* governance.

An architecture modernization program may prioritize:

* Engineering Change linkage;
* service and dependency catalogues;
* complexity and blast radius;
* longitudinal outcomes;
* portfolio economics.

A high-risk automated policy should require strong scores in evidence, governance, causal learning, platform reliability, and rollback before deployment.

== Critical-capability floor

An overall maturity score should never compensate for a dangerous weakness.

Examples:

* strong analytics do not compensate for prohibited-use risk;
* excellent dashboards do not compensate for missing source lineage;
* randomized assignment does not compensate for unobserved exposure;
* high adoption does not compensate for absent quality guardrails;
* economic upside does not compensate for unacceptable security risk.

For consequential decisions, define minimum dimension scores before use.

== Assessment process

A credible assessment should include:

. executive and decision-owner interviews;
. practitioner and domain interviews;
. source and platform architecture review;
. metric-definition review;
. sample lineage walkthroughs;
. experiment and decision record review;
. governance and access review;
. economic-model review;
. live demonstration of correction and replay;
. evidence-based scoring workshop.

The assessment should not rely solely on self-reported survey scores.

== Assessment outputs

The assessment should produce:

* dimension scores with evidence;
* decision risks;
* capability dependencies;
* quick wins;
* critical controls;
* recommended pilot decision;
* first-90-day plan;
* 12- to 24-month roadmap;
* owners and investment assumptions;
* review date.

== Selecting the first decision

The best first use case is consequential enough to matter but bounded enough to complete.

Strong candidates have:

* a named decision owner;
* two or more credible alternatives;
* meaningful cost or risk;
* an observable workflow;
* available source evidence;
* measurable outcomes within months;
* reversible or bounded implementation;
* practitioner interest;
* no need for individual performance use.

Examples include:

* selecting a pull-request review tool;
* improving CI queue capacity;
* changing review routing;
* evaluating a merge queue;
* prioritizing an architecture bottleneck;
* comparing an AI coding-assistant configuration;
* changing deployment policy for low-risk services.

== Poor first decisions

Avoid beginning with:

* universal engineering productivity;
* individual performance scoring;
* enterprise-wide agent autonomy;
* a complete engineering ontology;
* every source system at once;
* long-term architecture value with no short-term outcomes;
* a decision with no accountable owner;
* a politically predetermined vendor selection.

== First 30 days: define and bound

=== Outcomes

By day 30, the program should have:

* one consequential decision question;
* an executive decision owner;
* a cross-functional working group;
* a mapped Engineering Change lifecycle;
* a source and evidence inventory;
* initial governance and prohibited-use agreement;
* candidate primary metrics and guardrails;
* identified data-quality gaps;
* a written pilot charter.

=== Activities

. Interview decision owners and practitioners.
. Document alternatives and decision timing.
. Map actors, artifacts, queues, validation, deployment, and outcomes.
. Identify source systems and source owners.
. Review privacy, security, and employment-use boundaries.
. Select the smallest eligible population.
. Draft metric and experiment specifications.
. Establish a weekly evidence review.

=== Exit criteria

Do not proceed merely because instrumentation is available.

Proceed when:

* the decision and alternatives are clear;
* evidence requirements are bounded;
* owners accept the governance constraints;
* outcomes and guardrails are observable or can become observable;
* the pilot can produce a decision.

== Days 31 to 60: build the evidence path

=== Outcomes

By day 60, the program should have:

* immutable ingestion for the required sources;
* canonical event mapping;
* actor, repository, service, and ownership references;
* Engineering Change linkage for the pilot population;
* linkage and source-quality reporting;
* draft governed metrics;
* correction and replay capability;
* a shadow decision product.

=== Activities

. Implement or harden connectors.
. Preserve event and ingestion time.
. Establish source contracts.
. Reconstruct Engineering Changes.
. Calibrate linkage confidence on a labeled sample.
. Implement metric formulas and quality gates.
. Conduct practitioner validation.
. Review identity and access controls.
. Run historical backfills and reconciliation.

=== Exit criteria

Proceed when:

* source completeness and freshness are known;
* linkage quality is adequate for the declared use;
* metric semantics are reviewable;
* missingness is visible;
* corrections can propagate;
* practitioners recognize the workflow represented.

== Days 61 to 90: evaluate and decide

=== Outcomes

By day 90, the program should have:

* an approved study or evaluation protocol;
* deterministic assignment or a defensible comparison design;
* observed treatment exposure;
* operational guardrails;
* an economic model with scenarios;
* a decision record template;
* an implementation and value-realization plan.

=== Activities

. Conduct readiness and power review.
. Register the pre-analysis plan.
. Begin or complete the bounded evaluation.
. Monitor data quality and safety.
. Estimate direct cost, validation burden, and released capacity.
. Identify realization conditions and bottlenecks.
. Present effect size, uncertainty, limitations, and alternatives.
. Record an adopt, modify, extend, reject, or reverse decision.

=== Exit criteria

The first 90 days succeed when the organization closes one complete loop from decision question to governed evidence and recorded action.

A large platform without a decision is not a successful first quarter.

== Months 4 to 6: operationalize

Focus on:

* stabilizing source and metric contracts;
* expanding to adjacent populations;
* completing outcome follow-up;
* tracking value realization;
* implementing correction and appeal;
* documenting negative results;
* onboarding domain stewards;
* improving publication and decision products;
* reviewing platform cost and reliability.

The main objective is repeatability, not connector count.

== Months 7 to 12: expand causal and economic capability

Focus on:

* reusable assignment and exposure services;
* standard experiment templates;
* quasi-experimental methods for nonrandomized rollouts;
* business-case and realization ledgers;
* architecture and blast-radius analysis;
* cross-team replication;
* evidence and decision registries;
* quarterly learning portfolio reviews.

By the end of year one, several decisions should have complete evidence-to-outcome histories.

== Year 2: enterprise learning system

Potential priorities include:

* broader Engineering Change coverage;
* temporal organizational and service models;
* governed metric and model marketplaces;
* multi-region and regulated-domain controls;
* AI model, prompt, and agent provenance;
* continuous tool evaluation;
* architecture digital twins and simulations;
* bounded automated policy;
* cross-organization benchmarking under privacy controls.

These capabilities should be justified by demonstrated decision value from earlier stages.

== Capability dependency map

Some capabilities must precede others.

Examples:

* causal analysis depends on stable outcomes, eligibility, assignment, and exposure;
* economic realization depends on measured effects and operational ownership;
* automated policy depends on metric quality, model validation, governance, and rollback;
* architecture intelligence depends on service, dependency, deployment, and ownership data;
* AI evaluation depends on model and prompt provenance plus human validation evidence;
* portfolio optimization depends on comparable business cases and realized outcomes.

Skipping dependencies creates false maturity.

== Team and sponsorship model

A launch team should include:

* accountable executive sponsor;
* Engineering Intelligence product owner;
* data or platform engineering;
* measurement or causal expertise;
* developer-experience or workflow expertise;
* domain representatives;
* security and privacy;
* finance partner for economic evaluation;
* practitioner representatives.

A large permanent organization is not required initially. Clear ownership and access to these capabilities are required.

== Funding model

Initial funding should be tied to a bounded decision portfolio.

A useful investment structure is:

* foundational platform funding for common evidence and governance;
* domain funding for source integration and semantic stewardship;
* decision-owner funding for studies and interventions;
* central measurement expertise shared across programs;
* explicit operational ownership after pilot completion.

The platform should not become an unfunded central reporting service with unlimited demand.

== Adoption signals

Healthy adoption is visible when:

* teams request help framing decisions rather than requesting dashboards;
* metric definitions are challenged constructively;
* practitioners use correction workflows;
* decision owners accept uncertainty and bounded rollout;
* negative results affect investment;
* finance distinguishes capacity from savings;
* governance incidents are surfaced rather than hidden;
* platform priorities follow decision value;
* prior decisions are revisited using realized outcomes.

== Warning signals

The program is drifting when:

* connector count is the primary progress metric;
* leaders ask for individual rankings;
* local teams rebuild metrics outside the registry;
* experiments lack exposure or guardrails;
* every result is positive;
* modeled time becomes reported savings;
* practitioners do not recognize the evidence model;
* governance review occurs only after deployment;
* old decisions are never revisited;
* AI usage is treated as AI value.

== Maturity reassessment cadence

Reassess at least every six months during the first two years.

Also reassess after:

* major organizational change;
* new source-control or deployment platform;
* significant AI model or agent adoption;
* governance incident;
* metric or reconstruction redesign;
* expansion into regulated populations;
* transition from advisory to automated policy.

Each reassessment should compare promised and demonstrated capability.

== Roadmap decision rules

Prioritize work that:

* closes a complete decision loop;
* improves evidence quality for several decisions;
* reduces a critical governance or validity risk;
* enables causal evaluation;
* creates reusable semantics rather than vendor lock-in;
* supports correction and reproducibility;
* can demonstrate value within a bounded horizon.

Deprioritize work that:

* adds data without a purpose;
* optimizes dashboard aesthetics before semantics;
* builds universal abstractions before a working use case;
* automates decisions before governance maturity;
* requires unsupported individual surveillance;
* has no owner for realized value.

== Assessment anti-patterns

=== Maturity theater

Scores are inflated to satisfy leadership expectations rather than grounded in evidence.

=== Average-score optimism

Strong dimensions conceal one critical weakness.

=== Tool-based maturity

Owning a warehouse, graph database, experimentation platform, or AI gateway is mistaken for operating capability.

=== Roadmap by connector

The program plans source integrations without specifying which decisions each enables.

=== Enterprise-wide first step

The first release attempts to model every team and workflow.

=== Permanent pilot team

The central team continues running local processes that should have operational owners.

=== Governance last

Privacy and acceptable-use constraints are introduced after detailed individual evidence is already broadly available.

=== No realization review

The program demonstrates metric movement but never determines whether the decision produced value.

== Maturity and adoption checklist

. Assess demonstrated capabilities by dimension rather than relying on one maturity score.
. Cite concrete evidence, limitations, and decision risks for every score.
. Define minimum capability floors for consequential uses.
. Choose one bounded decision with alternatives, an owner, observable outcomes, and reversibility.
. Complete a full evidence-to-decision loop in the first 90 days.
. Build only the source, semantic, metric, governance, and analytical capabilities required for that loop.
. Measure source quality, linkage confidence, uncertainty, and prohibited uses explicitly.
. Separate released capacity, realized value, and financial savings.
. Expand through replication and reuse rather than immediate enterprise breadth.
. Reassess after major technical, organizational, model, or governance change.
. Judge progress by decisions improved, harmful choices avoided, and learning retained.
