= Governance, Privacy, and Acceptable Use Standard

[abstract]
This appendix defines an operating standard for governing Engineering Intelligence systems. It translates ethical principles into concrete controls for data collection, access, metric use, experimentation, AI evaluation, automated policy, privacy, retention, incident response, appeal, and audit. The objective is to enable credible organizational learning while preventing surveillance, unsupported employment decisions, discriminatory treatment, and unsafe automation.

== Purpose

Engineering Intelligence systems combine evidence from source control, planning tools, CI, deployment systems, incidents, identities, organizational structures, AI tools, and business outcomes.

That combination creates substantial value and substantial risk.

The same evidence can be used to:

* improve review routing;
* evaluate an AI tool;
* reduce queue delay;
* identify architecture constraints;
* optimize infrastructure cost;
* support portfolio decisions;

or to:

* rank individuals through weak proxies;
* infer sensitive characteristics;
* monitor private behavior;
* automate punitive decisions;
* conceal uncertainty behind authoritative dashboards;
* create irreversible policy from invalid metrics.

Governance must therefore control not only what data is collected, but how evidence is interpreted and used.

== Core principles

The principles below are not a checklist; they are the constraints that keep an Engineering Intelligence programme legitimate over time. Every subsequent rule in this appendix — classification, registration, retention, appeal — is an operational expression of one or more of these principles, and any exception process should trace its reasoning back to them explicitly.

=== Purpose limitation

Data and metrics may be used only for declared, reviewed purposes.

=== Data minimization

Collect the least detailed evidence needed to support the approved decision.

=== Proportionality

The intrusiveness and consequence of measurement should be proportionate to the value and risk of the decision.

=== Transparency

Affected practitioners should understand what is collected, how metrics are defined, and which uses are prohibited.

=== Contestability

People and teams must be able to challenge incorrect data, interpretations, models, and decisions.

=== Human accountability

Automated systems may recommend or execute bounded actions, but accountable organizational owners remain responsible for policy and outcomes.

=== Evidence quality

No high-consequence use should proceed when source quality, metric validity, linkage, or uncertainty is inadequate.

=== Temporal relevance

Evidence should be interpreted in the context in which it was produced. Old metrics and model outputs should not be treated as permanently valid.

=== Separation of learning and punishment

Exploratory and operational learning systems should not be silently repurposed into employment or disciplinary systems.

== Roles and responsibilities

=== Executive sponsor

Provides mandate, resources, and accountability for the overall program.

=== Engineering Intelligence owner

Owns the platform, evidence quality, operating model, and adherence to this standard.

=== Data owner

Owns source semantics, permitted use, retention, and source-specific access decisions.

=== Metric owner

Owns construct definition, interpretation, versioning, quality gates, and retirement.

=== Study owner

Owns experimental design, exposure, analysis, guardrails, and reporting.

=== Domain representative

Validates local workflow meaning and operational feasibility.

=== Privacy reviewer

Assesses purpose limitation, minimization, retention, re-identification, and sensitive-data risk.

=== Security reviewer

Assesses access, integrity, isolation, model and tool risk, and adversarial threats.

=== Legal or compliance reviewer

Assesses contractual, regulatory, employment, labor, and jurisdictional obligations.

=== Practitioner representative

Provides the perspective of people whose work or teams are represented in the system.

=== Governance board

Approves high-risk uses, resolves disputed interpretations, and reviews incidents and policy changes.

== Use classification

Every proposed use should be classified by consequence and sensitivity. The class determines which review body inspects the proposal, what evidence must accompany it, how deeply access is scoped, and how frequently the use is re-examined. A single misclassification — most commonly a Class 3 or 4 use being registered as Class 1 to speed approval — undermines every downstream control, so classification decisions themselves are audited and appealable.

=== Class 0: platform operations

Examples:

* connector health;
* event freshness;
* schema failures;
* metric pipeline incidents.

These uses generally involve low human consequence but may expose sensitive technical metadata.

=== Class 1: descriptive team learning

Examples:

* queue analysis;
* review-workload distribution;
* CI bottleneck discovery;
* workflow retrospectives.

Results are intended for local improvement and should use appropriate aggregation.

=== Class 2: organizational decision support

Examples:

* tool procurement;
* platform investment;
* architecture prioritization;
* staffing-capacity analysis;
* portfolio allocation.

These uses require governed metrics, uncertainty, and decision records.

=== Class 3: policy influence

Examples:

* mandatory review rules;
* risk-based deployment gates;
* automated reviewer routing;
* access or approval policy;
* organization-wide tool defaults.

These uses require causal or strong operational evidence, guardrails, monitoring, and rollback.

=== Class 4: individual or employment consequence

Examples:

* performance evaluation;
* promotion;
* compensation;
* disciplinary action;
* termination;
* individual productivity ranking.

Engineering telemetry and derived metrics should be presumed unsuitable for these uses unless a separate formal process demonstrates exceptional validity, fairness, necessity, legal authority, transparency, and due process. In most organizations, these uses should be prohibited.

== Permitted uses

Subject to appropriate review and controls, permitted uses include:

* improving engineering-system reliability;
* identifying process bottlenecks;
* evaluating tools and interventions;
* estimating human attention and infrastructure cost;
* improving review, CI, deployment, and incident workflows;
* understanding architecture and dependency risk;
* supporting capacity and portfolio planning;
* measuring source and metric quality;
* conducting approved research;
* supporting practitioner-led retrospectives;
* auditing automated engineering actions.

Permission is tied to the declared purpose, not to broad access to the data.

== Prohibited uses

The following uses should be prohibited by default:

* ranking individual engineers by commit, pull-request, review, or ticket volume;
* inferring individual productivity from cycle time or visible activity;
* covert monitoring of work patterns outside an approved purpose;
* using exploratory metrics in compensation or disciplinary decisions;
* collecting keystrokes, screen activity, or message content without exceptional necessity and explicit approval;
* inferring health, disability, family status, political activity, protected characteristics, or private beliefs;
* penalizing people for correcting erroneous data or challenging a metric;
* using AI-generated assessments as sole evidence for an adverse employment decision;
* publishing identifiable comparisons without legitimate need;
* reusing data for a materially different purpose without review;
* allowing vendors to train external models on organizational evidence without explicit contractual permission;
* executing irreversible high-impact actions solely from an unvalidated model or metric.

== Restricted uses

Some uses are neither automatically permitted nor universally prohibited.

Examples include:

* individual coaching;
* expertise discovery;
* workload or burnout-risk indicators;
* staffing decisions;
* organizational redesign;
* fraud or insider-threat detection;
* compliance investigation;
* model-based risk gating.

These require heightened review, narrow access, evidence of necessity, minimized data, explicit due process, and ongoing monitoring.

== Purpose registration

Every nontrivial use should be registered.

Example:

[source,yaml]
----
use_id: use_review_routing_2026_03
name: Risk-based specialist review routing
owner: secure_development_platform
use_class: 3
purpose: Route high-risk Engineering Changes to qualified security reviewers while reducing unnecessary low-risk review load
population: production_application_repositories
data:
  - engineering_change_complexity
  - dependency_blast_radius
  - security_sensitive_paths
  - historical_severe_findings
metrics:
  - ei.review.specialist_wait_time@1.2.0
  - ei.quality.severe_security_escape_rate@1.0.0
decisions:
  - reviewer_assignment
prohibited_extensions:
  - engineer_performance_ranking
  - promotion_decisions
review_date: 2026-10-01
----

The registration should include purpose, owner, affected population, data, metrics, decisions, access, retention, risks, prohibited extensions, and expiration date.

== Approval criteria

A use may be approved only when:

* the decision purpose is clear;
* the proposed evidence is relevant;
* less intrusive alternatives were considered;
* source and metric quality are adequate;
* affected populations are defined;
* privacy and security controls are proportionate;
* prohibited interpretations are documented;
* an accountable owner exists;
* correction and appeal are available;
* monitoring and retirement criteria are defined.

High-risk uses additionally require evidence of validity, subgroup behavior, causal justification where relevant, and rollback capability.

== Data inventory

The platform should maintain an inventory of:

* source systems;
* event types;
* entity types;
* personal and organizational identifiers;
* sensitive technical information;
* generated model outputs;
* derived metrics;
* access roles;
* retention periods;
* approved uses;
* downstream consumers.

Unknown or undocumented data should not enter decision-grade products.

== Data classification

Recommended classifications include:

=== Public

Approved for external release.

=== Internal

General organizational use with low sensitivity.

=== Confidential

Business, organizational, or technical information requiring restricted access.

=== Restricted

Sensitive code, identity, security, incident, model, or individual-level evidence.

=== Highly restricted

Evidence whose exposure could create substantial security, privacy, employment, legal, or regulatory harm.

Classification should propagate to derived datasets unless de-identification or aggregation demonstrably reduces risk.

== Sensitive data

Sensitive categories may include:

* individual identity and activity history;
* private repository metadata;
* security findings;
* incident details;
* access credentials or secrets;
* customer or regulated data;
* source-code content;
* model prompts and retrieved context;
* organizational restructuring information;
* employment-related records;
* protected or inferred personal characteristics.

Sensitive data requires explicit purpose, restricted access, retention limits, and enhanced audit.

== Data minimization review

For each field, ask:

* Is it necessary for the approved purpose?
* Can it be replaced by a less detailed feature?
* Can it be aggregated earlier?
* Can identity be pseudonymized?
* Can content be replaced by metadata?
* Can the retention period be shortened?
* Can computation occur locally without centralizing raw evidence?

Fields without a defensible purpose should be removed.

== Identity handling

Canonical identity is useful for lineage and temporal ownership, but it creates risk.

Controls should include:

* separate storage for identity mappings;
* pseudonymous analytical identifiers;
* role-based re-identification;
* time-bounded access;
* access logging;
* minimum aggregation thresholds;
* prohibition on unnecessary individual display;
* deletion or correction workflows.

Identity resolution confidence should be visible when evidence is used.

== Aggregation policy

Aggregation can reduce risk but does not guarantee anonymity.

Policy should define:

* minimum group size;
* treatment of small teams;
* suppression of sparse dimensions;
* prevention of repeated-query reconstruction;
* time-window minimums;
* handling of unique roles;
* cross-filter restrictions.

Small-group suppression should consider whether values can be inferred from totals.

== Re-identification risk

A dataset may be nominally anonymous but easily re-identifiable through repository, timestamps, language, team, or rare events.

Risk review should evaluate:

* uniqueness;
* linkability with other systems;
* rare-event exposure;
* repeated releases;
* access to auxiliary data;
* insider knowledge.

De-identification claims should be tested rather than assumed.

== Access-control model

Access should be granted by role, purpose, data class, and time.

Recommended access layers include:

=== Raw source evidence

Limited to connector operators, data stewards, investigators, and approved researchers.

=== Canonical row-level evidence

Limited to platform, metric, and domain owners with a registered purpose.

=== Restricted analytical datasets

Available to approved analysts under purpose-bound access.

=== Team aggregates

Available to relevant practitioners and leaders, subject to minimum group size.

=== Enterprise aggregates

Available to broader decision makers when interpretation and uncertainty accompany the metric.

Access should expire automatically and require periodic recertification.

== Purpose-based access

A person’s organizational role alone should not grant access to every use.

Access requests should identify:

* registered use;
* required datasets;
* necessary dimensions;
* expected duration;
* output audience;
* export requirements;
* accountable sponsor.

Using data outside the approved purpose should be treated as a governance incident.

== Least privilege

Users and services should receive only the data and operations required.

Examples include:

* aggregated metrics instead of raw events;
* repository-limited access instead of enterprise-wide access;
* read-only views instead of export privileges;
* pseudonymous IDs instead of names;
* short-lived credentials;
* separate access for security-sensitive fields.

== Separation of duties

High-consequence systems should separate:

* metric definition from employment decision making;
* model development from approval;
* data administration from audit;
* experiment analysis from unilateral rollout authority;
* vendor evaluation from procurement approval;
* policy recommendation from production deployment.

Separation reduces conflicts of interest and hidden manipulation.

== Data retention

Retention should be based on purpose and risk rather than indefinite convenience.

A retention schedule should distinguish:

* immutable technical audit evidence;
* individual-level activity detail;
* raw message or content data;
* model prompts and outputs;
* experiment assignments;
* derived metrics;
* decision records;
* security and compliance evidence.

Detailed sensitive data should generally have shorter retention than governed aggregates and decision records.

== Deletion

Deletion workflows should support:

* legal or policy deletion;
* source-system deletion propagation;
* user correction requests;
* removal of expired data;
* vendor termination;
* experiment closure;
* decommissioned metrics.

Deletion should include replicas, caches, indexes, model-training sets, and exports where applicable.

Where complete deletion is impossible due to immutable audit requirements, policy should document the legal basis, access restrictions, and minimization approach.

== Export controls

Exports create copies outside central controls.

Policy should define:

* who may export;
* approved formats;
* encryption requirements;
* destination restrictions;
* expiration;
* watermarking or tagging;
* onward-sharing prohibition;
* deletion confirmation;
* audit logging.

Highly restricted evidence should remain within controlled analytical environments where possible.

== Vendor governance

Vendors processing Engineering Intelligence evidence should be assessed for:

* data-use rights;
* model-training restrictions;
* data residency;
* subprocessors;
* retention and deletion;
* incident notification;
* security controls;
* audit rights;
* model and service changes;
* output ownership;
* portability and exit.

Vendor effectiveness should be evaluated using organization-controlled evidence rather than vendor-reported usage alone.

== AI model governance

Introducing an AI-assisted use into Engineering Intelligence changes the substrate of the decision, not just its interface. The model's training data, prompt, retrieval context, and evaluation history become part of the analytical claim; a silent upgrade rewrites that substrate without informing anyone who relied on the previous behaviour. AI model governance therefore treats each configuration as a versioned artefact under the same registration and rollback disciplines applied to metrics and experiments.

AI-assisted Engineering Intelligence uses require registration of:

* provider;
* model and version;
* purpose;
* input fields;
* prompt and policy version;
* retrieved context;
* output use;
* human review requirements;
* known limitations;
* evaluation results;
* permitted populations;
* rollback and replacement plan.

A model upgrade is a treatment change and may require revalidation.

== Generated explanations

AI-generated summaries and recommendations should be labeled as generated inference.

They should not be presented as direct observation.

Important outputs should link to:

* source evidence;
* model and configuration;
* uncertainty;
* generated timestamp;
* human review status;
* corrections.

Generated narratives should never silently replace underlying incident, review, or decision records.

== Automated decisions

Automated or semi-automated actions should be classified by consequence and reversibility. The four tiers below are not arranged by sophistication of the automation but by how much of the decision can be reversed without harm. A well-tuned model operating at the wrong tier is more dangerous than a crude one operating at the right tier, because reviewers assume the safeguards match the actions.

=== Advisory

The system recommends; a human decides.

=== Confirm-before-action

The system prepares an action requiring explicit approval.

=== Bounded autonomous

The system acts within preapproved low-risk limits and records the action.

=== High-impact autonomous

The system executes consequential action without immediate approval.

High-impact autonomous use should require exceptional evidence, strict bounds, continuous monitoring, independent review, and rapid shutdown.

== Automation control requirements

Automated policy should include:

* explicit objective;
* eligible scope;
* evidence threshold;
* model and metric versions;
* confidence requirement;
* action limits;
* human override;
* appeal;
* audit trail;
* guardrails;
* stop and rollback rules;
* expiration or review date.

No automated policy should depend on a metric in unavailable or degraded quality state.

== Individual-level views

Individual-level evidence may be operationally necessary for debugging, review assignment, incident response, or personal self-reflection.

Controls should include:

* narrow declared purpose;
* access limited to the person, appropriate operator, or authorized investigator;
* prohibition on comparative ranking;
* visible data provenance;
* correction workflow;
* retention limits;
* audit of access.

An individual should generally be able to see the evidence attributed to them unless doing so would compromise a legitimate investigation or security control.

== Practitioner notice

Practitioners should receive clear notice describing:

* source systems used;
* categories of data collected;
* approved purposes;
* prohibited uses;
* aggregation practices;
* retention;
* who may access detail;
* correction and appeal;
* contact for governance concerns.

Notice should be understandable without requiring interpretation of a long legal policy.

== Consent

Employment settings complicate meaningful consent because participation may not be freely optional.

Organizations should not use nominal consent to justify disproportionate monitoring.

Where consent is appropriate, it should be specific, informed, revocable where feasible, and separated from mandatory operational collection.

For required collection, organizations should rely on necessity, proportionality, transparency, governance, and applicable legal authority rather than fictional voluntariness.

== Correction rights

Affected people and teams should be able to request correction of:

* identity attribution;
* team ownership;
* Engineering Change linkage;
* source-event interpretation;
* metric population inclusion;
* model classification;
* generated explanation;
* decision-record factual errors.

The system should preserve correction provenance and recompute affected outputs where material.

== Appeal process

High-consequence decisions should provide an appeal route.

An appeal should support:

* access to relevant evidence;
* explanation of metrics and models;
* opportunity to provide context;
* review by a person independent of the original decision;
* correction of data errors;
* documented resolution;
* protection from retaliation.

Appeal volume and outcomes should be monitored as governance signals.

== Fairness and subgroup review

Uses affecting populations should be evaluated across relevant groups and contexts.

Relevant dimensions may include:

* geography;
* organization;
* role;
* tenure band;
* language and technology stack;
* repository maturity;
* accessibility needs;
* work type;
* shift or time-zone constraints.

Protected characteristics should not be inferred casually. Fairness analysis involving sensitive attributes requires explicit legal and privacy review.

== Distributional effects

An intervention may improve the average while shifting burden to a smaller group.

Governance review should consider:

* who receives benefit;
* who bears validation work;
* who experiences false positives;
* who loses autonomy;
* who is excluded from treatment;
* who carries residual risk;
* whether scarce experts become more overloaded.

Distributional outcomes should accompany aggregate results.

== Metric-use review

Before a metric is used in a new context, verify:

* the purpose is compatible with its specification;
* the population is valid;
* the interpretation remains supported;
* quality gates are met;
* the use does not violate prohibited interpretations;
* aggregation and privacy controls are adequate;
* the metric version is current;
* behavioral effects are acceptable.

A governed metric is not universally reusable.

== Experiment governance

Experiments should receive review proportional to risk.

Review should cover:

* intervention consequence;
* assignment fairness;
* informed notice where appropriate;
* exposure logging;
* guardrails;
* stop rules;
* privacy;
* subgroup risk;
* rollback;
* post-study data retention.

Randomization does not excuse unsafe treatment.

== Research publication

External publication should review:

* confidentiality;
* intellectual property;
* re-identification;
* employee privacy;
* customer and regulated data;
* vendor restrictions;
* security exposure;
* reproducibility tradeoffs.

Organizations should share methods, schemas, synthetic data, and aggregate findings where raw evidence cannot be disclosed.

== Monitoring approved uses

Approved uses should be monitored for:

* data-quality degradation;
* population drift;
* metric-version changes;
* access anomalies;
* export activity;
* use outside declared purpose;
* behavioral gaming;
* subgroup harm;
* model drift;
* decision reversal or appeal;
* lack of continued value.

Monitoring should be tied to an owner and review cadence.

== Audit logging

Audit records should include:

* identity of user or service;
* purpose and registered use;
* datasets and fields accessed;
* query or action;
* timestamp;
* export destination;
* metric or model version;
* automated decision or override;
* approval and review status.

Audit logs themselves are sensitive and require protection.

== Periodic access review

Access should be recertified periodically.

Review should remove:

* inactive users;
* expired projects;
* overly broad roles;
* access inherited from previous teams;
* unused export permission;
* service accounts without owners;
* vendor access after contract change.

== Governance incident

A governance incident includes:

* unauthorized access;
* purpose violation;
* prohibited individual ranking;
* erroneous high-impact metric;
* undisclosed vendor reuse;
* re-identification;
* automated action outside bounds;
* missing audit evidence;
* failure to delete expired data;
* retaliation for correction or appeal;
* material subgroup harm.

== Incident response

Response should include:

. contain access or automated action;
. preserve evidence;
. identify affected data, people, decisions, and outputs;
. notify appropriate owners and reviewers;
. correct or suppress affected metrics and decisions;
. satisfy legal or contractual notification duties;
. review root causes and control failures;
. remediate systems and policy;
. communicate findings to affected populations where appropriate;
. track follow-up to closure.

== Metric and model rollback

The platform should support rapid rollback of:

* metric versions;
* model versions;
* prompts and policies;
* access grants;
* automated actions;
* dashboard publications;
* derived datasets;
* experiment treatments.

Rollback procedures should be tested before high-risk use.

== Misuse detection

Potential signals include:

* repeated queries targeting one person;
* unusually small group filters;
* exports of individual-level data;
* access outside registered use;
* attempts to join restricted identity data;
* dashboards embedded in performance-review workflows;
* automated actions exceeding policy limits;
* metric use after deprecation.

Detection should be balanced against legitimate privacy for analysts and investigators.

== Policy exception process

Exceptions should require:

* written justification;
* defined scope;
* accountable sponsor;
* legal, privacy, and security review;
* compensating controls;
* expiration date;
* monitoring;
* documented renewal or termination.

Permanent informal exceptions undermine the standard.

== Governance review cadence

=== Weekly operational review

Access anomalies, data incidents, automated-action failures, and active guardrail breaches.

=== Monthly use review

New uses, changed populations, metric changes, access requests, and exceptions.

=== Quarterly governance review

Retention, privacy, subgroup effects, model performance, appeals, and policy retirement.

=== Annual program review

Overall effectiveness, prohibited-use compliance, trust, incidents, organizational value, and changes in law or technology.

== Governance maturity levels

=== Level 0: implicit

Rules exist mainly as informal expectations.

=== Level 1: documented

Permitted purposes, access, and prohibited uses are written.

=== Level 2: operational

Use registration, review, access controls, retention, and correction are functioning.

=== Level 3: auditable

Lineage, purpose-based access, audit, incidents, and periodic recertification are measurable.

=== Level 4: adaptive

Governance responds continuously to metric, model, workflow, and population change.

=== Level 5: trustworthy autonomy

Bounded automated policy operates with strong evidence, monitoring, appeal, and rollback.

Maturity should be demonstrated through controls and outcomes, not policy documents alone.

== Governance anti-patterns

=== Collect first, justify later

Data becomes permanent before purpose and risk are considered.

=== Access by seniority

Leadership role is treated as a universal need to inspect individual detail.

=== Aggregate means anonymous

Small-group and auxiliary-data re-identification are ignored.

=== Metric governance without use governance

A valid metric is reused for an invalid purpose.

=== Consent theater

Employees are asked to agree to monitoring they cannot realistically refuse.

=== AI explanation as due process

A generated rationale substitutes for access to evidence and independent review.

=== Permanent pilot

Experimental access and data collection continue after the study ends.

=== Policy without enforcement

Prohibited uses are documented but not detectable or sanctionable.

=== Governance as delay only

Review adds bureaucracy without improving evidence quality, safety, or accountability.

== Standard implementation checklist

. Establish accountable governance roles and a review board.
. Classify uses by consequence and sensitivity.
. Register purpose, population, data, metrics, owner, and expiration.
. Publish permitted, restricted, and prohibited uses.
. Maintain data inventory, classification, lineage, and retention schedules.
. Apply purpose-based least-privilege access and separation of duties.
. Minimize identity detail and enforce aggregation thresholds.
. Provide practitioner notice, correction, and appeal.
. Govern vendors, models, prompts, and automated policy versions.
. Monitor access, exports, drift, misuse, subgroup effects, and appeals.
. Maintain incident response and tested rollback procedures.
. Recertify access and approved uses on a declared cadence.
. Retire data, metrics, models, and uses that no longer justify their risk.
. Judge governance by preserved trust and safe decision quality, not review volume.
