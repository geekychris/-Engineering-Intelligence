= Economic Evaluation and Business Case Standard

[abstract]
This appendix defines a rigorous method for evaluating the economic value of engineering tools, platforms, architecture investments, workflow changes, and AI-assisted development systems. It distinguishes accounting savings from released capacity, modeled value from realized value, and local efficiency from enterprise outcomes. The standard is designed to prevent inflated return-on-investment claims while still enabling organizations to make timely decisions under uncertainty.

== Purpose

Engineering investments are often justified using incomplete arithmetic.

Common claims include:

* engineers save several hours each week;
* the tool pays for itself if productivity improves by one percent;
* automation replaces a fixed amount of labor;
* faster delivery automatically produces revenue;
* avoided incidents equal historical incident cost;
* generated code volume represents productive output.

These claims may be directionally useful, but they are not decision-grade until assumptions, mechanisms, opportunity costs, uncertainty, and realization conditions are explicit.

This standard supports decisions such as:

* purchase or renew a vendor tool;
* build an internal platform;
* expand an AI assistant;
* increase CI capacity;
* fund an architecture migration;
* change review policy;
* retire a legacy system;
* allocate investment across engineering portfolios.

== Core economic model

The economic value of an intervention is the difference between outcomes under the intervention and outcomes under the credible alternative.

[stem]
++++
NetValue = IncrementalBenefits - IncrementalCosts
++++

The comparison is not against zero. It is against the best realistic counterfactual, such as continuing the current tool, building internally, choosing another vendor, delaying investment, or stopping the activity.

== Economic evaluation principles

=== Compare alternatives

Every business case should evaluate at least:

* proposed intervention;
* current state;
* credible alternative;
* defer or do-nothing option.

=== Count net effects

Savings in one area may create cost elsewhere. AI generation may reduce authoring effort while increasing validation, infrastructure, security, and maintenance costs.

=== Distinguish capacity from cash

Time released does not become financial savings unless the organization changes spending, avoids hiring, delivers additional valuable work, or reduces delay.

=== Value outcomes, not activity

More code, reviews, deployments, or suggestions are not automatically benefits.

=== Include uncertainty

Business cases should use ranges, scenarios, and sensitivity analysis rather than a single precise forecast.

=== Track realization

An approved business case is a hypothesis. Actual costs, adoption, capacity use, outcomes, and benefit realization must be measured after implementation.

=== Include distribution

Benefits and costs may fall on different teams, roles, business units, or time horizons.

== Business-case identity

Each evaluation should contain:

* business-case identifier;
* intervention and version;
* decision owner;
* economic owner;
* study or evidence references;
* eligible population;
* comparison alternatives;
* evaluation horizon;
* currency and price basis;
* discount rate where relevant;
* approval and review dates.

Example:

[source,yaml]
----
business_case_id: bc_ai_review_2026_02
intervention: enterprise_ai_code_review
version: 1.0
decision_owner: vp_engineering_platform
economic_owner: finance_business_partner
population: 8000_engineers_and_eligible_repositories
alternatives:
  - current_review_tool
  - internal_build
  - no_enterprise_standard
horizon_years: 3
currency: USD
price_basis_year: 2026
review_date: 2027-01-15
----

== Define the counterfactual

The business case should describe what happens without the investment.

A credible counterfactual includes:

* current operating cost;
* expected price changes;
* current adoption and effectiveness;
* planned hiring or attrition;
* likely architecture and volume growth;
* known reliability or security risk;
* replacement needs;
* costs already committed;
* other interventions likely to occur.

Using a static current state as the counterfactual can overstate value when the organization would have improved anyway.

== Cost taxonomy

=== Acquisition cost

Includes licenses, subscriptions, usage charges, committed spend, minimums, support tiers, and taxes.

=== Implementation cost

Includes integration, security review, procurement, migration, configuration, testing, enablement, and rollout labor.

=== Operating cost

Includes infrastructure, inference, storage, networking, observability, support, administration, model evaluation, and governance.

=== Human attention cost

Includes training, validation, review, prompt or policy maintenance, troubleshooting, workflow interruption, and exception handling.

=== Switching cost

Includes contract exit, data migration, retraining, workflow changes, decommissioning, temporary duplication, and productivity loss during transition.

=== Risk cost

Includes expected defects, incidents, security exposure, regulatory failure, vendor dependency, data leakage, and operational concentration.

=== Opportunity cost

Includes valuable work displaced by implementation, migration, or ongoing administration.

=== Residual and terminal cost

Includes decommissioning, archival, contract termination, retained infrastructure, and knowledge-transfer obligations at the end of the horizon.

== Benefit taxonomy

=== Direct cash savings

Examples include:

* lower vendor spend;
* reduced infrastructure bills;
* contract consolidation;
* lower support or outsourcing cost;
* eliminated duplicated systems.

These are the strongest benefits when they produce an observable budget change.

=== Avoided cost

Examples include:

* hiring avoided because capacity meets growth;
* future infrastructure expansion avoided;
* renewal price increase avoided;
* migration or compliance cost avoided.

Avoided cost requires evidence that the expenditure would otherwise have occurred.

=== Released capacity

Time or attention becomes available for other work.

Released capacity is valuable, but it is not identical to cash savings.

=== Throughput value

The organization delivers additional valuable outcomes with the same resources.

This requires evidence that released capacity reaches constrained, valuable work and that downstream systems can absorb it.

=== Cost-of-delay reduction

Earlier delivery may produce value through earlier revenue, risk reduction, customer retention, regulatory compliance, or learning.

=== Quality and reliability value

Includes fewer defects, incidents, rollbacks, support cases, security failures, and emergency interruptions.

=== Risk reduction

Represents lower probability or consequence of adverse outcomes.

=== Option value

An investment may create future choices, such as easier vendor switching, faster experimentation, reusable platform capability, or access to new markets.

=== Capability value

Includes improved expertise, operational resilience, architecture understanding, and ability to act without external dependency.

Capability value should be described carefully and not converted to cash without a defensible model.

== Labor cost model

A fully loaded labor rate may include:

* salary;
* bonus;
* benefits;
* payroll taxes;
* facilities and equipment;
* management and shared services;
* contractor premiums.

However, multiplying minutes saved by a loaded hourly rate usually estimates capacity value, not cash savings.

[stem]
++++
ReleasedCapacityValue = HoursReleased * LoadedHourlyRate * RealizationFactor
++++

The realization factor reflects how much released time becomes valuable productive capacity.

== Capacity realization

Released time creates value only when several conditions hold:

. the time is truly released rather than shifted;
. the time appears in usable blocks;
. engineers recognize and can redirect it;
. there is valuable queued work;
. downstream review, testing, product, and release systems can absorb it;
. incentives do not cause the time to disappear into additional low-value activity.

A realization factor should be explicit and sensitivity-tested.

Example scenarios:

* conservative: 20 percent realized;
* expected: 45 percent realized;
* optimistic: 70 percent realized.

These are assumptions, not universal defaults.

== Fragmentation discount

Five minutes saved across many tasks may be less useful than one uninterrupted hour.

A fragmentation discount can reflect whether released capacity is actionable.

[stem]
++++
UsableCapacity = GrossHoursReleased * ContinuityFactor
++++

The continuity factor may depend on task batching, workflow design, interruption, and local control over scheduling.

== Bottleneck adjustment

Improving a non-bottleneck may not increase system throughput.

For example, faster code generation may produce no delivery benefit when specialist review or test environments remain constrained.

Economic models should identify:

* current bottleneck;
* expected bottleneck after intervention;
* capacity of adjacent stages;
* queue and utilization effects;
* possible congestion created by higher upstream output.

== Queueing effects

Small reductions in service time can produce disproportionate waiting-time benefits near high utilization.

Conversely, a modest increase in work arrival rate can destabilize a queue.

Business cases should consider:

* arrival rate;
* service rate;
* variability;
* utilization;
* queue discipline;
* shared specialists;
* peak periods.

This is especially important for review, CI, test environments, security approval, and release systems.

== Cost of delay

Cost of delay estimates the value lost while an outcome is unavailable.

Possible components include:

* deferred revenue;
* delayed customer learning;
* continued operational cost;
* extended security or compliance exposure;
* customer dissatisfaction;
* competitive disadvantage;
* delayed retirement of legacy systems.

The model must state whether earlier technical completion actually leads to earlier release and benefit.

== Quality economics

Quality value should include frequency and consequence.

[stem]
++++
ExpectedLoss = sum_i Probability_i * Consequence_i
++++

Consequences may include:

* engineering response effort;
* customer support;
* service credits;
* revenue loss;
* data remediation;
* regulatory penalties;
* reputation damage;
* future prevention work;
* interruption of planned work.

Historical incident averages may be misleading when severity has a heavy-tailed distribution.

== Risk-adjusted value

For uncertain outcomes, use expected values and scenarios.

Example:

[source,yaml]
----
risk_reduction:
  event: severe_change_related_incident
  baseline_annual_probability: 0.12
  treated_annual_probability: 0.08
  consequence_range_usd:
    low: 500000
    expected: 2500000
    high: 15000000
----

Rare catastrophic outcomes should also be considered through risk tolerance, not expected value alone.

== AI tool economics

AI systems require a complete net-value model.

Costs may include:

* per-seat license;
* inference and token usage;
* premium models;
* retrieval and indexing;
* human validation;
* false-positive handling;
* generated-code maintenance;
* security review;
* model evaluation;
* vendor integration;
* policy and governance;
* incident risk;
* fallback capability.

Benefits may include:

* reduced routine authoring effort;
* earlier defect detection;
* improved test coverage;
* faster information retrieval;
* reduced review burden;
* reduced queue delay;
* increased accessibility to unfamiliar systems;
* faster experimentation.

Usage or acceptance rate alone is insufficient for valuation.

== Validation burden

AI-produced output creates validation work.

[stem]
++++
NetAttentionChange = AuthoringAttentionReduced - ValidationAttentionAdded - ReworkAttentionAdded
++++

Validation burden should include:

* reviewing suggestions;
* checking correctness;
* resolving false positives;
* understanding generated code;
* investigating subtle failures;
* correcting style or architecture drift;
* security and compliance checks.

A tool can have high adoption and negative net attention value.

== Vendor pricing models

Compare pricing under realistic scale and usage.

Common models include:

* per seat;
* per active user;
* per request or review;
* token or compute usage;
* repository tier;
* enterprise flat fee;
* committed capacity;
* outcome-based pricing.

The evaluation should model:

* growth;
* unused seats;
* usage concentration;
* price escalation;
* minimum commitments;
* overages;
* premium-feature dependency;
* negotiation and renewal risk.

== Build versus buy

A build-versus-buy analysis should compare:

* time to usable capability;
* product and engineering labor;
* infrastructure;
* support burden;
* model and feature evolution;
* security and compliance;
* integration depth;
* customization;
* vendor concentration;
* switching cost;
* opportunity cost;
* expected lifetime.

Internal build cost should include ongoing product ownership, not only initial engineering effort.

== Architecture investment economics

Architecture investments often produce indirect, delayed, and distributed value.

Potential benefits include:

* lower coordination cost;
* reduced change blast radius;
* fewer incidents;
* easier testing;
* faster onboarding;
* independent deployment;
* reduced vendor dependency;
* easier regulatory separation;
* greater option value.

The evaluation should link architectural mechanisms to observable delivery, quality, and operating outcomes.

== Platform economics

A platform creates shared capability and may exhibit scale economies.

Model:

* fixed platform cost;
* marginal cost per tenant or workload;
* adoption curve;
* avoided duplicated investment;
* local integration cost;
* support and reliability obligations;
* migration cost;
* value concentration among heavy users;
* cost of forced standardization.

A platform is not valuable merely because many teams use it.

== Allocation of shared costs

Shared costs should be transparent even when not charged back.

Possible allocation methods include:

* equal allocation;
* usage-based allocation;
* active-user allocation;
* transaction allocation;
* capacity allocation;
* business-value allocation;
* central strategic funding.

The chosen method can change behavior and should not be mistaken for the underlying economic cost.

== Sunk costs

Past spending that cannot be recovered should not determine the forward decision.

However, switching costs, remaining contract obligations, migration effort, and residual value are relevant.

The analysis should distinguish sunk cost from avoidable future cost.

== Evaluation horizon

Choose a horizon appropriate to the intervention.

Examples:

* workflow configuration: 6 to 18 months;
* vendor platform: 2 to 4 years;
* architecture migration: 3 to 7 years;
* foundational platform: longer, with staged decision gates.

Long horizons increase uncertainty and require scenario analysis.

== Discounting

Future costs and benefits may be discounted.

[stem]
++++
NPV = sum_(t=0)^T (Benefit_t - Cost_t) / (1+r)^t
++++

Where stem:[r] is the discount rate and stem:[t] is the time period.

The business case should disclose the rate and whether values are nominal or real.

== Standard financial outputs

Useful outputs include:

* net present value;
* internal rate of return where meaningful;
* payback period;
* annualized cost;
* cost per eligible Engineering Change;
* cost per active user;
* cost per accepted outcome;
* expected value;
* break-even adoption;
* break-even effect size.

No one output should replace the underlying assumptions.

== Break-even analysis

Break-even analysis is often more useful than a single forecast.

Examples:

* How many review minutes must be saved per change to cover the vendor cost?
* What adoption level is required?
* How much incident probability reduction is required?
* How many hires must be avoided?
* What inference price makes the tool uneconomic?

[stem]
++++
BreakEvenMinutesPerChange = AnnualIncrementalCost / (EligibleChanges * LoadedCostPerMinute * RealizationFactor)
++++

== Scenario analysis

At minimum, provide conservative, expected, and optimistic scenarios.

Scenarios should vary multiple coherent assumptions rather than changing only one number.

Example dimensions include:

* adoption;
* effect size;
* realization;
* price;
* eligible volume;
* quality effect;
* migration duration;
* staffing growth;
* vendor discount.

== Sensitivity analysis

Identify which assumptions most affect the decision.

Common high-sensitivity variables include:

* human time saved;
* realization factor;
* vendor price;
* inference usage;
* outcome attribution;
* incident consequence;
* adoption;
* time to rollout;
* useful life.

A business case dominated by one weak assumption should be treated cautiously.

== Monte Carlo analysis

For large or uncertain investments, model assumptions as distributions rather than fixed values.

Outputs may include:

* probability of positive NPV;
* expected NPV;
* downside percentile;
* probability of payback within horizon;
* major value drivers.

Distribution choices and correlations must be documented.

== Evidence strength

Each economic input should be labeled by evidence strength.

Suggested levels:

* observed accounting fact;
* measured operational fact;
* randomized causal estimate;
* credible quasi-experimental estimate;
* calibrated model estimate;
* expert assumption;
* vendor claim;
* placeholder.

A business case should not present all inputs as equally certain.

== Attribution

Benefits should be attributed only to the incremental effect of the intervention.

Avoid double-counting when several programs influence the same outcome.

Possible methods include:

* randomized experiment;
* phased rollout;
* difference-in-differences;
* contribution model;
* explicit shared attribution;
* conservative residual attribution.

== Double-counting controls

Common double-counting includes:

* counting time savings and the same increased throughput separately;
* counting avoided hiring and labor savings for the same capacity;
* counting fewer incidents and recovered engineering time separately without adjustment;
* counting revenue acceleration and total revenue;
* assigning the full outcome to multiple tools.

The business case should include a benefit-dependency map.

== Capacity, savings, and value labels

Every benefit should use one of these labels:

=== Realized cash impact

An observed budget, spend, or revenue change.

=== Contracted or committed impact

A signed or formally approved future financial change.

=== Avoided planned cost

A documented expenditure no longer required.

=== Released capacity

Measured time or attention available for other work.

=== Modeled outcome value

Estimated value based on causal or predictive assumptions.

=== Strategic or option value

A qualitative or scenario-based future capability.

These labels prevent modeled capacity from being reported as booked savings.

== Value-realization plan

Before approval, define how benefits will become real.

For released capacity, specify:

* which bottleneck is relieved;
* which work will absorb capacity;
* who owns reallocation;
* how utilization will be observed;
* what downstream constraints must change;
* when realization will be reviewed.

For cash savings, specify:

* contract action;
* budget owner;
* timing;
* accounting treatment;
* dependency on migration or decommissioning.

== Realization ledger

Track forecast and actual value over time.

Example:

[source,yaml]
----
benefit_id: benefit_review_capacity
business_case_id: bc_ai_review_2026_02
benefit_type: released_capacity
forecast_annual_value_usd: 4200000
forecast_hours: 28000
realization_factor: 0.45
actual_to_date:
  measured_hours_released: 9100
  verified_reallocated_hours: 3400
  booked_cash_savings_usd: 0
  avoided_hiring_usd: 650000
confidence: medium
owner: developer_productivity
review_date: 2027-01-15
----

== Post-implementation review

Review actual performance against the case.

Measure:

* implementation cost;
* operating cost;
* adoption and eligible coverage;
* treatment fidelity;
* measured effect;
* validation burden;
* quality outcomes;
* capacity realization;
* cash or avoided cost;
* unanticipated costs;
* vendor and switching risk;
* continued strategic relevance.

The review should support expand, modify, renegotiate, replace, or retire decisions.

== Renewal economics

Renewal should not rely on the original purchase case.

Reevaluate:

* current alternatives;
* actual usage;
* causal or outcome evidence;
* realized value;
* price changes;
* switching cost;
* market evolution;
* model or product changes;
* concentration risk;
* contract flexibility.

A successful initial rollout does not imply an indefinitely favorable renewal.

== Portfolio allocation

Compare investments using more than nominal ROI.

Portfolio decisions should consider:

* expected value;
* downside risk;
* strategic alignment;
* time to value;
* reversibility;
* evidence strength;
* dependency on scarce expertise;
* option value;
* correlation with other investments;
* organizational capacity to execute.

A smaller high-confidence investment may be preferable to a larger speculative return.

== Decision thresholds

Possible economic decision rules include:

* positive expected NPV with downside limit;
* payback within a specified horizon;
* probability of positive value above threshold;
* cost per outcome below an alternative;
* noninferior quality at lower cost;
* strategic capability under a maximum affordable loss.

Thresholds should reflect organizational risk tolerance and reversibility.

----

== Business-case maturity levels

=== Level 0: narrative

Benefits and costs are described qualitatively.

=== Level 1: estimated

Major cost and benefit categories are quantified with visible assumptions.

=== Level 2: evidence-linked

Inputs are connected to operational data, studies, contracts, and metric versions.

=== Level 3: decision-grade

Alternatives, uncertainty, scenarios, break-even, risks, and realization are complete.

=== Level 4: realized

Actual costs and benefits are tracked after implementation.

=== Level 5: adaptive portfolio evidence

Realization history improves future forecasts and portfolio allocation.

== Anti-patterns

=== Salary multiplication as savings

Minutes saved are multiplied by salary and reported as cash savings without a realization mechanism.

=== Vendor usage as value

High invocation or acceptance is treated as proof of economic benefit.

=== Gross benefit without validation cost

Automation savings omit the human work required to trust the output.

=== Static counterfactual

The current state is assumed never to change.

=== Best-case-only forecast

Adoption, effect size, and price all use optimistic assumptions.

=== Double-counted benefit

Time savings, throughput, avoided hiring, and revenue are all claimed from the same effect.

=== Ignored bottleneck

Local efficiency is assumed to improve end-to-end outcomes.

=== Ignored migration and exit

Implementation and switching costs are omitted.

=== Modeled value reported as realized savings

Forecast capacity is presented as booked financial impact.

=== No post-implementation review

The original business case is never tested against reality.

== Economic evaluation checklist

. Define the intervention, decision, horizon, owner, and credible alternatives.
. Model the changing counterfactual rather than a frozen current state.
. Include acquisition, implementation, operating, human, switching, risk, and opportunity costs.
. Separate cash savings, avoided cost, released capacity, outcome value, and option value.
. Measure validation burden and downstream work created by automation.
. Identify system bottlenecks and queueing effects.
. State realization and fragmentation assumptions for released capacity.
. Link quality and risk value to probability and consequence.
. Use scenarios, sensitivity, break-even, and uncertainty analysis.
. Label every major input by evidence strength.
. Prevent double-counting with a benefit-dependency map.
. Define a concrete value-realization plan before approval.
. Track forecast versus actual value in a realization ledger.
. Reevaluate economics at renewal, expansion, and material treatment change.
. Report modeled value honestly and reserve “savings” for realized or committed financial impact.
