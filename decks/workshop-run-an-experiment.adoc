== Learning outcomes

By the end of the ninety minutes each team will:

* *Apply a playbook* — AI reviewer, merge queue, PR size, CI acceleration, review routing — to a real intervention on your own team
* *Write a pre-registration* — primary outcome, guardrails, MDE, stopping rule, interpretation table
* *Name and avoid the top failure modes* — wrong unit, missing exposure, interference, novelty, seasonality

[.lead]
You will leave with a signed document you could actually launch.

// ============================================================================

== What this workshop is _not_

This is not a causal-inference lecture. We are not teaching DiD, IV, or synthetic controls today.

We are teaching how to *drive one experiment end-to-end from a playbook* — the operational document, not the estimator.

If you need the inference technique, the sibling workshop covers it.

// ============================================================================

== The problem

A director walks in. "We're rolling out the AI reviewer to all 180 backend teams next month. Can you *A/B test* it?"

You nod. You should not have nodded.

* *Assignment unit?* Reviewers are shared across teams
* *Compliance?* Teams may install but never open the findings
* *Interference?* One team's PR is another team's review
* *Guardrails?* What breaks if the tool is wrong
* *Decision rule?* At what effect size do we _adopt_

[quote]
"Let's just A/B test it" is not a design. It is a wish.

== Segment 1 — pick and hypothesise (15 min)

*Task.*  Choose a playbook. Choose an intervention on _your_ team.

Write, on one sheet:

. *Decision* — what commits or refuses on the basis of this experiment
. *Treatment* — the exact versioned thing
. *Control* — the exact status-quo it replaces
. *Hypothesis* — direction and mechanism, in one sentence

_Rule:_ if the decision does not commit the org to _adopt / revise / reject_, pick a different intervention.

_Report back:_ the decision sentence.

// ============================================================================

== Assignment vs analysis unit

* *Assignment unit* — receives the treatment (team, repository, engineer, time block)
* *Analysis unit* — contributes an outcome (Engineering Change, PR, service)

They can differ. The mismatch is where most designs die.

[.lead]
Pull-request assignment breaks when reviewers are shared. Team-level assignment costs power. *Engineering Change is usually the right analysis unit for workflow interventions.*

// ============================================================================

== Exposure is not assignment

Assignment is _policy_. Exposure is _use_.

* Team A is assigned the AI reviewer
* Only 71% of authors open a finding on any given PR
* Reporting the average outcome across Team A confuses _mandating_ the tool with _using_ it

Instrument both. The gap between them is the story.

[stem]
++++
\widehat{\tau}_{ITT} \neq \widehat{\tau}_{\text{on the compliers}}
++++

// ============================================================================

== Sample size and MDE

The _minimum detectable effect_ decides whether the experiment can possibly answer your question.

For a two-sample z-test on means:

[stem]
++++
n = 2 \left( \frac{(z_{\alpha/2} + z_\beta) \sigma}{\delta} \right)^2
++++

Work backwards from the _smallest effect worth acting on_:

* effort improvement < 5% → probably not worth vendor cost
* < 3% → within noise of a Monday morning

[.lead]
If your throughput can't reach _n_ in a quarter, redesign.

// ============================================================================

== Guardrails

Guardrails are the outcomes that must _not_ get worse.

They exist because a well-optimised primary outcome can silently break the system.

* Merge queue: queue age, urgent-change latency, CI cost
* AI reviewer: escaped defects, security findings, cognitive reload, trust calibration
* CI acceleration: flaky-test rate, cache correctness, missed defects
* Autonomous agent: unrelated-file mods, license violations, escalation rate

_Every playbook names its guardrails._ Copy that list. Do not shorten it.

// ============================================================================

== Segment 2 — design (25 min)

For your chosen intervention, write:

. *Assignment unit* — and _why_ that unit (interference, shared reviewers, shared infra)
. *Analysis unit* — usually Engineering Change; justify if different
. *Exposure instrumentation* — how you will know it was actually used
. *Eligibility and exclusions* — who is in, who is out, why
. *Sample size / MDE* — declare latexmath:[$\sigma$], the smallest useful effect latexmath:[$\delta$], compute _n_
. *Duration* — _n_ divided by your real throughput

_Report back:_ the assignment unit, and the compliance rate you are willing to tolerate before the ITT number becomes uninterpretable.

// ============================================================================

== Pre-registration — what goes in

* *Primary outcome* — one, quality-adjusted, at the Engineering Change level
* *Guardrail outcomes* — the ones the playbook names
* *Analysis plan* — estimator, adjustment variables, subgroups
* *Stopping rule* — how you handle interim looks
* *Expected effect direction* — sign, rough magnitude, mechanism
* *Interpretation table* — what each outcome pattern _means_

[quote]
Pre-analysis plans belong before data, not in the discussion section.

// ============================================================================

== The interpretation table

Decide, _before_ launching, what every outcome means. Four cells:

[cols="1,3,3"]
|===
| Outcome | Reading | Action

| *Win*
| Primary improves ≥ MDE, guardrails hold
| Adopt; roll out; register decision record

| *Null*
| Primary within ± MDE of zero, guardrails hold
| Reject at current cost; revisit at price change or model refresh

| *Regression*
| Primary worse, or a guardrail breached
| Reject; investigate mechanism; do not retry the same design

| *Can't tell*
| CI wider than MDE, or exposure < threshold
| Extend, redesign, or abandon — do _not_ report as null
|===

// ============================================================================

== Stopping rules and peeking

Engineering experiments run continuously. Teams peek.

Repeated naive tests at latexmath:[$\alpha = 0.05$] inflate false positives fast (1 look 5%, 5 looks ~14%, 10 looks ~19%).

Pick one, pre-declare, do not deviate:

* *Fixed horizon* — commit to _n_; do not analyse before
* *Group-sequential (O'Brien-Fleming)* — strict early, conventional late
* *Always-valid (mSPRT / e-value)* — safe under continuous monitoring

// ============================================================================

== Segment 3 — write the pre-reg (25 min)

Draft, on one sheet:

. *Primary outcome* — expression and window
. *Guardrails* — from the playbook, verbatim
. *Analysis plan* — estimator, adjustment, ITT + secondary CACE
. *Stopping rule* — fixed / OBF / always-valid, with the numbers
. *Expected direction* — sign and rough magnitude
. *Interpretation table* — the four cells above, filled in

_Sign it._ Names on the sheet. That signature is the pre-registration.

_Report back:_ read your interpretation table aloud. The room can push back on ambiguous cells.

// ============================================================================

== The failure modes

* *Wrong unit* — PR metrics for Engineering-Change-level effects
* *Multiple mechanisms* — new model + prompt + UI + policy shipped together
* *Missing exposure* — assignment recorded, actual use unknown
* *Short window* — speed measured; quality and learning still to arrive
* *Interference* — treated and control units affect each other (shared reviewers, shared CI capacity)
* *Novelty* — the effect is being new, not being better
* *Seasonality* — the effect is a release freeze
* *No decision threshold* — significance without practical significance

// ============================================================================

== External factors

Scope to affected entities and time windows, not global notes.

[cols="1,1"]
|===
| release and planning cycles | reorganizations
| holidays and staffing       | new tools or model releases
| incidents and outages       | repository migrations
| policy and compliance       | vendor or platform failures
|===

Every one of these has ended a well-designed experiment. Record them from day one.

// ============================================================================

== Segment 4 — red-team (15 min)

Swap pre-registrations with the next team.

For _their_ design, break it. Look for:

* *Interference* — their assignment unit is porous
* *Novelty* — the effect will decay in six weeks
* *Seasonality* — the pilot window is not representative
* *Non-random assignment* — who volunteered, who was chosen
* *Missing exposure* — assignment ≠ use
* *Ambiguous interpretation cell* — the "can't tell" quadrant is not defined

Return the sheet with a red-line list.

_Rule:_ the receiving team must _address_ each red-line — accept, mitigate, or defend — before the pre-reg is final.

// ============================================================================

== Pre-experiment readiness

Do not launch until:

. treatment and control are technically stable
. assignment and exposure are instrumented
. primary outcomes and guardrails are available
. baseline variance and power are understood
. security, legal, and ethical reviews are complete
. stopping and rollback rules are defined
. concurrent interventions are registered

[.lead]
The cost of a delayed launch is almost always smaller than the cost of a study that has to be redone.

// ============================================================================

== Worked example — the AI-review pre-reg

180 backend teams. Vendor _V_, model _M_ @ 2024-08, prompt _P_ rev 12. Assign by team; analyse by Engineering Change. Stratified by baseline review latency and language.

Baseline latexmath:[$\sigma = 42$] minutes per change; MDE = 8%.

[stem]
++++
n = 2 \times \left(\frac{(1.96 + 0.84)\sigma}{0.08 \bar{Y}}\right)^2 \approx 1{,}950\ \text{changes per arm}
++++

*Result:* −6.4% effort (CI 2.1%–10.7%); escapes unchanged.

Below the pre-declared 8% MDE. Real, but too small at current licensing cost. Interpretation-table cell: _Can't tell → revisit at next model refresh._

// ============================================================================

== Post-experiment decision record

Whatever the outcome, write:

* estimated causal effects and intervals
* practical significance vs. pre-declared threshold
* guardrail status
* heterogeneous effects
* external-factor sensitivity
* economic interpretation and limitations
* *adopt · revise · reject · extend*
* rollout and monitoring plan

[.lead]
The output of a good experiment is organizational knowledge — even when the intervention fails.

// ============================================================================

== What you go home with

* A *signed pre-registration* for one experiment you can actually run
* A *red-line list* from a peer team, with each item addressed
* A *decision the pre-reg commits you to*, per interpretation cell
* A *launch date* — or a written reason the design is not yet ready
