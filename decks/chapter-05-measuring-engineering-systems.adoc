*Chapter 5 — Measuring Engineering Systems*

_From telemetry to measurement: constructs, governance, and the discipline of a decision-relevant metric._

[.authors]
Christopher J. Collins  ·  Chapter Overview

// ============================================================================

== Telemetry is not measurement

Engineering organizations produce abundant telemetry. Most of it never changes a decision.

* A dashboard that cannot change a decision is descriptive at best, distracting at worst
* Every metric should trace to a *stakeholder · action · threshold* triad
* Without that triad you have logs, not evidence

[.lead]
The purpose of measurement is to improve a decision.

// ============================================================================

== The decision questions

Engineering Intelligence begins every metric with a question:

* Should review capacity be added?
* Is CI the dominant constraint?
* Is an AI reviewer reducing total human effort?
* Are changes becoming riskier after a workflow change?
* Which repository boundaries create avoidable coordination cost?

A metric that cannot be traced to a question is telemetry, not measurement.

// ============================================================================

== The measurement hierarchy

image::figures/mermaid/measurement-hierarchy.png[The measurement hierarchy]

. *Events* — pull-request created, review submitted, CI completed
. *Observations* — normalized facts (time to first review, failed checks)
. *Features* — dependency depth, ownership boundaries, complexity
. *Metrics* — summarized quantities that answer a question
. *Models* — relationships to explain, predict, or counterfactually estimate
. *Decisions* — actions taken because of the evidence

A commit count is an observation. It becomes a metric only when attached to a construct and a decision.

// ============================================================================

== Properties of a good metric

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Decision relevance | Informs a specific choice
| Construct validity | Measures what it claims
| Reliability        | Repeats under equivalent conditions
| Sensitivity        | Moves when the construct moves
| Robustness         | Resists gaming and workflow drift
| Explainability     | Users can drill to component evidence
| Stability          | Noise is quantified with intervals
| Collection cost    | Instrumentation earns its keep
|===

Miss any of these and the metric is unfit for load-bearing decisions.

// ============================================================================

== The metric taxonomy

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| *Flow*         | lead time, active time, queue time, review latency, WIP
| *Quality*      | escaped defects, change-failure rate, rollback, rework
| *Economic*     | attention cost, AI cost, coordination cost, cost of delay
| *Human*        | context recovery, cognitive inventory, review burden
| *AI*           | precision, recall, duplication, offload, reload
| *Architecture* | dependency depth, fan-out, blast radius, ownership
|===

[.lead]
No category should be optimized independently. Faster flow with worse quality is not an improvement.

// ============================================================================

== Direct vs derived metrics

*Direct* — computed closely from events: build duration, time to first review, deployment attempts.

*Derived* — depends on a model: cognitive burden, expected cost of delay, blast-radius score, Engineering Change complexity.

Derived metrics are often more decision-relevant, but require:

* published definitions and model version
* training data and calibration
* confidence intervals and known limitations
* validation evidence and recomputation history

Without this scaffolding a derived metric is a black box that quietly changes meaning.

// ============================================================================

== Leading, lagging, local, system

Leading indicators change *before* the outcome:

* review queue age, CI instability, dependency growth

Lagging indicators confirm *realized* outcomes:

* incidents, escaped defects, customer impact

Local optimization can harm the system. Fewer CI tests cut compute — and raise rework. Lighter reviews shift burden to authors.

[.lead]
A strong scorecard pairs leading with lagging, and local with system-level.

// ============================================================================

== Composite metrics with visible parts

A Review Friction Index combines normalized queue age, response cycles, unresolved comments, context-recovery events, and verification effort:

[stem]
++++
RFI(e) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k z_k(e)
++++

* stem:[z_k] are standardized components
* stem:[w_k] are calibrated — not chosen to make the answer look plausible

Users must be able to inspect component values. A composite that hides its parts is a slogan.

// ============================================================================

== Guardrails on every optimization

Every optimization metric needs guardrails.

An AI-reviewer experiment may target lower human review effort while guarding against:

* severe defect increase
* rollback rate
* security escapes
* author misunderstanding
* long-term reviewer disengagement

[.lead]
A change is beneficial only if the primary outcome improves *and* guardrails stay within predeclared tolerances.

// ============================================================================

== Statistics for skewed, seasonal data

Engineering metrics are skewed, heavy-tailed, seasonal, clustered. Means alone are rarely sufficient.

* median and selected quantiles
* confidence or credible intervals
* sample size and missingness
* distribution plots, control limits
* stratification by change class
* hierarchical estimates for sparse groups

Control charts separate common-cause from exceptional variation. Bayesian updating supports direct statements about the probability of a meaningful improvement.

// ============================================================================

== Metric versioning

image::figures/mermaid/metric-lifecycle.png[The metric lifecycle]

A metric without a version is not reproducible evidence.

* *patch* — corrects computation
* *minor* — adds a compatible refinement
* *major* — breaks historical comparability

Every material metric retains name, formula, source schemas, population, model version, computation time, and lineage to raw evidence.

// ============================================================================

== Anti-patterns

* *Activity as productivity* — commits, LOC, PR count are easy to game
* *Individual ranking* — telemetry is confounded by assignment and invisible work
* *One universal score* — hides trade-offs, invites Goodhart
* *Silent definition changes* — invalidate history without warning
* *Correlation as causation* — early adopters differ before adoption
* *Dashboard accumulation* — every dashboard needs owner, audience, decision, review date

// ============================================================================

== Worked example: unversioned metric change

Review latency drops from 8.4h to 5.9h in Q2 — a "30% improvement" credited to the new AI reviewer.

* April: metric owner silently excluded drafts and bot-authored PRs
* Recomputed under new definition, Q1 was really *6.7h*, not 8.4
* True improvement: 12%, not 30%

Expected annual net benefit falls from *$650k* to *$95k*. The AI-reviewer decision would have been reversed had the metric been governed properly.

[.lead]
A single unversioned change misdirected half a year of investment.

// ============================================================================

== The metric specification

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Name and version    | Stable identifier for reproducibility
| Decision            | Question the metric informs
| Construct           | Concept being measured
| Formula             | Exact computation
| Unit and population | Scope and denominator
| Sources             | Required events and entities
| Latency             | Real-time, daily, weekly, retrospective
| Validation          | Evidence of validity and reliability
| Bias and gaming     | Known failure modes
| Guardrails          | Outcomes that must not worsen
| Owner               | Accountability for definition and maintenance
|===

Metric catalogues are governed products, not undocumented queries.

// ============================================================================

== Key takeaways

* Telemetry becomes measurement only when connected to a *construct* and a *decision*
* Good metrics are relevant, valid, reliable, sensitive, robust, explainable, stable, and worth their cost
* *Flow, quality, economics, human, AI, architecture* — evaluated together, not in isolation
* Derived metrics need model governance, versioning, uncertainty, and validation
* Every optimization needs *guardrails*; every comparison needs *normalization*
* Metric catalogues are governed products with owners and retirement dates
