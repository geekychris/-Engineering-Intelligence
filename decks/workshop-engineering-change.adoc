== &nbsp;

*Reconstructing an Engineering Change*

A hands-on workshop derived from Chapter 3 of _Engineering Intelligence_.

[.authors]
90 minutes  ·  4 hands-on segments

// ============================================================================

== Learning outcomes

After this workshop you can:

* Draw the *boundary* of one Engineering Change from a messy artifact pile
* Enumerate *linking signals* between artifacts and assign a defensible confidence
* Sketch a *change graph* — intents, PRs, deploys, approvals — annotated with provenance
* Attribute *cost, latency, and blast radius* to a reconstructed change instead of to individual PRs
* Name the *failure modes* your own reconstruction is most exposed to

// ============================================================================

== The problem

Friday afternoon. A payments incident.

Leadership asks: _"What change caused this, what did it cost us to build, and who's on point for the fix?"_

Your evidence: 4 pull requests across 2 repos, 3 tickets (one closed as duplicate), a feature flag, two deployments six hours apart, and a Slack thread nobody remembers.

*What is the "unit of change" you're going to attribute this to?*

Try to answer with PRs alone. Notice what falls off the table.

// ============================================================================

== Why the pull request isn't the unit

A customer capability may span web, mobile, API, shared library, migration, flag, deploy, docs, verification. Each in a different system.

The reverse: one PR often bundles refactor + dependency bump + feature — three unrelated intents in one artifact.

Repository conventions decide the boundary — not engineering intent.

[.lead]
Measurement must separate observable artifacts from the logical change they realise.

// ============================================================================

== The Engineering Change, formally

An Engineering Change is the temporally bounded set of activities that transform *one coherent intent* into *one or more accepted outcomes*.

[stem]
++++
E = (I, A, R, O, \tau, \Pi)
++++

Intent latexmath:[$I$]  ·  Artifacts and activities latexmath:[$A$]  ·  Relationships latexmath:[$R$]  ·  Outcomes latexmath:[$O$]  ·  Time latexmath:[$\tau$]  ·  Provenance latexmath:[$\Pi$].

The last one — provenance — is what makes the graph auditable.

// ============================================================================

== The artefact pack

Each pair receives a printed pack:

* 4 pull requests across 2 repositories (titles, authors, timestamps, files touched, references)
* 3 tickets — one open, one closed-fixed, one closed-as-duplicate
* 2 deployment records with build IDs
* 1 feature-flag configuration change
* 1 incident record from four days later
* 1 design doc fragment

None of them carry an `Engineering-Change:` trailer. Welcome to your existing estate.

// ============================================================================

== Segment 1 (15 min) — draw the boundary

Pair up. Read the pack.

On paper, list which artefacts you believe *belong to the same Engineering Change* and which do not. Circle the boundary. Put question marks on the ones you're unsure about.

Do *not* draw edges yet. Just membership.

_Report back:_ one artefact your pair fought about, and the argument that changed one of your minds.

// ============================================================================

== What did you actually use?

You just used *signals* — even if you didn't name them.

* Shared ticket ID or explicit reference
* Overlapping authors or reviewers
* Temporal proximity
* Shared files, packages, or services
* Coordinated deployments
* Shared feature-flag names
* Semantic similarity of titles and descriptions

Every one of these is *inferential*. None is ground truth.

// ============================================================================

== Signals aren't equal

[cols="2,1,3"]
|===
| Signal | Typical weight | Fails when...

| Explicit change-ID trailer   | 1.00 | Rarely — this is declared linkage
| Ticket reference in PR body  | high | Ticket stale, reused, or copied across unrelated work
| Shared author + tight window | medium | Active engineers straddle many changes
| Coordinated deploy window    | medium | Release train batches unrelated intents
| Semantic title similarity    | low   | Template titles ("bump deps", "fix flake")
| Same feature flag            | high | Flag reused for a follow-up epic
|===

// ============================================================================

== Confidence, as a first-class attribute

Every membership decision carries a number.

[cols="1,1,3"]
|===
| Class | Range | Use it for...

| Declared          | 1.00      | Executive attribution, financial reporting
| Strongly inferred | 0.90–0.99 | Governed metrics, cost accounting
| Inferred          | 0.70–0.89 | Team dashboards, flow analytics
| Tentative         | 0.50–0.69 | Exploration only
| Rejected          | below     | Not in the graph
|===

The threshold depends on *which decision* the number will support.

// ============================================================================

== Segment 2 (20 min) — score the signals

Back in your pair. For each artefact you provisionally included in Segment 1:

. List every signal that links it to the intent
. Assign each signal a weight (your own — argue about it)
. Multiply or combine to a *membership confidence in [0,1]*
. Classify it: declared / strongly inferred / inferred / tentative / rejected

Re-examine your boundary. Do any artefacts fall below your threshold?

_Report back:_ one artefact you *demoted* after scoring, and the signal you had over-trusted.

// ============================================================================

== From membership to graph

Membership tells you _which_ artefacts belong. The graph tells you _how_ they relate.

[stem]
++++
G_E = (V_E, E_E, X_V, X_E, T)
++++

image::figures/mermaid/engineering-change-lifecycle.png[Engineering Change lifecycle]

Edges — `IMPLEMENTS`, `DEPENDS_ON`, `REVIEWS`, `DEPLOYS`, `VERIFIES`, `ROLLS_BACK`, `CAUSED_BY` — carry *time, source, and confidence* just like nodes.

// ============================================================================

== Provenance rides every edge

An edge answers *"why are these linked?"* — not just _that_ they are.

[source,json]
----
{
  "artifact": "repo-a#1842",
  "engineeringChange": "EC-01J8...",
  "confidence": 0.96,
  "evidence": [
    {"type": "ticket_match", "value": "PAY-431", "weight": 0.52},
    {"type": "explicit_pr_reference", "value": "repo-b#991", "weight": 0.27},
    {"type": "coordinated_deployment", "windowMinutes": 18, "weight": 0.11}
  ],
  "modelVersion": "reconstruct-v3.4"
}
----

Silent mutation destroys trust. Every link must be *reversible with an explanation*.

// ============================================================================

== Segment 3 (20 min) — draw the change graph

Take a fresh sheet.

. Put the *intent* at the top (one node — the coherent purpose)
. Add each *accepted artefact* as a node
. Draw *typed edges* between them: `IMPLEMENTS`, `REVIEWS`, `DEPLOYS`, `VERIFIES`
. On each edge, annotate: *source of the link*, *signal(s) used*, *confidence*
. Mark the *outcome* node — accepted, partial, rolled back, abandoned

Do not skip the annotation. That is the exercise.

_Report back:_ the edge with the *lowest confidence* on your graph, and what would raise it.

// ============================================================================

== The graph unlocks questions PRs can't

* Which artefact lies on the *critical path*?
* How many *repo and ownership boundaries* were crossed?
* Which *approvals* delayed acceptance?
* Did the incident originate from a change spanning *several repositories*?
* How much *human and AI effort* accumulated across the whole change?

Each is a *graph traversal*, not a flat-table query.

// ============================================================================

== Critical path and blast radius

Path duration through the DAG of your change:

[stem]
++++
T(E) = \max_{p \in Paths(E)} \sum_{a \in p} d(a)
++++

* *Latency* = time along the critical path from intent to accepted outcome
* *Cost* = effort accumulated across all artefacts and reviews, weighted by confidence
* *Blast radius* = services, teams, and users affected by the outcome (or by its rollback)

Attributing any of these to a single PR under-counts, over-counts, or attributes to the wrong owner.

// ============================================================================

== Weighted cost, honestly

For your reconstructed change, effort splits by evidence class:

[stem]
++++
\widehat{C}(E) = \underbrace{\sum_{a \in A_d} c(a)}_{\text{deterministic floor}} + \sum_{a \in A_p} c(a) \cdot conf(a \in E)
++++

Report a *floor* (deterministic only), an *expected* (confidence-weighted), and a *ceiling* (all evidence unweighted). Do not collapse them to one number on an exec slide.

_Analogy from Chapter 3's worked example:_ 20.3h floor, 24.1h expected, 26.4h ceiling — all three ship together, all three stamped `reconstruct-v3.4`.

// ============================================================================

== Segment 4 (15 min) — attribute the incident

Return to the incident record in your pack. It surfaced four days after the second deployment.

Using *only* the graph you drew:

. Which artefact(s) are the most plausible cause? What's your confidence?
. Which *owners* would you page? What's the smallest set?
. What is the *blast radius* — services, users, downstream changes?
. If you had to report a *cost of the change so far* to your CFO, what floor / expected / ceiling would you cite?

_Report back:_ the attribution decision your pair is *least confident* about, and what evidence would move it.

// ============================================================================

== Failure modes you just felt

* *Ticket links as ground truth* — one of your tickets was closed-as-duplicate. Did you trust it?
* *Clustering by author + time* — did an active engineer create a false join?
* *Ignoring deployment evidence* — flow truncates at merge; your incident lived downstream
* *Displaying inferred links as facts* — did any 0.7 edge sneak onto the exec slide?
* *One giant initiative node* — did you resist the temptation to lump?
* *Recomputing without versioning* — every number needs a model version stamp

// ============================================================================

== What to take home

* You now have a *reconstructed change graph* for one real intent
* You have *floor / expected / ceiling* effort numbers, each with provenance
* You have a *list of signals your organisation has* — and the ones it lacks
* You have a *confidence threshold* you can defend for the next decision class up

The next step in your own estate is to instrument a change ID and *earn* declared-linkage confidence for new work — while keeping probabilistic reconstruction for the backlog.
