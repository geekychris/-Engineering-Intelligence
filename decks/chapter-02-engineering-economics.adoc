== Why cost is harder than it looks

Hours times loaded rate is a budgeting number. It is not an economic model.

Hidden every time:

* authors idled while waiting on review
* scarce specialists whose queue depth dwarfs their hourly rate
* required approvals that add labor of minutes and delay of days
* AI reviewers that cut authoring effort and raise verification effort
* changes cheap to implement, expensive to coordinate
* defects avoided — value real, evidence invisible

[.lead]
The right question is not "how much labor was recorded?" — it is what resources, what risks, what value, and what was displaced.

// ============================================================================

== Four words to keep separate

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| *Cost*       | resources consumed by the change or process
| *Price*      | what you paid to acquire an input (may hide cost)
| *Value*      | benefit created or protected by the outcome
| *Investment* | current cost incurred to raise future capability
|===

A process can get cheaper while destroying value. A change can be expensive and exceptional. A platform investment lowers throughput today to raise capacity tomorrow.

// ============================================================================

== The total cost of an Engineering Change

[stem]
++++
C_{total}(e) = C_{human} + C_{tool} + C_{infra} + C_{coord} + C_{delay} + C_{quality} + C_{risk}
++++

* additive for explanation — *not* independent in practice
* a tool may cut authoring _and_ raise quality
* more review raises human cost _and_ lowers expected defects
* a slower rollout raises delay _and_ reduces operational risk

A useful model records both the components _and_ the assumptions connecting them.

== Human effort: three refinements

The naive estimate — hours latexmath:[$\times$] loaded rate — is right for budgeting and wrong for decisions.

. *Active vs. elapsed* — a PR open 40 hours did not consume 40 engineer-hours. Separate authoring, review, response, queue, and blocked time.
. *Attention-weighted* — an hour from a single qualified crypto reviewer is not an hour from a broadly available contributor.
. *Context recovery* — interruptions reload cost that appears nowhere in the timesheet.

[.lead]
False precision destroys trust. Report intervals, not 6.237 hours.

// ============================================================================

== Attention scarcity

[stem]
++++
C_{attention}(e) = \sum_{p \in P_e} h_{p,e} \times r_p \times s_p
++++

* stem:[s_p] is a scarcity _multiplier_, not a judgement of personal worth
* estimated from queue depth, demand-to-capacity, time-to-assignment, count of qualified substitutes
* if migration reviewers face 2x demand, their marginal attention constrains delivery more than their rate suggests

// ============================================================================

== AI and tool cost — net, not gross

[stem]
++++
C_{AI,net}(e) = C_{inference} + C_{validation} + C_{administration} - V_{offload}
++++

A cheap model producing many low-value comments can cost more than a costly model producing few trusted findings.

* *Cost per comment* is rarely useful
* *Cost per validated defect prevented* — meaningful
* *Cost per hour of quality-adjusted attention saved* — meaningful

Capture marginal usage per change: tokens, passes, retries, cache hits, latency.

// ============================================================================

== Coordination cost is nonlinear

image::figures/mermaid/coordination-boundaries.png[Coordination boundaries and handoffs]

The pairwise communication paths among stem:[n] participants:

[stem]
++++
L(n) = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}
++++

Adding a second team is often cheap. Adding a fifth can be catastrophic. Ownership, interfaces, and boundaries are economic instruments.

// ============================================================================

== Delay is not free

[stem]
++++
C_{delay}(e) = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} v_e(t)\,dt
++++

Report ranges — never fabricated precision.

[cols="1,3"]
|===
| Standard        | value roughly linear in the relevant window
| Fixed date      | value collapses after a deadline
| Expedite        | incident-grade — delay is immediate and severe
| Exploratory     | value is learning; delay postpones information
|===

Cost of delay is a prioritization aid — not a rhetorical device for manufacturing urgency.

// ============================================================================

== Queueing: Little's Law and the 70% wall

[stem]
++++
L = \lambda W
++++

* 10 changes/week completed, 3-week flow time latexmath:[$\Rightarrow$] ~30 in flight
* WIP up without throughput up latexmath:[$\Rightarrow$] flow time up

A review queue or CI cluster at 90% utilization is not "a bit slower" than one at 70% — it is *qualitatively different*. Small arrival-rate variance dominates the tail.

Carrying cost approximation: stem:[C_{carry}(e) = W_e \times k_e].

// ============================================================================

== Quality: prospective and retrospective

Expected loss makes trade-offs explicit:

[stem]
++++
C_{quality}^{expected}(e) = \sum_{f \in F_e} P(f \mid e) \times I(f)
++++

*Worked case.* A $600 security review that reduces the probability of a $200,000 loss from 1.0% to 0.4%:

[stem]
++++
(0.010 - 0.004) \times 200{,}000 = 1{,}200
++++

$1,200 of expected risk reduction for $600 of direct cost. Uncertain — but explicit.

Prevention · Appraisal · Internal failure · External failure. The objective is minimum *total* quality-adjusted cost.

// ============================================================================

== Tail risk and the skew of cost

Averages mislead when the distribution is right-skewed and rare events dominate.

* two changes with identical expected loss can carry very different catastrophic tail risk
* historical averages under-price rare, severe outcomes
* a policy tuned to the mean under-invests in the tail; tuned to the tail, it over-invests in routine

Regulatory violation is not made acceptable by a low expected-loss estimate. Report quantiles and CVaR, not just means.

// ============================================================================

== Marginal vs. average — name the cost

Averages answer allocation questions. Marginal values answer change questions.

* running one more test — *marginal* cost
* comparing vendors — *fully allocated* cost
* reducing capacity — *avoidable* cost
* replacing a system — *replacement* cost
* displaced alternatives — *opportunity* cost

[quote]
____
Choosing the wrong cost category is a more common failure than computing incorrectly.
____

Ten minutes shaved off average review does not free ten minutes of usable capacity if the fragments are too dispersed to reallocate.

// ============================================================================

== Uncertainty is the deliverable

Propagate it — don't hide it. A credible decision report:

* *median* annual net benefit: $650,000
* *80% credible interval*: -$120,000 to $1.5M
* *P(net benefit > 0)*: 74%
* *most influential input*: average cost of escaped defects

[.lead]
A single number is nearly indistinguishable from an educated guess. A distribution tells you which evidence would change the decision.

// ============================================================================

== Common failure modes

* *Monetizing everything* — forced dollars manufacture false certainty
* *Salary as value* — compensation is an input price, not output
* *Ignoring displaced work* — waiting and switching are assigned nowhere
* *Double counting* — time saved, throughput, and cost of delay overlap
* *Optimizing components* — cheaper CI can mean more failures
* *Ranking individuals* — process-cost estimates corrupt when scored

// ============================================================================

== Key takeaways

* Total cost = human + tool + infra + coord + delay + quality + risk
* Elapsed time is not labor time — queueing and recovery are separate
* Value human attention by scarcity, not just loaded rate
* AI economics must include validation burden and quality effects
* Cost of delay connects flow to business impact — in *ranges*
* Quality and risk are *expected* quantities, including failures prevented
* Marginal, avoidable, and allocated costs are not interchangeable
* Objective: maximum value under quality, risk, and human constraints — not minimum local cost
