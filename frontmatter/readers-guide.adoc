== Reading conventions

The book uses several recurring distinctions.

*Observed evidence* is directly recorded from a source system.

*Reconstruction* links evidence into entities, relationships, or lifecycle state.

*Inference* estimates something not directly observed.

*Prediction* estimates an unknown or future result.

*Causal evidence* estimates how an intervention changed an outcome relative to a counterfactual.

*Decision* is the organizational action selected from alternatives.

These categories should not be collapsed.

== Applying the framework to a real decision

A practical reading and application sequence is:

. Write the decision question.
. Identify the unit of consequence and affected population.
. Define the construct rather than beginning with available telemetry.
. Map the Engineering Change lifecycle and actors.
. Identify attention, queue, architecture, quality, and economic mechanisms.
. Specify metrics and guardrails.
. Assess source, linkage, validity, and privacy quality.
. Choose descriptive, predictive, experimental, or quasi-experimental analysis.
. Compare credible alternatives economically.
. Make a bounded, reversible decision.
. Record the decision, assumptions, and review date.
. Measure adoption, exposure, guardrails, and realized outcomes.
. Preserve corrections, limitations, and negative results.

Appendix G provides reusable records for this process.

== What not to do

The book should not be used as justification for:

* ranking engineers by visible activity;
* treating one metric as universal productivity;
* reporting released capacity as booked savings;
* claiming causation from a before-and-after chart;
* assuming adoption proves value;
* deploying AI without measuring validation burden;
* collecting all available data before defining purpose;
* allowing generated narratives to replace evidence;
* automating consequential policy without rollback and appeal.

== A note on context

No metric, model, benchmark, or organizational practice is context-free.

The definitions and standards in this book are designed to make context explicit: population, workflow, architecture, risk, work mix, time, ownership, source quality, treatment version, and decision consequence.

Readers should adapt implementations while preserving these semantic and scientific constraints.

== A note on evidence maturity

Some foundations in this book draw on mature research in measurement, queueing, causal inference, organizational learning, and software delivery. Other areas—particularly long-term AI effects, autonomous engineering agents, skill formation, digital twins, and prescriptive engineering policy—remain active research questions.

The book distinguishes durable principles from emerging possibilities and treats uncertainty as information rather than an editorial weakness.

== A note on terminology

Capitalized terms such as _Engineering Change_ refer to controlled concepts defined in Appendix H. Tool-specific terminology should be mapped to these concepts rather than used as a substitute for them.

== Using the templates

The reference templates are deliberately technology-neutral. They may become:

* versioned files in a repository;
* forms in a developer portal;
* registry records;
* workflow objects;
* governed documents;
* API resources.

Their value comes from preserving purpose, ownership, evidence, versions, uncertainty, conditions, and review—not from the storage format.

== The intended outcome

The book succeeds when it helps an organization replace vague claims such as “engineering is faster” or “the AI tool is productive” with more useful statements:

[quote]
For this population and intervention version, under these evidence-quality conditions, we observed this effect and uncertainty, these guardrails remained within bounds, this amount of capacity was released, this portion became realized value, and this bounded decision will be reviewed on this date.

That is the language of an engineering organization capable of learning.
