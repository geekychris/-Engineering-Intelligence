== How to use the measurement framework

The book distinguishes several layers that are often collapsed into a single metric:

. *Activity* — what happened.
. *Flow* — how work moved through the system.
. *Quality and risk* — what defects, hazards, or exposure were created or avoided.
. *Attention* — where scarce human cognitive capacity was spent.
. *Capability* — what the system became able to do.
. *Outcome* — what changed for customers, operations, or the business.
. *Value* — whether the outcome justified the resources and risk involved.

A useful measurement program does not maximize every layer. It builds enough evidence across the layers to support a particular decision.

== How to read metric definitions

Metrics in this book are treated as governed analytical products rather than convenient queries. A complete metric should identify:

* the decision it supports;
* the construct it claims to represent;
* the population and unit of analysis;
* event-time and observation-window semantics;
* exclusions and missingness behavior;
* uncertainty and sensitivity;
* known failure modes;
* ownership and review cadence.

Appendix B provides the normative metric-definition template.

== How to read evidence claims

The manuscript separates:

* established research findings;
* emerging empirical evidence;
* standards and regulatory guidance;
* practitioner synthesis;
* original _Engineering Intelligence_ constructs.

Chapter source-note sidecars explain the research foundation and limitations behind each major section. Appendix I provides the consolidated evidence base and bibliography.

== How to use the diagrams

The diagrams are not decorative summaries. They express system boundaries, causal relationships, data flows, operating loops, and decision structures. Each Mermaid source is maintained under `figures/mermaid/` and rendered into publication-ready SVG during the build.

== Recommended organizational use

Teams can use the book in several modes:

* as an executive briefing on engineering economics and evidence;
* as a design reference for an engineering intelligence platform;
* as a metric governance standard;
* as an experiment-planning handbook;
* as a portfolio-review framework;
* as a maturity roadmap for organizational learning.

== A caution about scorecards

A scorecard is useful only when its measures retain meaning under use. Once a number becomes a target, people and systems adapt to it. The resulting behavioral response may destroy the relationship between the measure and the construct it was intended to represent.

For that reason, this book favors portfolios of evidence, explicit uncertainty, drill-down paths, and decision records over single composite productivity scores.

== A caution about individual measurement

The framework is designed primarily for understanding systems, workflows, investments, and organizational conditions. Individual-level data may be necessary for debugging instrumentation or supporting a person’s own work, but it should not be converted casually into surveillance or performance ranking.

Chapter 13 and Appendix C define the governance expectations for responsible use.

== What success looks like

An organization practicing engineering intelligence can explain:

* what changed;
* why it probably changed;
* how confident it is;
* who benefited or absorbed cost;
* whether the change should be expanded, modified, or stopped;
* what the organization learned that can improve the next decision.

That capability—not the number of dashboards—is the goal.
